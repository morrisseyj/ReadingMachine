### State Intervention and Strategic Alliances
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing nations is significantly influenced by state intervention and strategic alliances between governments and major business entities (Hellman et al. 2000; Litvack et al. 1998; Manor 1999; Rodrik 1993; World Bank 1991). This revival is marked by state-led initiatives, subsidies, and protectionist measures designed to steer economic activities, manage investment risks, and promote industrial diversification (Gereffi 2013; Chang et al. 2020; Armstrong et al. 2024; Bam et al. 2019). Governments are increasingly addressing market failures, such as coordination issues and externalities, which impede economic transformation and industrial upgrading (Stiglitz et al. 2013_1; Lin et al. 2009; Berasaluce 2024; Rodrik 2004). The political climate has become more conducive to development goals, with governments adopting belief systems that align economic and political power to facilitate effective policy implementation (Fernandes et al. 2026; Juhász et al. 2024_2; Das n.d.; Rodrik et al. 2011).

In highly industrialized countries, industrial policy is often integrated into national economic strategies, focusing on enhancing competitiveness and addressing geopolitical concerns (Evenett et al. 2024; Wade 2014; Bivens 2023). For example, the United States has adopted industrial policy through federal initiatives like the CHIPS Act, aimed at strengthening domestic manufacturing and technological leadership (Irwin 2023; Berasaluce 2024). Similarly, the European Union has set goals to increase the industrial share of GDP, emphasizing the reinforcement of domestic capabilities (UNIDO 2014). The pandemic has further prompted a reevaluation of government roles, suggesting a new framework for state intervention to serve the public interest (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Manak 2024).

In industrializing countries, state intervention is vital for overcoming the challenges of late industrialization, as demonstrated by the historical experiences of East Asian economies like Korea and Japan, where government-led strategies facilitated rapid industrial growth and diversification (Amsden 1989; Wade 1990; Chang 2007; Nguyen 2019). These nations have utilized state intervention to create competitive advantages in global markets, often through alliances with large business groups that act as agents of industrialization (Amsden 1989; Shiraishi et al. 2019). Brazil's hosting of major international events like the World Cup and Olympic Games has also spurred significant infrastructure investments, contributing to the resurgence of industrial policy (Gereffi 2013).

The global appetite for industrial policy as a development tool persists, despite its limitations (Fernandes et al. 2026; Lin 2012; Walleri 1978). In Nigeria, entrepreneurship policies have been initiated to reduce unemployment and stimulate economic development (Ikonne et al. 2020). Advanced economies predominantly employ industrial policy through subsidies and export promotion rather than import tariffs (Fernandes et al. 2026; Juhász et al. 2024_1; UNCTAD 2018). The strategic use of public ownership and incentives, such as purchase subsidies and tax exemptions for electric vehicles, exemplifies how governments advance industrial policy objectives (Altenburg et al. 2017; UNCTAD 2014; Mardia 2025). Moreover, the renewed interest in industrial policy is expected to endure, driven by the need to address societal goals like economic resilience and environmental sustainability (Aiginger et al. 2020; Mazzucato 2013; Maltais et al. 2025).

Recent years have witnessed a resurgence of interest in industrial policy among economists, recognizing its role in addressing market failures and promoting economic development (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1; Stiglitz et al. 2002). This interest is bolstered by the comparative success of EU member states, Asian Tigers, and BRICS, which underscores the state's role in economic development (Ferrannini et al. 2021). The debate around industrial policy has evolved, focusing on systemic approaches that extend beyond correcting market failures to include societal goals for long-term collective interest (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Stiglitz et al. 2013_2). The political feasibility of industrial policy is also shaped by national characteristics such as market size, government capacity, and fiscal space, which determine the types of policies that can be effectively implemented (Fernandes et al. 2026). The strategic emphasis on reinforcing domestic industrial capabilities and securing technological leadership is facilitated by the broader fiscal space available to high-income countries (UNIDO 2024; Wade 2010).

Overall, the resurgence of industrial policy is a complex interplay of strategic state intervention, political support, and collaboration with business sectors to drive economic transformation and growth (Suarez et al. 2025). This includes addressing coordination failures, aligning training programs with industry needs, and leveraging political and economic forces that are inevitably intertwined (Juhász et al. 2024_2; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Gajinov 2023; Tyabji 1984). The renewed focus on industrial policy is expected to continue, driven by the need to address global challenges such as climate change, economic inequality, and technological advancement (Rodrik 2014; Mazzucato 2013; Stiglitz 2018).

### Historical and Geopolitical Influences
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing nations is deeply rooted in historical and geopolitical contexts. Historically, the transition from import substitution industrialization (ISI) to export-oriented industrialization (EOI) during the 1980s, facilitated by global value chains and transnational corporations, established a framework for contemporary industrial strategies (Gereffi 2013; Gereffi 2014). This shift was enabled by the reduction of trade barriers and the establishment of the World Trade Organization (WTO), alongside the policy prescriptions of the Washington Consensus (Gereffi 2013). The economic successes of East Asian countries, such as Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan, which effectively employed industrial policy as a mechanism for growth, serve as influential models for current policy decisions (Juhász et al. 2024_1; Lin 2012; Amsden 1989). The postwar political equilibria in East Asia and Singapore supported comprehensive industrial policymaking and the necessary economic reforms (Juhász et al. 2024_2). These historical precedents underscore the role of state intervention in achieving economic development and structural transformation, challenging the Washington Consensus and neoliberal paradigms (Stiglitz 2018; UNCTAD 2014; Chang 2007). The developmental state model in East Asia, which emerged in response to Western dominance, emphasized state-building and economic development from above, focusing on productivity politics (Shiraishi et al. 2019). Furthermore, the sophisticated historical use of industrial policies by both developed and developing countries highlights their ongoing relevance (Gereffi 2013).

Geopolitically, the resurgence of industrial policy is driven by national security concerns and geopolitical tensions, prompting nations to bolster domestic production and self-sufficiency in strategic sectors (Fernandes et al. 2026; Evenett et al. 2024; Mardia 2025). The shifting geopolitical landscape, characterized by the decline of U.S. hegemony and the rise of China, has reshaped global economic dynamics, necessitating industrial policy as a tool for maintaining competitiveness and addressing strategic vulnerabilities (Irwin 2023; Shiraishi et al. 2019; Rodrik 2011). Historical geopolitical events, such as the oil shocks of the 1970s, have influenced countries like Brazil to adopt industrial policies to mitigate economic impacts (Altenburg et al. 2017). The rise of protectionism, economic nationalism, and new climate-related regulations in advanced economies have constrained traditional trade-led growth, prompting a resurgence of industrial policy (Fernandes et al. 2026; Berasaluce 2024). Additionally, geopolitical risks to supply chains and the necessity for economic diversification have further fueled this resurgence (Mardia 2025).

The interplay of these historical and geopolitical influences highlights the complexity of the industrial policy resurgence. Nations are revisiting industrial strategies to tackle contemporary challenges, such as climate change and technological innovation, while drawing lessons from past successes and failures (Manak 2024; UNIDO 2024; Armstrong et al. 2024). This resurgence reflects a broader reevaluation of the state's role in economic development, balancing market mechanisms with strategic government intervention to foster sustainable growth and resilience in a rapidly evolving global context (Lin 2012; Wade 1990; Chang 2005). The resurgence is also informed by insights from earlier developmental experiences, including the global failures of Washington Consensus policies and the successes in East Asia, as well as advances in economic understanding (Stiglitz 2018; Stiglitz et al. 2013_2). Moreover, the resurgence is partly driven by a misinterpretation of history, where it is erroneously believed that wealthy countries achieved success through manufacturing protectionism, thus justifying industrial policy today (Manak 2024).

### Technological Advancements and Innovation
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is significantly propelled by technological advancements and the imperative for innovation (Bogers et al. 2019; Khorsheed 2015; Rodrik 2015). The rapid pace of digitalization and the shortening of technological cycles necessitate the adoption of industrial policies to sustain competitiveness and foster technological leadership (Chang et al. 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Mazzucato 2013; Berasaluce 2024). Governments are increasingly forming strategic collaborations with the private sector to identify and develop emerging technologies that address future societal needs and global challenges. This is exemplified by initiatives such as the U.S. government's support for foundational technologies like the internet and semiconductors (Wade 2014; Mazzucato 2013; UNCTAD 2014; Wade 1990). The COVID-19 pandemic has further accelerated digital transformation, underscoring the necessity for agile and flexible production systems and supply chains, and highlighting the importance of leveraging science, technology, and innovation to meet social needs and tackle environmental challenges (Ferrannini et al. 2021; OECD 2021).

The Fourth Industrial Revolution, characterized by disruptive technologies such as artificial intelligence, robotics, and the Internet of Things, is reshaping production and consumption patterns, thereby stimulating renewed interest in industrial policy (Aiginger et al. 2020; UNIDO 2024; Nkosi et al. 2023; UNIDO 2014). In developing economies, industrial policies are increasingly focused on leveraging new technologies to enhance competitiveness and integrate into global value chains (Lin et al. 2009). For instance, China's strategic promotion of electric vehicles aims to leapfrog into advanced automotive technologies while addressing environmental concerns (Altenburg et al. 2017). Similarly, the adoption of advanced digital production technologies is seen as a pathway for developing countries to upgrade their industrial capabilities and participate in the global technological frontier (UNIDO 2024; Amsden 1989).

Moreover, the paradigm shift to electric driving is likely to cause a major industry shake-up, making it easier for newcomers to compete as much of the accumulated knowledge and physical assets of incumbents lose their value (Altenburg et al. 2017). The technological shift from internal combustion engines to electric propulsion is seen by China as a chance to enhance competitiveness in the global car industry (Altenburg et al. 2017). In advanced economies, the focus is on technological innovation in general, while developing economies prioritize expanding into new industries due to fewer existing capabilities (Fernandes et al. 2026; Lin 2012; Nguyen 2019).

The resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by the need to address premature deindustrialization in developing countries, influenced by labor-saving technological progress and globalization (Altenburg et al. 2017; Litvack et al. 1998). The recognition that higher capabilities in manufacturing distinguish rich countries from poor ones, as manufacturing generally has higher productivity and faster productivity growth than agriculture or services, further fuels this resurgence (Chang 2007). Innovation subsidies are motivated by weak incentives for innovation, as new technologies can often be copied or are unaffordable for most customers (Fernandes et al. 2026). Government demand can incentivize businesses to innovate, especially in the presence of capacity constraints (Fernandes et al. 2026). Advanced production methods or industries, which go beyond a country’s initial comparative advantage, may ultimately become competitive in export markets (Fernandes et al. 2026).

Overall, the resurgence of industrial policy is driven by the need to harness technological advancements for economic growth, address market failures, and ensure sustainable development (Juhász et al. 2024_1). This involves a strategic alignment of innovation policies with national development goals, emphasizing the role of the state in facilitating technological diffusion and industrial upgrading (Stiglitz et al. 2013; UNCTAD 2018; Comin et al. 2010; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1). The new industrial revolution and sustainability concerns are driving the latest evolutionary phase of industrial policies, with a focus on digital and advanced manufacturing supply-chain technologies (UNCTAD 2018; Evenett et al. 2024). The strategic integration of digital transformation into national development plans is essential for maximizing the benefits of digitalization, which can drive economic growth and innovation (UNIDO 2024).

### Environmental Sustainability and Green Industrial Policy
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is significantly driven by the imperative of environmental sustainability and the adoption of green industrial policies. This shift is largely motivated by the urgent need to address climate change, necessitating systemic changes in energy systems, production practices, and mobility (Chang et al. 2020; Altenburg et al. 2017; Wade 2010; Aiginger et al. 2020). Governments are increasingly prioritizing environmental sustainability within their industrial strategies, fostering the development of clean energy technologies and enhancing energy efficiency (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Altenburg et al. 2017; Wade 2010; Kemp et al. 2017). International agreements, such as the Paris Agreement, have further catalyzed this trend by committing countries to limit global temperature increases, thereby driving the adoption of green industrial policies (Altenburg et al. 2017; Juhász et al. 2024_2; Turan et al. 2025).

Government intervention plays a crucial role, as market mechanisms alone have proven insufficient to address the externalities associated with environmental degradation and carbon emissions (Armstrong et al. 2024; Rodrik 2014; Mazzucato 2013; Evenett et al. 2024). State-led initiatives, including subsidies, procurement policies, and incentives for clean technology, are pivotal in steering economies towards low-carbon, resource-efficient pathways (Mazzucato 2013; Maltais et al. 2025; Wade 2010). Countries like China and Germany exemplify this approach, with substantial investments in renewable energy and electric vehicles, which not only address environmental concerns but also enhance national competitiveness (Altenburg et al. 2017; Mazzucato 2013; UNIDO 2024).

The COVID-19 pandemic has underscored the need for a sustainable and equitable global development paradigm, further emphasizing the role of science, technology, and innovation in tackling environmental challenges (Ferrannini et al. 2021; UNCTAD 2018; OECD 2021). The transition to a green economy is increasingly seen as a strategic driver of long-term growth, offering opportunities for job creation, technological advancement, and inclusive economic development (Rodrik 2014; Altenburg et al. 2017; UNIDO 2024; Bivens 2023). Integrated Sustainable Industrial Development (ISID) will be a key driver for the successful integration of the economic, social, and environmental dimensions required to fully realize sustainable development (UNIDO 2014). Emerging consumption and production patterns present challenges for innovation in green technologies, which could drive structural transformation and create employment and wage opportunities (UNCTAD 2014). Thus, green industrial policies are not only a response to environmental imperatives but also a means to achieve broader economic and social objectives, reflecting a comprehensive approach to industrial policy resurgence (Altenburg et al. 2017; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Berasaluce 2024).

Emerging economies are increasingly producing a significant share of green innovations, driven by domestic green policies and demand from developed countries (Altenburg et al. 2017; Bogers et al. 2019). The need for quantum leaps in resource efficiency and radically different technologies and business models to stay within a safe operating space provides a strong rationale for green industrial policy (Altenburg et al. 2017). Additionally, the political economy of industrial policy resurgence involves leveraging green industrial policies as a strategic complement to carbon pricing, which helps shift political equilibria in favor of broader climate policy reforms by creating supportive constituencies (Juhász et al. 2024_2; Aidt et al. 2002).

The resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by the need to address broader national objectives such as national security, ecological sustainability, human rights, and equality, which are more prominent considerations now than in earlier periods (Fernandes et al. 2026; World Bank 1993; Bhattacharyya 2019; Cornia et al. 2017; Diao et al. 2005; Fabrizio et al. 2010; Fitoussi et al. 2000; Fliess et al. 2005; Galitsky et al. 2004; Ikonne et al. 2020; Juhász et al. 2024_1; Kingsly 2024; Lin 2012; Manak 2024; Potter et al. 2012; Stiglitz 2018; Sulistyawati et al. 2024; UN 2013; Wang et al. 2019; Whitsel et al. 2024; World Bank 1985; World Bank 1989; World Bank 1992; World Bank 2004; World Bank 2005_2; World Bank 2007_2; World Bank 2008; World Bank 2009_1; World Bank 2009_2; World Bank et al. 2013). The under-pricing of carbon generates an independent motive for industrial policy in the area of green technologies (Rodrik 2014). Furthermore, unbearable urban air pollution in countries like China has led to health problems, driving an increase in demand for electric mobility (Altenburg et al. 2017). The global energy transition, including the development of green hydrogen, presents strategic opportunities and challenges for emerging markets and developing economies (UNIDO 2024). Geopolitical risks to supply chains have become a focus, alongside rising risks related to climate change, driving the resurgence of industrial policy (Mardia 2025).

### Economic Crises and Resilience
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is significantly driven by economic crises, notably the 2008 financial collapse and the COVID-19 pandemic. These events exposed the limitations of market mechanisms and underscored the necessity for government intervention to enhance economic resilience and stability (Stiglitz et al. 2013_1; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Chang et al. 2020; Wade 2010; Lin 2012; Cafruny et al. 2007; Rodrik et al. 2011; World Bank 1987; World Bank 2005_1). The crises highlighted systemic market failures, such as coordination failures and information asymmetries, prompting a reevaluation of the role of industrial policy in addressing these deficiencies (Altenburg et al. 2017; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1; Juhász et al. 2024_1). The 2008 financial crisis, in particular, led to a broader consensus on the need for strong government intervention to prevent economic collapse, as evidenced by policy shifts in the United States and Europe (Stiglitz et al. 2013_2; Warwick 2013; Berasaluce 2024; Alpízar et al. 2010).

The COVID-19 pandemic further accelerated this trend by revealing vulnerabilities in global supply chains and emphasizing the strategic importance of sectors such as health and logistics, thereby necessitating industrial policy as a central pillar of recovery strategies (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Mardia 2025; Maltais et al. 2025; Juhász et al. 2024_2). The pandemic's differential impact across social groups and economies also highlighted the need for policies that promote social inclusion and economic stability (Ferrannini et al. 2021). Additionally, the pandemic exacerbated existing issues such as inequality and unsustainable production and consumption patterns, further justifying the need for industrial policy (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Moreover, these crises have catalyzed a shift towards industrial policies that integrate environmental and social goals, reflecting a broader recognition of the need for sustainable and inclusive growth (Ferrannini et al. 2021; UNIDO 2024; Mazzucato 2013; UNCTAD 2014). This shift is supported by the increasing understanding that unfettered market-based resource allocation is unlikely to foster structural change in a socially optimal manner, thus driving the resurgence of industrial policy as a tool for managing structural transformation and enhancing productivity (Altenburg et al. 2017; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1; Bivens 2023). The need to address climate change and promote innovation and climate-smart growth during economic downturns has also been a significant driver (World Bank 2010_2).

Emerging economies have become a major engine of growth as world trade rebounds from the 2008–09 economic crisis, highlighting the role of industrial policy in supporting this growth (Gereffi 2013). Following the global financial crises, the EU's industrial policy effort intensified as growth further diminished (Yülek 2018). Many developing countries have realized the need for industrial policy to upgrade their economies following the end of the China-driven commodity boom, with middle-income countries in Asia and Latin America viewing it as a tool to overcome the 'middle-income trap' (Chang et al. 2020). In Africa, industrial policy discussions focus on poverty alleviation (Chang et al. 2020). The resurgence in Latin America has been driven by opportunities from the commodity price super cycle and financial support from BRICS countries, particularly China (Páez 2018).

The resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by the need to improve enabling institutions, such as accountable and capable implementing agencies, and strong economy-wide fundamentals like an educated workforce and infrastructure (Fernandes et al. 2026; UNIDO 2014). Governments across the world have begun to resort to industrial policy due to slowed global growth, automation thinning out the labor market, and the surge of protectionism and subsidies in advanced and middle-income economies (Fernandes et al. 2026; World Bank 1985). The current period of uncertainty warrants experimentation with industrial policy to determine what is effective and efficient (Fernandes et al. 2026).

Overall, the economic crises have not only prompted a reevaluation of industrial policy but have also reinforced its role as a critical mechanism for achieving economic resilience, addressing market failures, and steering structural transformation in a way that aligns with broader societal goals (Stiglitz et al. 2013_1; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Chang et al. 2020; Rodrik 1993; World Bank 1993). The resurgence is further supported by the need to address geopolitical concerns, climate change, and national security, which have become increasingly important in shaping industrial policy (Evenett et al. 2024; UNCTAD 2018; World Bank 2009_1). Additionally, the expansion of the middle class in many developing economies justifies renewed focus on manufacturing for local markets, as well as on productivity gains in labor-intensive services like health care and retail (Fernandes et al. 2026). The increasing frequency of natural disasters and the emergence of new infectious diseases have added further motivation for industrial policy to secure against risks (Fernandes et al. 2026).

### Globalization and Trade Dynamics
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is intricately linked to globalization and evolving trade dynamics, particularly through the transformation of global value chains (GVCs) and shifting trade patterns. The integration of industries into GVCs has fundamentally reshaped global production and trade, prompting nations to adopt industrial policies that leverage these international supply chain linkages to enhance their roles in global markets (Gereffi 2013; Gereffi 2014). Emerging economies, notably the BRICs, have become pivotal engines of growth and investment, necessitating industrial policies that support economic diversification and competitiveness (Gereffi 2013; Gereffi 2014). The increasing specialization of firms in production tasks and the demand for intermediate goods have led to productivity gains and expanded markets, further promoting industrial policy (Chang et al. 2020).

The transition from import-substituting industrialization (ISI) to export-oriented industrialization (EOI) has been a critical driver, facilitated by transnational corporations and the liberalization of trade barriers, which enabled the rise of GVCs (Gereffi 2013; Gereffi 2014). This shift has been further supported by strategic responses to global economic shifts, such as addressing trade imbalances and competitive pressures from emerging economies (Aiginger et al. 2020; Fernandes et al. 2026). Additionally, the need for economic diversification, particularly in resource-dependent regions like the Gulf, has led to industrial policies aimed at reducing reliance on primary commodities and enhancing manufacturing capabilities (Chang and Andreoni 2020). The reconfiguration of GVCs, driven by geopolitical tensions and the quest for resilient supply chains, has also prompted countries to reconsider their industrial strategies to ensure economic resilience and strategic autonomy (UNIDO 2024; Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Technological advances, cost-reduction opportunities associated with offshoring, and increasing openness to trade and investments are significant drivers of industrial policy resurgence (Chang et al. 2020; Shiraishi et al. 2019). The necessity for national and subnational industrial systems to mitigate risks and exploit opportunities connected to globalization processes and disruptive technological changes further drives this resurgence (Ferrannini et al. 2021). China's competitive manufacturing, partly due to an undervalued exchange rate, has intensified pressures on developing countries, leading to renewed industrial policy efforts (Wade 2010). Labor-saving technological progress and globalization contribute to premature deindustrialization in developing countries, necessitating policy responses (Altenburg et al. 2017; Juhász et al. 2024_1).

Governments are increasingly providing market access assistance and quality infrastructure to help businesses connect to international buyers and establish product standards, addressing information asymmetry and driving industrial policy (Fernandes et al. 2026). Rising protectionism and economic nationalism may limit developing economies' access to large export markets, prompting a strategic shift in industrial policies (Fernandes et al. 2026). The expansion of talent due to rising education levels globally and a supportive political environment also contribute to the resurgence of industrial policy (Fernandes et al. 2026). Export bans, particularly on minerals critical to digital technologies, are increasingly used to promote domestic industries (Fernandes et al. 2026).

The resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by the need to address market failures, such as coordination failures and information asymmetries, while responding to geopolitical shifts and the rise of protectionism (Fernandes et al. 2026; Evenett et al. 2024). The strategic use of special economic zones (SEZs) and investment incentives is employed to stimulate industrial development and attract foreign direct investment (FDI) (UNCTAD 2018). The resurgence is characterized by a strategic review of investment policies, focusing on attracting FDI, liberalizing entry conditions, and promoting investment through incentives and SEZs, while addressing national security concerns through investment restrictions (UNCTAD 2018).

Geopolitical pressures, economic decentralization, and the need for economic reforms in response to falling behind economically, as seen in Korea, Indonesia, and China, are additional drivers of industrial policy resurgence (World Bank 2006). Internal and external factors, including high unemployment, labor market pressures, historical state intervention traditions, and demographic changes, also play a crucial role (World Bank 2004). Demographic shifts, such as labor force expansion and migration patterns, significantly influence labor supply and demand dynamics, particularly in regions like MENA (World Bank 2004). Addressing structural rigidities in labor markets, such as public sector dominance and employment segmentation, is essential for reducing unemployment and encouraging private sector employment (World Bank 2004). Furthermore, the need to address contemporary challenges like ecological sustainability, human rights, and equality, while responding to competitive pressures in global markets, underscores the strategic importance of industrial policy (World Bank 2004; Aidt et al. 2002; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1).

Overall, the resurgence of industrial policy is a response to the complex interplay of globalization, trade dynamics, and the strategic need to enhance national competitiveness in a rapidly evolving global economic landscape (Gereffi 2013; Chang and Andreoni 2020; UNIDO 2024; Rodrik 2014).

In contemporary times, industrial policy has evolved to integrate considerations of inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality. The transition from 'producer-driven' to 'buyer-driven' commodity chains in the 1970s and 1980s further exemplifies the shift towards market-oriented strategies (Gereffi 2013). Additionally, the dissolution of the Soviet bloc in the late 1980s heralded an era of free market triumphalism, reinforcing the global ideological dominance of free market and free trade economics (Chang et al. 2020).

In summary, the evolution from protectionism to market orientation in industrial policy signifies a broader trend towards global economic integration while simultaneously addressing domestic socio-economic objectives. This progression highlights the critical need to balance state intervention with market mechanisms to achieve sustainable and inclusive growth (Wade 1990; Lin 2012; Aiginger et al. 2020).

The transformation of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day underscores a significant shift towards integrating inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality into policy frameworks. The integration of these considerations into industrial policy frameworks highlights a broader understanding of development that transcends mere economic growth to include social and environmental dimensions (World Bank 2006; UNIDO 2024; Wade 2010).

Overall, the evolution of industrial policy demonstrates a growing emphasis on inclusive and sustainable growth, focusing on creating equitable opportunities and benefits for all while ensuring that economic development aligns with broader societal goals (World Commission on the Social Dimension of Globalization 2004; UNIDO 2024; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1; World Bank 1987). ### Role of Innovation and Technology
The trajectory of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day underscores the increasing centrality of innovation and technology as pivotal elements in driving economic growth and enhancing competitiveness (Aiginger et al. 2020; Das n.d.; Juhász et al. 2024_1; Khorsheed 2015; Lin 2012; Stiglitz et al. 2013_2). Initially, the focus was predominantly on basic research and military applications. However, there has been a discernible shift towards fostering innovation through strategic public-private partnerships and targeted investments in research and development (R&D) (Wade 2014; Mazzucato 2013; Chang et al. 2020). This evolution reflects a broader conceptualization of industrial policy as a mechanism for structural transformation, emphasizing the development of science and technology infrastructure and the integration of education and training to cultivate human capital (Gereffi 2014; Chang et al. 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Contemporary industrial policies have transcended traditional protectionist measures, adopting 'smart' policies that promote knowledge generation and technological advancement (Chang 2007; Lin et al. 2009; Mardia 2025; OECD 2021; Rodrik 2004; Warwick 2013). This includes substantial investments in education, R&D, and the creation of innovation ecosystems that underpin long-term economic growth (Chang et al. 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021; UNCTAD 2018). The emphasis has shifted from merely increasing research outputs to fostering interactive learning processes and enhancing absorptive capacities, thereby bridging the gap between scientific discovery and practical application (Ferrannini et al. 2021; World Bank 2007; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1).

Countries such as Germany and China have implemented strategic initiatives like 'Industry 4.0' and 'Made in China 2025', underscoring the global implications of innovation-driven industrial policies (Chang et al. 2020; UNIDO 2024). These initiatives aim to bolster competitiveness by integrating digital transformation and clean energy technologies, reflecting a shift towards sustainable and inclusive growth (UNIDO 2024; Dieu 2003).

The role of the state has evolved from merely addressing market failures to actively shaping markets and driving technological change, particularly in green technologies. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted nationalist approaches to technological development, especially in pharmaceuticals, as evidenced by the early availability of vaccines to countries capable of producing them (Berasaluce 2024). This involves long-term investments and mission-oriented approaches that align with contemporary goals of inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality (Mazzucato 2013; UNCTAD 2014; Stiglitz 2018). The integration of ecological sustainability and social equity into industrial policy frameworks marks a significant departure from past approaches, emphasizing the need for systemic, comprehensive strategies that address economic, social, and environmental challenges (Dieu 2003; Kemp et al. 2017; Wade 2010; Tyabji 1984; UNCTAD 2006; Yülek 2018).

In the current industrial policy discourse, there is an acknowledgment that learning has become increasingly disconnected from production, with a prevailing view that innovation is primarily generated by R&D units, such as public research institutes and corporate R&D departments (Chang et al. 2020). Successful countries have not only invested in education and R&D but also in knowledge-generating activities closely linked to production, such as worker training and technological consulting services for SMEs (Chang et al. 2020). This underscores the importance of integrating learning in production as a catalyst for industrial dynamics and innovation (Chang et al. 2020; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1).

The shift towards post-industrial economies is propelled by technological advancements in manufacturing, shifts in demand towards services, and increased knowledge-intensity in manufacturing (Altenburg et al. 2017; Gajinov 2023; World Bank 1987; World Bank 1991; World Bank 1997; World Bank 2005_2; World Bank 2007_1). This transition is supported by policies that emphasize the development of innovation ecosystems and the role of civil society and citizens as sources of new ideas and solutions (Ferrannini et al. 2021). The transition management approach in contemporary industrial policy highlights the importance of policy learning and adaptation, allowing for adjustments based on new market and technological circumstances, which is essential for achieving socio-economically acceptable and successful implementation of green technologies (Kemp et al. 2017).

### State's Evolving Role in Industrial Policy
The evolution of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day marks a significant transformation in the role of the state, transitioning from direct intervention and control to a more strategic and facilitative approach (Amsden 1989). Initially, industrial policy was dominated by state-led initiatives such as import-substituting industrialization (ISI), which aimed to foster domestic industries by replacing imports with locally produced goods (Gereffi 2014; Gereffi 2013). This strategy was prevalent during the 1960s and 1970s, focusing on nurturing national industries and reducing reliance on industrialized nations (Gereffi 2014; Gereffi 2013).

In recent decades, particularly following the 2008 financial crisis, there has been a resurgence in the recognition of industrial policy, moving away from ideological debates towards a more pragmatic approach (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Berasaluce 2024). Modern industrial policy now emphasizes a whole-of-government approach, integrating with other policy areas such as competition, education, and environmental policies (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Aiginger et al. 2020). This reflects a broader understanding of industrial policy as a tool for structural transformation, promoting inclusive and sustainable growth (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Contemporary industrial policy involves balancing market forces with strategic state intervention, focusing on creating an enabling environment for industries and addressing market failures (Armstrong and Wu 2024; Ferrannini et al. 2021). This includes providing public goods, such as workforce training and infrastructure, and fostering innovation through public-private partnerships (Armstrong and Wu 2024; Ferrannini et al. 2021). The state's role has evolved to include facilitating stakeholder dialogues and creating consensus on development pathways, emphasizing participatory governance and collective learning (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Altenburg et al. 2017).

Moreover, industrial policy has expanded beyond manufacturing to encompass sectors like agriculture and services, reflecting a broader definition that includes all elements of contemporary production dynamics (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Ferrannini et al. 2021). This shift underscores the importance of aligning industrial policy with national objectives such as sustainable development and human rights, ensuring that economic growth is both inclusive and equitable (Mazzucato 2013; Ferrannini et al. 2021). The definitions and approaches to industrial policy have also evolved to include anticipatory measures such as clear announcements of policy priorities, long-term national visions, and explicit performance targets to mitigate conflicts and accusations of corruption (Chang et al. 2020; Bivens 2023).

Three distinct types of industrial policies can be identified: 'horizontal' policies that affect the entire national economy, 'selective' (or 'vertical') industrial policies targeted at specific industries or sectors, and GVC-oriented industrial policies that leverage international supply chain linkages to enhance a country’s role in global or regional value chains (Gereffi 2013). The distinction between vertical and horizontal policies has blurred, leading to a more integrated perspective (Ferrannini et al. 2021). Successful industrial policy requires clarity of objectives, as attempting to achieve multiple goals simultaneously can lead to missed targets (Armstrong and Wu 2024; Armstrong et al. 2024).

### Integration of Social and Environmental Goals
The evolution of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day reveals a significant shift towards integrating social and environmental objectives within economic frameworks. Initially, industrial policies were predominantly focused on economic growth and productivity, characterized by protectionist and state-led strategies such as import substitution and heavy industry development (World Bank 1993; Chang 2007). However, contemporary approaches increasingly emphasize inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality, aligning with broader societal and environmental objectives (Altenburg et al. 2017; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Milanovic 2020).

This shift is driven by the recognition of the limitations inherent in traditional industrial policies, which often neglected social equity and environmental sustainability. Modern industrial policies now incorporate ecological sustainability, as evidenced by the adoption of green industrial policies that aim to decouple economic development from resource depletion and pollution (Altenburg et al. 2017; UNIDO 2024). These policies emphasize the transition to low-carbon economies, the adoption of renewable energy technologies, and the development of circular economy models (Altenburg et al. 2017; Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Furthermore, there is a growing emphasis on aligning industrial policy with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), which advocate for inclusive and sustainable industrialization as a means to reduce poverty and inequality (UNIDO 2024; OECD 2021). This includes integrating gender equality and human rights into policy frameworks, as seen in initiatives like gender mainstreaming and the promotion of decent work (OECD 2021; Suarez et al. 2025). The inclusiveness of industrial policy also depends on constructing 'productive coalitions' that invest in collective capabilities and equitable value distribution (Chang et al. 2020).

The integration of social and environmental goals into industrial policy involves rethinking governance and implementation processes to ensure that policies are inclusive and equitable. This includes fostering public-private partnerships, enhancing institutional frameworks, and ensuring that policies do not foreclose development pathways for less-industrialized countries (UNIDO 2024; World Bank 2006). The political space of interests and power relations should be managed to avoid reinforcing inequalities (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Recent shifts in industrial policy approaches have been influenced by the expansion of talent available to governments due to rising education levels and a more supportive political environment for development objectives, which have contributed to stronger macroeconomic management and service delivery, aligning politics with development goals (Fernandes et al. 2026). The United States, for example, has shifted from conducting industrial policy under the radar to explicitly articulating it as an essential component of federal government policy, with new investment priorities like the Inflation Reduction Act aimed at speeding up the green transition (Armstrong et al. 2024).

The evolution of industrial policy has also been marked by a growing recognition of the importance of institutions over policies, with the New Growth Theory highlighting that 'good' institutions are more critical for growth than 'good' policies, aligning with the broader shift towards inclusive and sustainable development goals (World Bank 2005_2). This includes a departure from 'one size fits all' policy reforms, recognizing the need for tailored approaches that account for specific institutional and economic contexts (World Bank 2005_2).

Moreover, contemporary industrial policy in high-income countries is increasingly designed to avoid pre-empting or displacing industrial opportunities in less-industrialized countries, recognizing the importance of maintaining development pathways for these economies (Shah 1994). The resurgence of industrial policy in high-income countries poses risks to development outcomes in less-industrialized countries, as wealthy nations can leverage significant financial resources to compete in global markets, potentially narrowing development pathways for countries at earlier stages of industrialization (Shah 1994).

Overall, the contemporary approach to industrial policy reflects a holistic understanding of development that balances economic, social, and environmental objectives, marking a departure from the growth-centric models of the past (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Altenburg et al. 2017). This evolution includes a focus on sustainable structural changes, addressing market failures, and fostering innovation and technological advancement in alignment with global sustainability goals (Mazzucato 2013; UNIDO 2024).

### Globalization and Trade Liberalization
The trajectory of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present has been significantly shaped by the forces of globalization and trade liberalization, which have redefined economic development strategies across the globe (Kuroiwa 2009). Initially, many nations, particularly in East Asia, adopted import substitution industrialization (ISI) strategies. These strategies were characterized by high tariffs and substantial state intervention aimed at protecting nascent domestic industries (World Bank 1987; Chang 2007; Amsden 1989; Gajinov 2023). However, by the 1980s, a notable shift occurred towards export-oriented industrialization (EOI), driven by the imperative to integrate into global markets and enhance international competitiveness (Gereffi 2014; World Bank 1993; Wade 1990; World Bank 2005_2). This transition was facilitated by the emergence of global value chains (GVCs), which redefined industrial policy by emphasizing the importance of aligning domestic industries with global production networks and buyer-driven commodity chains (Gereffi 2013; UNCTAD 2018).

The establishment of the World Trade Organization (WTO) in 1995 further entrenched trade liberalization as a cornerstone of industrial policy, imposing stricter regulations on trade and investment that limited traditional protectionist measures (Chang et al. 2020; Rodrik 2011; Irwin 2023). While globalization has spurred rapid industrialization and economic growth in regions like East Asia, it has also led to challenges such as premature deindustrialization and increased inequality in other areas (Rodrik 2015; UNCTAD 2014; Altenburg et al. 2017).

Countries such as South Korea and Japan exemplified the shift towards EOI by strategically leveraging foreign direct investment (FDI) and technological transfers to enhance their participation in GVCs (Juhász et al. 2024_1; World Bank 1993; Yülek 2018). Japan's industrial policy, for instance, adapted pragmatically to external pressures from entities like the IMF and GATT, developing sophisticated strategies such as the 'industrial structure' concept (Tyabji 1984). Following its accession to the WTO, China adapted these strategies to a more globalized context, using incentives and controls on FDI as tools of industrial policy, as seen in the Chinese automobile sector's requirement for foreign producers to enter joint ventures with local automakers (Juhász et al. 2024_1).

The 1980s marked a pivotal era with the IMF–World Bank Structural Adjustment Programmes, which emphasized fiscal austerity, trade liberalization, deregulation, and privatization across the developing world (Chang et al. 2020; WCSDG 2004). The dual-track approach to trade liberalization advised transitional economies to provide temporary protection or subsidies to non-viable industries in an open market (Lin 2012). Latin America and Europe and Central Asia underwent sweeping reforms during this period, refocusing strategies into coherent market-oriented policy packages to encourage private sector-led export growth (Das n.d.). This era of hyperglobalization prioritized deep economic integration, often at the expense of national policy autonomy, compelling countries to adopt more market-oriented approaches (Rodrik 2011; Akyüz 2008). The collapse of the Soviet bloc further reinforced the ideological dominance of free market economics globally (Chang et al. 2020).

The shift from producer-driven to buyer-driven commodity chains at the global industry level reflects broader trends towards integrating into global markets while addressing domestic socio-economic goals (Gereffi 2013; Wade 1990; Fliess et al. 2005). The export-led model, described as 'trade-led' growth, relies on promoting exports and maintaining low tariffs on imported intermediate and capital goods necessary for production (Fernandes et al. 2026).

The evolution of industrial policy has also been influenced by the need to balance national policy autonomy with international economic integration, as seen in the historical context of the Bretton Woods system, which allowed for domestic policy measures like import quotas and expropriation rights (Rodrik 2011; UNCTAD 2014; Chang 2005). However, the dissolution of the Bretton Woods Consensus and the shift from fixed exchange rates to floating currencies marked a significant change in economic management approaches (Rodrik 2011).

Despite the global trend towards liberalization, many countries have continued to employ strategic industrial policies to build their industrial bases. South Korea's focus on economic development through import bans and high tariffs in the 1970s, and China's strategic use of high tariffs to integrate with the global economy, exemplify this approach (Chang 2007; Evenett et al. 2024). The ongoing challenge is to balance the benefits of global integration with the need for policies that promote human rights, gender equality, and ecological sustainability, ensuring that industrial policy contributes to broader developmental goals (OECD 2021; UNCTAD 2018; Adewale et al. 2025).

The evolution of industrial policy is characterized by a complex interplay of diverse perspectives, aiming to reallocate GDP across sectors without directly altering income distribution (Bivens 2023). Integrating technical, functionalist, and normative perspectives is crucial to avoid detrimental misalignments in policy formulation (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

This shift also includes new patterns of accumulation, value creation, and capture, influenced by the financialization of the global economy and new forms of imperialism (Chang et al. 2020).

The role of political economy is critical, as the success of industrial policies often hinges on political systems and governance structures rather than purely economic factors (Das n.d.). Development thinking has shifted repeatedly over the past forty years, with progress occurring through a series of successes and failures, leading to a gradual accumulation of knowledge and insight (World Bank Group 1991). The emerging literature suggests that industrial policy is no longer dismissed as ineffective, with contemporary studies providing a more precise and pragmatic picture than earlier critiques (Juhász et al. 2024_1). ## What challenges and constraints do less-industrialized countries face in realizing effective industrial policy?
## Question Overview
Less-industrialized countries encounter a multitude of challenges in implementing effective industrial policy, primarily due to sectoral dependence, trade liberalization, macroeconomic instability, environmental constraints, and governance issues. These nations often rely heavily on specific sectors, such as textiles and commodities, which limits diversification and exposes them to global market fluctuations. Trade liberalization and global trade practices further restrict their policy space, as international agreements often favor developed countries, limiting the use of protective measures crucial for industrial growth. Macroeconomic challenges, including overvalued exchange rates and fiscal imbalances, exacerbate these difficulties by deterring investment and creating economic instability. Environmental and climate change constraints add another layer of complexity, as these countries must balance economic development with adherence to international environmental standards. Additionally, weak institutional frameworks and governance issues, such as corruption and limited administrative capacity, undermine policy effectiveness. Financial constraints, including limited fiscal space and high debt levels, further hinder their ability to invest in necessary infrastructure and services. Technological and innovation barriers, such as limited access to advanced technologies and insufficient domestic innovation capacity, also impede industrial policy efforts. Finally, conflicts arising from international agreements and the resurgence of industrial policy in advanced economies create additional hurdles, as these dynamics often limit the policy autonomy of less-industrialized countries.

### Sectoral Dependence and Diversification Challenges
The industrial diversification of less-industrialized countries is significantly hampered by their dependence on specific sectors, such as textiles and primary commodities. This reliance is intensified by global value chains (GVCs), which often confine these nations to low-value-added activities, thereby restricting their progression up the value chain (Gereffi 2014; Chang et al. 2020). The predominance of multinational corporations within GVCs further limits opportunities for local firms, as these corporations tend to overshadow domestic industries and curtail technological spillovers (Wade 2010; Gereffi 2013). Additionally, the small market size and the underdevelopment of domestic firms in these countries impede their ability to attract foreign direct investment (FDI) and establish backward linkages, both of which are essential for industrial diversification (Gereffi 2014; Páez 2018). The economies' reliance on commodity exports and foreign capital inflows renders them susceptible to external shocks, such as commodity price fluctuations, leading to economic instability and restricted fiscal flexibility (Páez 2018; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1). This vulnerability is further exacerbated by premature de-industrialization, where manufacturing sectors decline before reaching maturity, thus limiting sustainable economic growth potential (Rodrik 2015; Páez 2018). 

Efforts to diversify are also challenged by competitive pressures from advanced economies, which can preempt or displace entry-level industrial opportunities, thereby narrowing development pathways for less-industrialized countries (Altenburg et al. 2017; Fernandes et al. 2026; World Bank 2012). The resurgence of industrial policy in wealthier nations poses additional threats, as these countries utilize substantial financial resources to dominate sectors traditionally accessible to developing economies (Altenburg et al. 2017; Aiginger et al. 2020). Structural constraints, such as inadequate infrastructure, limited access to finance, and insufficient human capital, further impede the implementation of effective industrial policies and diversification efforts (Lin 2012; Gereffi 2013). These challenges necessitate strategic government intervention to facilitate industrial upgrading and diversification, yet such interventions are often limited by fiscal constraints and governance issues (Lin 2012; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1). 

The diverse economic and social characteristics of emerging economies affect their roles in the global economy and their integration into GVCs (Gereffi 2013). For instance, large emerging economies like Brazil struggle to enhance the technological content of their exports to engage in higher-value activities, as evidenced by their trade with China (Gereffi 2013). The presence of global contract manufacturers offers immediate benefits such as job creation but also presents challenges in realizing effective industrial policy due to dependency on imported inputs and limited domestic value addition (Gereffi 2013). Developing countries also face difficulties in realizing effective industrial policy due to limited productivity growth in services, which are less tradable and thus a weaker engine of growth (Páez 2018). In Latin America, manufacturing has become more capital- and skill-intensive, reducing its capacity to absorb large amounts of unskilled labor, posing a challenge for less-industrialized countries (Páez 2018). 

The reliance on FDI firms for national development is problematic, as seen in Malaysia, where FDI factories remain largely 'enclaves' with minimal domestic value addition through linkages (Wade 2010). In Africa, high industrial labor costs relative to productivity hinder the replication of manufacturing success (Aiginger et al. 2020). Strategic public procurement and skills development are crucial for promoting domestic enterprise development (UNCTAD 2018). Identifying and supporting industries that align with a country's latent comparative advantages is critical, as inappropriate development strategies can lead to inefficiencies and economic instability (Lin 2012). External factors such as trade barriers and protectionism in industrialized countries further limit market access and affect export competitiveness, particularly in agriculture (World Bank 1992; Watkins et al. 2002). Economic exposure to price volatilities in the mineral market and structural over-supply in global commodity markets complicate efforts to integrate these resources into national development strategies (Renner et al. 2020; Watkins et al. 2002). The historical context of globalization and trade liberalization has pressured these countries to open their markets, yet they often lack the institutional and economic capacity to compete effectively, leading to a reliance on commodity exports and vulnerability to global market fluctuations (Rodrik 2011). Internal constraints such as inadequate infrastructure, unclear economic rules, and bureaucratic inefficiencies further hinder industrial development and global competitiveness (World Bank 1987; World Bank 2005_2).

### Trade Liberalization and Global Trade Practices
The challenges posed by trade liberalization and global trade practices significantly impede the ability of less-industrialized countries to implement effective industrial policies. High tariffs, subsidies, and non-tariff barriers imposed by industrialized nations restrict market access for developing countries, particularly in sectors where they hold competitive advantages, such as agriculture and labor-intensive manufacturing (World Bank 2005_2; Watkins et al. 2002; Chang 2007; World Bank 2009_1). The global trading system is skewed in favor of developed countries, often enforcing rules that limit the policy space of less-industrialized nations, thereby hindering their ability to employ industrial policies that were historically successful for now-developed countries (Rodrik 1997; Wade 2003; Chang 2007). International trade agreements, particularly those under the WTO, impose stringent conditions that restrict the use of protective measures like subsidies and local content requirements, which are crucial for nurturing domestic industries and fostering technological advancement (Rodrik 2004; UNCTAD 2014; Altenburg et al. 2017). These agreements often reflect the interests of industrialized nations and transnational corporations, further constraining the policy autonomy of developing countries (Watkins et al. 2002; World Bank 2005_2).

Internally, less-industrialized countries grapple with inadequate infrastructure, weak governance, and limited institutional capacity, which undermine their competitiveness in global markets and their ability to implement effective industrial policies (World Bank 2005_1; World Bank 2010_1; World Bank 2007_2). Balancing protectionist policies with the need to attract foreign investment adds complexity to their industrial strategies, as illustrated by Taiwan's experience (Wade 1990). Furthermore, the resurgence of industrial policy in highly industrialized countries threatens less-industrialized nations by potentially pre-empting entry-level industrial opportunities, thereby narrowing development pathways and exacerbating existing challenges such as limited access to financial resources and high debt burdens (World Bank 2005_2; UNCTAD 2014; Watkins et al. 2002). Competitive pressures from advanced economies, coupled with constraints imposed by international trade rules, limit the ability of less-industrialized countries to pursue industrialization as a means of economic development and poverty reduction (World Bank 2012; Wade 1990).

The global value chain (GVC) framework illustrates how new patterns of international trade, production, and employment shape development prospects and competitiveness, often transmitting global competition pressures to domestic markets in developing economies (Gereffi 2013). This integration into GVCs can restrict local firms' ability to independently design, produce, and market, complicating industrial policy efforts (Gereffi 2013). Additionally, the expansion of global supply chains, particularly in East Asia, presents challenges for less-industrialized countries in realizing effective industrial policy (Gereffi 2013). The increased inequality within countries, partly a result of globalization, further complicates these challenges (Milanovic 2020).

Less-industrialized countries also face challenges from international agreements that restrict the expansion of resource-using industries and compel them to adopt product substitutes or develop alternative technologies and business models (Altenburg et al. 2017). Green industrial policies may be constrained by international trade and investment law, as many industrial policy tools have trade-related aspects regulated through multi- and bilateral agreements with strong enforcement mechanisms (Altenburg et al. 2017). Rising protectionism and economic nationalism may limit developing economies' access to large export markets in higher-income economies (Fernandes et al. 2026). Furthermore, the dispersion of tariff rates across products is much larger in developing economies than in advanced economies, suggesting selective application to protect certain industries (Fernandes et al. 2026).

The investment regime imposed by developed countries is detrimental to development, hindering the imposition of domestic requirements that facilitate learning and renegotiation for a fair share of natural resources (Stiglitz 2018). Developing countries must resist demands by the US and other developed countries to accede to international agreements reflecting the economic interests of tech giants (Stiglitz 2018). The global intellectual property rights (IPR) regime is adverse to development, as it is designed to promote profits in certain politically powerful sectors rather than innovation (Stiglitz 2018).

Developing countries perceive the global trading system as unjust and unfair, with small farmers in Mexico and other producers in developing countries experiencing livelihood destruction due to subsidies and unfair trade practices in Europe and the USA (Watkins et al. 2002). Trade liberalization is often managed in a way that imposes costs on the most vulnerable sections of society, such as small farmers and low-paid workers, while concentrating advantages in the hands of the wealthy (Watkins et al. 2002). The WTO has been used as a blunt instrument to force open developing-country markets and advance the interests of Northern-based TNCs, which is inconsistent with fairness and balance (Watkins et al. 2002). Calls to honor past commitments, such as accelerating the phased removal of textile and agricultural trade barriers, were ignored by rich countries (Watkins et al. 2002).

The global economic environment, characterized by increased protectionism in industrial countries and the formation of trading blocs, poses additional challenges for less-industrialized countries by limiting their market access and complicating trade reform efforts (World Bank 1991). The constraints faced by less-industrialized countries in implementing effective industrial policy are exacerbated by external factors such as global trade dynamics, protectionism in industrial countries, and the volatility of primary commodity markets, which collectively limit their ability to engage in beneficial trade and investment (World Bank 1991). As discussed in earlier sections, the challenges faced by less-industrialized countries in realizing effective industrial policy are compounded by premature deindustrialization, driven by globalization and trade dynamics that expose these countries to adverse price trends from advanced economies, leading to a decline in manufacturing opportunities at much lower income levels than experienced by early industrializers (Rodrik 2015).

The interaction between international trade and climate change regimes poses significant challenges for less-industrialized countries, as they navigate potential trade frictions and the risk of 'green protectionism' from high-income countries, which could hinder their growth prospects and complicate the implementation of effective industrial policies (World Bank 2010_2). The ability of less-industrialized countries to implement effective industrial policies is hindered by both internal factors, such as limited absorptive capacity and inadequate institutional frameworks, and external factors, such as the influence of international trade and climate regimes, which collectively constrain their development pathways (World Bank 2010_2).

In conclusion, the interplay of trade liberalization and global trade practices creates a complex environment for less-industrialized countries, where external trade barriers and internal constraints collectively hinder their ability to implement effective industrial policies and achieve sustainable economic growth (World Bank 2005_2; Watkins et al. 2002; World Bank 2010_1). Addressing these challenges requires a reevaluation of international trade agreements to provide more equitable opportunities for developing countries and a focus on strengthening domestic capacities to better integrate into the global economy (Stiglitz 2018; UNCTAD 2014).

### Exchange Rate and Macroeconomic Challenges
Less-industrialized countries encounter profound macroeconomic challenges that impede the formulation and execution of effective industrial policies. Central to these challenges are overvalued exchange rates, the phenomenon of Dutch Disease, and broader macroeconomic imbalances (Amsden 1989). Overvalued currencies detrimentally affect export competitiveness, particularly within manufacturing sectors, by diminishing export profitability and inflating the cost of imported inputs (Chang et al. 2020; World Bank 1991). This issue is compounded by Dutch Disease, where nations rich in natural resources experience currency appreciation due to resource exports, thereby weakening other tradable sectors (Renner et al. 2020; UNCTAD 2014; Cornia et al. 2017). Furthermore, during commodity booms, countries reliant on commodity exports may face overvalued exchange rates, complicating the development of new export industries (Fernandes et al. 2026).

Macroeconomic instability, marked by high inflation and fiscal imbalances, further restricts industrial policy by fostering uncertainty and deterring investment (World Bank 1991; Akyüz 2008; Chang 2007). The volatility of international capital flows and the global financial cycle, influenced by the economic conditions of developed countries, exacerbates these challenges by inducing exchange rate instability and financial crises (UNCTAD 2014; Akyüz 2008; Cafruny et al. 2007). The current reserve system poses risks of weak aggregate demand and global macro-instability, which can further constrain less-industrialized countries (Stiglitz 2018).

Efforts to mitigate these issues, such as competitive devaluation, are fraught with risks, including inflation and potential retaliatory measures, complicating policy implementation (Fernandes et al. 2026). In Latin America, the absence of growth convergence processes, costly labor reallocation, and a focus on commodity production explain the limited success of competitive exchange rate devaluation (Fernandes et al. 2026). Additionally, structural constraints such as inadequate infrastructure and high transaction costs due to protective trade regimes further limit the effectiveness of industrial policies (World Bank 2004).

The interplay of these factors necessitates a delicate balance of macroeconomic policies, including exchange rate management and fiscal discipline, to support industrial development. However, the limited fiscal space and institutional capacity in many less-industrialized countries pose significant challenges to the effective implementation of such policies (IMF 2012; World Bank 1993). As previously discussed, emerging countries must be vigilant of potential premature deindustrialization and the impact of rising incomes on international competitiveness (Aiginger et al. 2020; Rodrik 2015). Addressing these macroeconomic challenges is essential for enhancing export competitiveness and achieving sustainable industrial growth. Moreover, regulating links to the global economy, including trade, foreign direct investment, and exchange rates, remains a critical challenge (Gereffi 2013).

### Environmental and Climate Change Constraints
Less-industrialized countries encounter formidable obstacles in crafting effective industrial policies due to environmental and climate change constraints. These nations are tasked with the dual imperative of fostering economic development while complying with international environmental standards, such as those mandated by the Paris Agreement, which often limit the growth of resource-intensive industries (Altenburg et al. 2017; Ferrannini et al. 2021). The absence of integrated sustainability frameworks in both academic and practical spheres further complicates efforts to align industrial policies with global environmental objectives (Ferrannini et al. 2021). The universality of the Agenda 2030 for Sustainable Development highlights the necessity for both developed and developing economies to fully leverage industry’s potential to contribute to enduring prosperity (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

The pressure to decouple economic growth from resource consumption and emissions is intensified by the need to adopt renewable energy technologies and enhance resource efficiency, which demands substantial financial, technological, and institutional support that is frequently insufficient (Altenburg et al. 2017). Developing countries face the dual challenge of advancing structural change towards higher productivity while decoupling human well-being and economic progress from resource consumption and emissions (Altenburg et al. 2017). Moreover, the current organization of economic transactions largely neglects the social cost of resource depletion and pollution, undermining the foundation for future economic development and threatening progress on social welfare (Altenburg et al. 2017).

As previously discussed, international trade and investment laws significantly hinder the use of industrial policy tools that could facilitate green transformations, such as subsidies and performance requirements (Altenburg et al. 2017). The resurgence of green industrial policies in major economies risks marginalizing less-industrialized countries by creating a two-speed transition where advanced economies progress while others lag due to inadequate infrastructure, financial resources, and technological capacity (Maltais & Suljada 2025). This situation is further complicated by protective measures in wealthier countries that exacerbate the technology gap and restrict equitable participation in global green value chains (Maltais & Suljada 2025).

Developing countries also risk becoming entrenched in unsustainable infrastructure and business practices, which could result in disproportionately high costs for transitioning to greener pathways in the future (Altenburg et al. 2017). However, economic latecomers have the advantage of not being deeply entrenched in existing unsustainable infrastructures, allowing them to build more sustainably (Altenburg et al. 2017). Nonetheless, the historical responsibility of developed countries for environmental degradation adds complexity, as less-industrialized nations must balance their economic needs with global environmental commitments (Altenburg et al. 2017).

Furthermore, these countries face challenges such as market failures where markets do not reflect the full environmental costs of investments or when market actors lack relevant information (Altenburg et al. 2017). The additionality criterion of the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) has been criticized for creating perverse incentives for developing countries, as it rewards the absence of climate mitigation policies (Altenburg et al. 2017). Compliance costs are a central reason why businesses often oppose environmental policy measures, posing a challenge for less-industrialized countries (Fernandes et al. 2026). Overall, new business formation slowed after emissions performance standards were introduced, indicating that compliance also increased the cost of market entry (Fernandes et al. 2026).

Targeted international support is essential for less-industrialized countries to overcome these barriers and achieve sustainable industrialization. Without such support, the benefits of a green transition may bypass local communities, reinforcing existing patterns of unequal development (Maltais & Suljada 2025). The challenges faced by less-industrialized countries in realizing effective industrial policy are compounded by the need to balance short-term economic gains from natural resources with long-term sustainable development, necessitating strategic government intervention to facilitate industrial diversification and address demographic pressures, such as the youth bulge and urban unemployment (Stiglitz et al. 2013).

### Institutional and Governance Challenges
Less-industrialized countries encounter formidable challenges in executing effective industrial policies, primarily due to internal governance deficiencies and external constraints. Internally, these nations grapple with weak institutional frameworks plagued by poor governance, corruption, and limited administrative capacity, which collectively undermine policy credibility and effectiveness (World Bank 2005_2; Rodrik 2004; World Bank 1997). Political instability and entrenched special interests often result in rent-seeking behaviors and policy capture by powerful groups, further complicating policy implementation (World Bank 2005_1; Wade 1990; World Bank 1991). The lack of competent bureaucracies and the prevalence of informal economies hinder the enforcement of regulations and the provision of public goods essential for industrial development (World Bank 2005_1; World Bank 1997). Labor market regulations typically apply only to formal sector workers, reinforcing segmentation between formal and informal employment, which adversely affects job creation and economic growth (World Bank 2006). Additionally, poorly designed government interventions can lead to inefficiencies, as evidenced in India, where pro-labor measures reduced output and productivity growth (World Bank 2006). The absence of credible institutions and the capacity for governance reform further constrain the effectiveness of industrial policies, resulting in reduced investment and growth (World Bank 2005_2).

Externally, global governance structures are skewed in favor of wealthier nations, limiting the policy autonomy of less-industrialized countries and their ability to negotiate favorable terms in international trade agreements (World Bank 2006; UNCTAD 2014; WCSDG 2004). High tariffs and trade barriers imposed by industrialized nations restrict market access and competitiveness (World Bank 2010_1; World Bank 2005_2). The influence of international financial institutions and the imposition of neoliberal policies have curtailed the policy space available for these countries to pursue interventionist strategies that could foster industrial growth (Rodrik 2011; Chang 2007). Moreover, the lack of systematic evidence on why industrial policy succeeds in some contexts but not others complicates the formulation of effective strategies (Armstrong et al. 2024). The inequitable distribution of power and resources in global markets and institutions limits the ability of less-industrialized countries to effectively implement industrial policies, as they face systemic disadvantages in negotiations and rule-setting processes (World Bank 2006).

The interplay between these internal and external factors creates a complex environment where less-industrialized countries struggle to implement effective industrial policies. The need for government intervention to address market failures, such as information asymmetries and coordination problems, is often inadequately managed due to limited state capacity and external pressures to liberalize and privatize (Lin 2012; Wade 1990). Consequently, these countries face challenges in aligning industrial strategies with their comparative advantages and in fostering sustainable economic growth (World Bank 1991; Lin 2012). Addressing these challenges requires strengthening institutional frameworks, enhancing governance capacity, and negotiating greater policy flexibility within the global economic system (World Bank 2005_2; UNCTAD 2014). Furthermore, the differential impact of global crises, such as Covid-19, has particularly affected vulnerable social groups and economies already at risk, highlighting the need for resilient policy frameworks (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Selective interventions in industrial policy must navigate the heterogeneous and self-interested requests of firms, rent-seeking pressures, and other sources of potential policy failure (World Bank 2005_1). Experience suggests that strategies involving selective interventions are fraught with complexity and can lead to significant failures (World Bank 2005_1). Import barriers and market restrictions, while popular, can result in monopoly profits for firms and reduce their incentive to perform efficiently, posing a challenge for less-industrialized countries (World Bank 2005_1). Moreover, the uncritical transplant of models from other countries can lead to poor or perverse results, emphasizing the need for ensuring institutional fit (World Bank 2005_1). The institutional structure often prescribed to less-industrialized countries, such as the unbundling of the energy system, poses a major hurdle, as it can lead to private entities blocking regulatory advancements that would support distributed electricity generation (Altenburg et al. 2017). Additionally, the political economy dynamics, such as the distribution of power across organizations and the influence of powerful groups, significantly affect institutional change and policy effectiveness (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

The historical context of colonization has left many less-industrialized countries with institutions that are not conducive to equitable development, as these institutions were often designed to benefit colonial powers rather than the local population, leading to persistent challenges in achieving effective industrial policy (World Bank 2006). The interplay of economic, political, and social inequalities creates 'inequality traps' that hinder less-industrialized countries from implementing effective industrial policies, as these traps are perpetuated through interlocking mechanisms that limit opportunities and reinforce existing power structures (World Bank 2006). These inequality traps, compounded by global governance structures that limit their voice and influence, create persistent barriers to effective policy implementation (World Bank 2006). The constraints faced by less-industrialized countries in implementing effective industrial policy are exacerbated by both internal factors, such as weak governance and infrastructure, and external factors, such as unfair global trade practices and limited representation in international decision-making bodies (World Bank 2006).

### Financial and Economic Constraints
Less-industrialized countries encounter profound financial and economic constraints that significantly hinder the realization of effective industrial policies. These constraints are primarily characterized by limited fiscal space, high levels of debt, and inefficiencies within their financial systems. The global financial architecture, dominated by international financial institutions, often imposes restrictive conditions that curtail policy space and autonomy, exacerbating these challenges (Gereffi 2014; Chang et al. 2020; Páez 2018; Wade 2010). High real interest rates and the trend of financialization further deter investment in manufacturing sectors, where substantial capital is required (Chang et al. 2020). Reliance on external financial flows, such as foreign direct investment (FDI) and official development assistance (ODA), renders these countries vulnerable to global economic fluctuations and crises, which can lead to diminished fiscal revenues and heightened poverty (World Bank 2009; UNCTAD 2018).

The international financial system's failure to effectively channel resources to less-industrialized countries results in instability and macroeconomic shocks, further constraining their industrial policy efforts (Chang et al. 2020). The dominance of wealthy industrialized countries in global markets, leveraging significant financial resources and advanced technology, often pre-empts or displaces entry-level industrial opportunities crucial for the development of less-industrialized countries (Altenburg et al. 2017; Fernandes et al. 2026). This competitive disadvantage is compounded by constraints imposed by international trade and investment agreements, which limit the use of industrial policy tools such as subsidies and tariffs (UNCTAD 2018).

Moreover, fiscal constraints are part of a vicious cycle of underdevelopment, where limited fiscal space restricts investment in necessary infrastructure and services, further hindering economic growth and industrialization (UNCTAD 2014). The high cost of capital and lack of access to long-term finance are major barriers to sustainable industrialization, as borrowing terms are often more expensive compared to developed countries (UNIDO 2024). Additionally, the financialization of economies and lack of global regulations have weakened governments' capacities to maintain favorable macroeconomic conditions for growth and to implement effective industrial policies (Chang et al. 2020).

Interest rate controls and selective credit policies in developing countries tend to have broad and unfavorable effects on the behavior of savers, lenders, and borrowers, reducing the efficiency of investment (World Bank 1987). These controls can lead to negative real interest rates, promoting investment in low-return projects and encouraging firms to build up inventories, as seen in Colombia where they reduced funds available for smaller-scale industrial enterprises (World Bank 1987). Development banks in less-industrialized countries faced financial frailty during the early 1980s recession, with many having a significant portion of loans in arrears, leading to financial pressure and threats of insolvency (World Bank 1987).

The transition from import substitution industrialization (ISI) to export-oriented industrialization (EOI) under pressure from international financial institutions in the 1980s further complicated these challenges, as ISI often led to trade deficits due to reliance on imported intermediate goods (Gereffi 2013). Small market sizes and less advanced domestic firms in these countries limit their ability to attract FDI and develop competitive industries (Gereffi 2013). Furthermore, the 'Brazil cost,' which includes poor infrastructure, excessive bureaucracy, corruption, and high interest rates, adds to the challenges faced by less-industrialized countries in realizing effective industrial policy (Gereffi 2013).

Less-industrialized countries also face compounded challenges from both internal fiscal constraints and external trade barriers imposed by high-income countries, which include subsidies and tariffs that disproportionately affect sectors where these countries have competitive advantages, such as agriculture and labor-intensive manufacturing (World Bank 2008). Additionally, the need for private sector investment in infrastructure and the limitations in statistical capacity to produce reliable economic data hinder their ability to design and execute policies that can effectively support industrial growth and development (World Bank 2008).

The effectiveness of industrial policy is further constrained by inadequate infrastructure, weak financial systems, and regulatory burdens, which are compounded by external pressures such as trade distortions from developed countries and the uncritical adoption of foreign regulatory models (World Bank 2005_1). Less-industrialized countries struggle with the dual challenge of needing to upgrade their industrial structures while simultaneously addressing the constraints of limited human capital and infrastructure, which are necessary for supporting industrial upgrading and diversification (Lin 2012).

The international tax architecture and practices such as transfer pricing abuses significantly undermine the fiscal space of less-industrialized countries, which is crucial for financing development and implementing effective industrial policies (UNCTAD 2014). The resurgence of industrial policy in developed countries poses a risk to less-industrialized countries by potentially pre-empting or displacing 'low-hanging fruit' industrial opportunities, which are crucial for their development, due to the financial and technological advantages that developed countries can leverage in global markets (UNCTAD 2014).

In summary, less-industrialized countries are constrained by a complex interplay of financial and economic challenges, including limited fiscal resources, high debt burdens, and the influence of international financial institutions and global market dynamics. These constraints limit their ability to implement effective industrial policies and achieve sustainable economic development. The costs of power shortages, underinvestment in infrastructure, and inefficiencies in financial systems further exacerbate these challenges, highlighting the need for strategic interventions to boost growth and improve industrial policy effectiveness (World Bank 1987; World Bank 2010_2).

### Technological and Innovation Barriers
The technological and innovation barriers confronting less-industrialized countries are profound, significantly impeding their ability to implement effective industrial policies. Central to these challenges is the persistent technology gap, which restricts these nations to low-value-added activities due to limited access to advanced technologies and a lack of domestic innovation capacity (Gereffi 2013; Wade 2010). This gap is exacerbated by a dependency on foreign technology, which often results in a lack of resources and expertise necessary for local firms to innovate independently (Altenburg et al. 2017; Fernandes et al. 2026). The restrictive intellectual property rights regimes, such as the TRIPS agreement, further compound these issues by increasing the cost of technology acquisition and limiting opportunities for reverse engineering and imitation, both crucial for technological catch-up (Chang 2007; Watkins et al. 2002).

The integration into global value chains (GVCs) offers both potential benefits and significant challenges. While GVCs can facilitate industrial upgrading, they often reinforce technological disparities by concentrating high-value activities in developed countries, relegating less-industrialized nations to low-value segments (Gereffi 2013; UNCTAD 2018). Moreover, the rapid pace of technological change, including automation and digitalization, threatens to undermine the competitive advantage of low-wage labor, traditionally a strength of these economies (Páez 2018; Altenburg et al. 2017).

Internally, these countries face constraints such as inadequate investment in research and development (R&D), weak educational systems, and insufficient public infrastructure, all of which hinder the development of a skilled workforce necessary for technological advancement (Páez 2018; UNIDO 2024). The absence of effective innovation systems and institutional support further limits their capacity to absorb and adapt foreign technologies (World Bank 2007; UNIDO 2024). Additionally, the financialization of the global economy and evolving global economic rules present new challenges for industrial policymakers (Chang et al. 2020).

Financial constraints, exacerbated by the COVID-19 crisis, along with regulatory and policy uncertainty and disparities in R&D spending, collectively hinder the ability of less-industrialized countries to innovate and compete on a global scale (OECD 2021). These challenges are further compounded by the need to transition to clean energy and achieve innovation-related targets, which have been further derailed by the pandemic (UNIDO 2024). Moreover, competitive pressures from both advanced economies and other developing economies, alongside rapid technological changes and rising protectionism in industrial economies, pose significant challenges for these countries in accessing advanced technology and maintaining competitiveness in global markets (World Bank 1993).

To surmount these barriers, less-industrialized countries require targeted industrial policies that promote technology transfer, enhance domestic innovation capabilities, and build robust educational and infrastructural foundations. International cooperation and support, including technology transfer agreements and capacity-building initiatives, are essential to bridge the technological divide and enable these countries to participate more fully in the global economy (Altenburg et al. 2017; UNIDO 2024). Furthermore, addressing the challenges of premature de-industrialization and the middle-income trap necessitates strategic policy interventions to foster innovation and industrial upgrading (Páez 2018; Wade 2010). The transition management approach, involving strategic planning and stakeholder engagement, is crucial for overcoming challenges of lock-in and market failures, but requires significant adaptation to local contexts and capabilities (Kemp et al. 2017).

### Conflict
The discourse surrounding industrial policy in less-industrialized countries is fraught with conflicts arising from both international constraints and domestic challenges. Globalization, while driving economic activity and technological advancement, simultaneously strains natural resources and complicates sustainable development efforts (Xian 2024). International agreements often restrict trade-distorting policies, thereby limiting the policy space available to these countries (World Bank 2005_1). The resurgence of industrial policy in advanced economies further exacerbates these challenges, as these nations leverage their financial resources to dominate global markets (World Bank 1991). The rise of protectionism, particularly through non-tariff barriers, threatens the export potential of less-industrialized countries, suggesting that reliance on export growth is risky and may necessitate a return to inward-looking policies that could hinder growth and employment (World Bank 1987, 1985). Economic nationalism argues that a liberal world order is detrimental to the interests of less-developed countries (Walleri 1978), and agreements between countries of unequal power often result in unbalanced outcomes, constraining national development policy space in lower-income countries (WCSDG 2004).

The interplay of global market dynamics and domestic policy constraints presents significant challenges for less-industrialized countries in realizing effective industrial policy. Advanced economies' financial leverage and policy frameworks can pre-empt or displace industrial opportunities (Rodrik 1993). International trade rules, such as those of the WTO, often restrict the policy space available to these countries, limiting the use of subsidies, local content requirements, and other industrial policy tools (Fernandes et al. 2026; UNCTAD 2018; Potter et al. 2012; Berasaluce 2024; Juhász et al. 2024_1). This is compounded by the rise of protectionism and economic nationalism in developed countries, which further limits market access for developing economies (Fernandes et al. 2026; Chang 2007). Additionally, international investment agreements can constrain industrial policy by limiting performance requirements and policy changes that affect investors' expectations (UNCTAD 2018). Developing countries face a conundrum between their demands for maneuvering space to implement development policies and their complaints about Northern protectionism (Rodrik 2001).

Internally, less-industrialized countries grapple with political capture, rent-seeking, and misalignment of industrial policies with comparative advantage, leading to inefficient policy outcomes (Lin 2012; Wade 2014). Balancing protectionist policies with attracting foreign investment is a delicate task, as illustrated by Taiwan's experience with the automobile industry (Wade 1990). These countries also face compounded challenges due to gender inequality, lack of land rights, and inadequate access to financial resources, exacerbated by international trade policies and structural adjustment programs (CWGL 2011). The strategic orientation of industrial policy is debated, with conflicting views on whether to follow or defy comparative advantage, further complicated by global trade dynamics and pressures from international financial institutions advocating for liberalization (Warwick 2013; Rodrik 2014).

Managing conflicts is crucial for the successful implementation of industrial policy, as its selective nature makes it prone to open conflicts (Chang et al. 2020). Industrial policy can lead to spillover effects, trade tensions, and retaliatory dynamics from an international perspective (Evenett et al. 2024). Societal objectives often conflict, complicating policy design and potentially leading to horizontal inequalities and social unrest (Ferrannini et al. 2021). Achieving SDGs and reducing inequality requires introducing ex ante measures to prevent conflicts and ex post measures for pacification and reconstruction (Cornia et al. 2017). The additionality criterion of mechanisms like the Clean Development Mechanism has been criticized for creating perverse incentives, highlighting the complexity of balancing competing objectives (Altenburg et al. 2017).

Furthermore, the interaction between international trade and climate change regimes presents significant challenges, as less-industrialized countries navigate potential trade frictions and the risk of 'green protectionism' from high-income countries, which could hinder their growth prospects (World Bank 2010_2). Wealthy and industrialized countries often oppose the industrialization of poorer nations due to climate change concerns, creating obstacles for less-industrialized countries (Kingsly 2024). The global economic environment, where wealthy countries leverage financial resources to dominate markets, further constrains the policy space for less-industrialized countries (Mazzucato 2013). The competition between Western liberal meritocratic capitalism and China's political capitalism creates a global economic environment that can constrain the policy space for less-industrialized countries (Milanovic 2020). The resurgence of industrial policy in developed countries poses a risk to less-industrialized countries by potentially increasing competition for 'low-hanging fruit' industrial opportunities, which are critical for their development (Fliess et al. 2005). These conflicting perspectives underscore the complex interplay of external constraints and internal policy challenges that less-industrialized countries face in realizing effective industrial policy.

## What key recommendations can Oxfam make to more industrialized countries so that their industrial policies do less harm to less-industrialized countries?
## Question Overview
Oxfam's recommendations for industrialized countries to mitigate the negative impacts of their industrial policies on less-industrialized countries encompass several key themes. Firstly, trade liberalization and market access should be prioritized by reducing trade barriers and enhancing market access for sectors where less-industrialized countries have competitive advantages. Fair trade practices should be promoted by avoiding protectionist measures and subsidies that distort global markets. Technological transfer and capacity building are essential, with a focus on fostering innovation and supporting education and training in less-industrialized countries. Environmental sustainability should be integrated into industrial policies to align with global climate goals, ensuring that these policies do not hinder the development pathways of less-industrialized countries. Finally, maintaining policy space and development pathways is crucial, allowing less-industrialized countries to pursue their own industrialization strategies without being constrained by the policies of wealthier nations. These recommendations emphasize the importance of equitable, transparent, and inclusive industrial policies that support sustainable global development.

### Trade Liberalization and Market Access
Oxfam's recommendations for industrialized countries aim to alleviate the negative effects of their industrial policies on less-industrialized nations by advocating for the reduction of trade barriers and the enhancement of market access. This is particularly crucial in sectors where less-industrialized countries hold competitive advantages, such as agriculture and textiles (World Bank 1987; Watkins et al. 2002; Gereffi 2013; Lin 2012; Shiraishi et al. 2019). The elimination of tariffs and non-tariff barriers that disproportionately impact value-added goods from developing countries is essential to foster fair competition and equitable trade practices (Watkins et al. 2002; World Bank 1987; Fliess et al. 2005; Stiglitz et al. 2013_1). Industrialized countries are encouraged to implement WTO-compliant industrial policies that eschew countervailing tariffs and facilitate the integration of less-industrialized countries into global value chains (Fernandes et al. 2026; Gereffi 2013; World Bank 1985). This includes offering market access assistance through export promotion and supplier development programs, enabling businesses in less-industrialized countries to connect with international buyers and meet product standards (Fernandes et al. 2026).

Furthermore, Oxfam suggests that industrialized countries should phase out agricultural subsidies and reduce protectionist measures in sectors like steel and textiles to enhance market flexibility, allowing developing countries to compete more effectively in global markets (World Bank 1987; Watkins et al. 2002; Diao et al. 2005). Trade liberalization should be paired with efforts to develop supply capacity in less-industrialized countries, ensuring that market openings translate into tangible benefits (Watkins et al. 2002; Lin 2012; Adewale et al. 2025). Oxfam also advocates for the use of public procurement policies to improve market access for manufacturing firms in less-industrialized countries, thereby supporting their competitiveness and capacity building (Yülek 2018; Thompson 1995). Additionally, industrialized countries should support the establishment of regional supply chains and provide technical and financial assistance to help less-industrialized countries meet export standards and mitigate the adverse effects of stringent regulations (Gereffi 2013; Fliess et al. 2005; World Bank 1992).

The introduction of tax-free export processing zones, as exemplified by Mauritius, could facilitate joint ventures if governance issues are addressed (Cornia et al. 2017). Oxfam also recommends that industrialized countries engage in multilateral trade negotiations to reinforce GATT rules and prevent the erosion of nondiscriminatory trade practices, ensuring that less-industrialized countries benefit from improved market access and reduced bilateral trade pressures (World Bank 1987; Watkins et al. 2002; World Bank 1980). Supporting international institutions like UNCTAD in securing non-reciprocal tariff reductions for less-industrialized countries could help these nations gain a larger share of the world market and stimulate economic growth (Walleri 1978).

Oxfam proposes that industrialized countries adopt a strategic approach to global integration for less-industrialized countries, encompassing not only trade and capital account liberalization but also policies that maximize opportunities and minimize risks, such as targeted capital controls and intermediate exchange rate regimes (UNCTAD 2006). Moreover, the impact of trade policies like AGOA on less-industrialized countries should be considered, as these policies can significantly influence export patterns and economic outcomes (Sandefur et al. 2024). Industrial policies should create new opportunities for trade and employment without removing protection from existing industries, as demonstrated by the EPZ scheme in Mauritius (Rodrik 2001). 'Open economy' industrial policies that include import replacement and reciprocal control mechanisms, where state assistance is contingent on performance, are recommended over mere trade protection (Wade 2010).

Successful industrial policies in East Asia, such as those in South Korea, focused on new export industries, involved close interaction with firms, and included monitoring to ensure compliance with export and investment targets (Armstrong et al. 2024). The experience of TVEs in China offers valuable lessons for other transition economies, highlighting the importance of liberal entry, competition, hard budget constraints, and appropriate fiscal incentives for local governments (World Bank 1996). Oxfam advises that industrialized countries ensure their industrial policies are market-friendly, intervening only when necessary, and maintaining transparency and accountability to avoid harming less-industrialized countries (World Bank 1991; Stiglitz 2002). They should also focus on reducing protectionist measures, such as high tariffs and non-tariff barriers, which have historically limited market access and development opportunities for less-industrialized countries (World Bank 1991; Stiglitz 2018).

### Fair Trade and Protectionism
Oxfam's recommendations for industrialized countries emphasize the importance of promoting fair trade and equitable distribution of trade benefits by discouraging protectionist measures and subsidies that distort global markets (Aiginger et al. 2020; Evenett et al. 2024; Irwin 2023; Manak 2024). These countries should adopt industrial policies that are trade-neutral and compliant with international trade agreements to prevent retaliatory tariffs and ensure market access for less-industrialized countries (Fernandes et al. 2026; Berasaluce 2024; UNCTAD 2014; World Bank 1987). Avoiding excessive localization requirements and protectionist measures, especially in green technology sectors, is crucial for fostering international cooperation and enabling less-industrialized countries to participate in global value chains (Maltais et al. 2025; Fernandes et al. 2026; Cornia et al. 2017). ### Conflict
The discourse surrounding industrial policies in more industrialized countries is marked by significant tensions between protectionism and free trade, as well as between domestic priorities and global equity. One perspective emphasizes the need for anticipatory and reactive conflict management measures, such as clear policy announcements, performance targets, and social insurance, to mitigate negative impacts on less-industrialized countries. This approach underscores the importance of inclusiveness in industrial development, suggesting that it is possible to advance multiple objectives simultaneously while effectively balancing trade-offs (Chang, Andreoni 2020; Chang et al. 2020; Fernandes et al. 2026).

In contrast, there are warnings against protectionist policies, particularly those in the United States, which are seen as reducing global welfare, hindering innovation, and potentially sparking trade wars (Fernandes, Reed 2026; Berasaluce 2024). The U.S. has reinforced national preferences for government purchasing by raising the domestic content threshold under the Buy American Act, a move that may not prioritize trading partners and could lead to a global subsidies race (Manak 2024; Berasaluce 2024). This perspective cautions against simplistic, isolationist approaches that undermine international cooperation (Manak 2024; Berasaluce 2024).

Another viewpoint critiques the imposition of free-market policies by industrialized nations, arguing that these countries should acknowledge their historical reliance on protectionism for development. It is suggested that less-industrialized countries should be allowed to employ similar strategies, such as protectionist measures and subsidies, to foster economic growth, rather than facing premature trade liberalization (Chang 2007; Wade 1990). This position highlights the contradiction in imposing neoliberal policies, which risks perpetuating inequality (Chang 2007).

Moreover, the notion that industrial development occurs solely through invisible market forces is challenged, with arguments that government intervention can lead to socially beneficial outcomes (Mazzucato 2013). The viability of the U.S. philosophical aversion to government roles in industrial goals is questioned, advocating caution against economic liberalization and reduced state intervention (Wade 1990).

Finally, there is a call for a collaborative approach that avoids beggar-thy-neighbor policies and promotes international cooperation to enhance global equity (UNCTAD 2018). This perspective advocates for reforming the international trading system to accommodate the developmental needs of less-industrialized countries, allowing them to regulate foreign investment and protect their industries as part of their industrialization efforts (Chang 2007; UNCTAD 2018). The debate remains unresolved, reflecting deep-seated tensions between national interests and global responsibilities.

Additionally, the establishment of co-innovation and green technology diffusion funds, supported by international climate finance instruments, could significantly aid green industrial development (Maltais et al. 2025). Addressing prohibitions in international investment agreements that limit performance requirements, such as technology transfer, is also crucial for increasing policy space (Altenburg et al. 2017).

Moreover, Oxfam can recommend that affluent countries support state-led investments in research and development (R&D) and infrastructure, drawing on successful models from countries like Korea and Finland, which have enhanced their innovation capacities through tailored national innovation ecosystems (Mazzucato 2013; Khorsheed 2015). Historical experiences, such as Korea's use of foreign technical assistance and a well-educated workforce to drive industrialization, offer valuable lessons (Amsden 1989). Public-private partnerships should be promoted to ensure equitable sharing of the benefits from state-led investments (Mazzucato 2013). The state should also facilitate the creation of technological opportunities and decentralized networks, thereby increasing policy space for less-industrialized countries (Mazzucato 2013).

Oxfam should further suggest that rich countries engage in international partnerships and knowledge exchange initiatives to facilitate technology transfer and innovation, which are essential for enhancing industrial capacities and integrating effectively into global value chains (UNIDO 2024; World Bank 1991). These partnerships should aim to build national capacities for technology absorption and adaptation, enabling less-industrialized countries to pursue their industrialization strategies effectively. Reforms should also support the expansion of higher education and skilled labor, attracting multinational businesses to establish R&D activities in these regions (Fernandes et al. 2026; OECD 2021; Suarez et al. 2025).

Finally, Oxfam can advocate for reforms that ensure robust national and global competition among alternative technologies and innovations, thereby leveling the playing field and avoiding preferential support for specific technologies, which would increase policy space for less-industrialized countries (Warwick 2013). TRIPS Article 66.2 mandates industrial countries to support technology transfer to the least developed countries (Rodrik 2004). Promoting multilateral agreements through the GATT and the World Intellectual Property Organization can provide a comprehensive global approach and minimize trade retaliation (World Bank 1991).

### Environmental and Green Industrial Policies
To promote green industrial policies and environmental sustainability, Oxfam should urge affluent nations to reform transnational institutions, thereby expanding the policy space for less-industrialized countries (Bivens 2023; Galitsky et al. 2004; Kingsly 2024; Mazzucato 2013; Wang et al. 2019; World Bank 1992). This necessitates a re-evaluation of industrial policy frameworks to incorporate sustainability and development objectives, moving beyond conventional practices to tackle the distinct challenges posed by green transformations (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Altenburg et al. 2017). A key aspect is the international harmonization of environmental taxation, which can mitigate competitiveness impacts and support the adoption of climate-smart technologies (Altenburg et al. 2017). Furthermore, international coordination on environmental pricing and the recycling of revenues from environmental fiscal reforms can help forge broad alliances and surmount political resistance (Altenburg et al. 2017).

Wealthier nations should facilitate the development and dissemination of green technologies through international cooperation and financial mechanisms, such as the Clean Development Mechanism and global climate funds, to aid technology transfer and capacity building in developing countries (Altenburg et al. 2017; UNIDO 2024; UN 2013; UNCTAD 2014; UNCTAD 2018; World Bank 1991; World Bank 2007_1; Xian 2024). Reforms should empower developing countries to implement emissions regulation schemes and retain revenue from carbon taxes, thereby broadening their policy space (Fernandes et al. 2026). Additionally, reforms should focus on enabling these countries to adopt and adapt cleaner technologies and efficient processes developed in more advanced economies (Altenburg et al. 2017).

International trade rules must be adapted to support green industrial policies by allowing exceptions for environmental objectives and facilitating the liberalization of trade in climate-smart technologies (World Bank 2010_2; Altenburg et al. 2017; Mardia 2025). This includes revising the WTO’s Subsidies and Countervailing Measures Agreement to accommodate subsidies with environmental aims (Berasaluce 2024). Moreover, the WTO Agreement on Government Procurement should permit environmentally motivated procurement to foster domestic capacity development (Altenburg et al. 2017).

Oxfam should advocate for the integration of sustainability criteria into monetary policy frameworks and the promotion of inclusive trade frameworks that align with climate goals, ensuring that green industrial policies are inclusive and support global decarbonization efforts (Maltais et al. 2025; Turan et al. 2025; Juhász et al. 2024_2). High-level political commitments to cooperation in green energy transitions must be grounded in operational collaboration between governments, industry, and research institutions across economies at all income levels (Maltais et al. 2025). Additionally, fostering international partnerships and co-innovation funds can enhance technology diffusion and support green industrial development in emerging markets (Maltais et al. 2025).

Finally, Oxfam can recommend that affluent countries demonstrate the business case for sustainable investments, highlighting their positive financial impact, and adopt international standards for sustainability-related information disclosure to set benchmarks that expand policy space for less-industrialized countries (OECD 2021). As previously noted, reforms should address the failure of market prices to value environmental resources, which poses a direct risk to livelihoods and is central to debates on international trade (Watkins et al. 2002). These reforms collectively aim to create a more equitable and sustainable global economic system that supports the industrialization and environmental goals of less-industrialized nations. Additionally, reforms should address the bias in technical progress that primarily saves on labor rather than capital, resources, and energy, which is influenced by policy decisions such as the taxation of labor and subsidies for fossil energy (Aiginger et al. 2020).

### Governance and Institutional Reforms
To enhance governance, transparency, and accountability within transnational institutions, Oxfam should propose several pivotal reforms. Central to these is the democratization of decision-making processes, which necessitates increased representation and influence for less-industrialized countries in global entities such as the WTO, IMF, and World Bank. This would ensure these nations have a substantive role in shaping international rules and policies (Watkins et al. 2002; World Bank Group 2006; WCSDG 2004). Transparency and accountability can be bolstered by the publication of meeting minutes, regular reporting, and periodic audits conducted by independent experts (Rodrik 2014; UNCTAD 2014). Furthermore, reforms should aim to create regulatory environments conducive to growth and participation across all income levels, aligning with the Doing Business framework's emphasis on effective rules as a cornerstone of social inclusion (World Bank Group 2013).

Oxfam should advocate for addressing the disparity in negotiation resources and intellectual capacity between industrialized and less-industrialized countries, which currently restricts the latter's policy space (Chang 2007; Watkins et al. 2002). Establishing participatory governance mechanisms in transnational institutions can ensure that a diverse array of stakeholders, including less-industrialized countries, collaboratively determine societal visions and objectives for industrial policy (Ferrannini et al. 2021). Investments in public deliberation, institution building, and collective learning could reduce transaction costs and foster a shared societal vision underpinning industrial policy design (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Oxfam can also recommend that affluent nations support multilateral cooperation and transparency initiatives, such as the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative (EITI), which should be made mandatory and extended to include producing firms and commodity trading companies (UNCTAD 2014). These initiatives can mitigate the resource drain caused by illicit financial flows and tax avoidance, thereby expanding the policy space for less-industrialized countries. Addressing financialization's complexities requires transnational institutional reforms, as tackling this issue from a single-country perspective is insufficient (Chang et al. 2020).

Moreover, Oxfam should suggest that wealthy countries promote multi-stakeholder initiatives involving both public and private actors to address collective action problems, thereby expanding the policy space available to less-industrialized countries (Gereffi 2013). A stable foundation for sustainable development will require both visionary leadership and pragmatic flexibility to guide a new generation of inclusive growth policies and institutional arrangements within the global economy (Gereffi 2013).

Oxfam can recommend that affluent countries establish comprehensive national quality policies with strategic objectives and roadmaps for developing effective Quality Infrastructure (QI) systems, which involve multiple institutions and stakeholders (Fernandes et al. 2026). This includes establishing national standards bodies that represent the country in international bodies like the ISO, aiding less-industrialized countries in developing effective QI systems and increasing their policy space (Fernandes et al. 2026). Additionally, Oxfam can advocate for reforms that shift the policies of international organizations and donor countries away from external conditionality towards national ownership of policies, thereby increasing the policy space available to less-industrialized countries (WCSDG 2004).

Oxfam should argue against the 'level playing field' rhetoric in international negotiations, as it unfairly constrains developing countries, and instead promote principles that acknowledge the unequal capabilities of countries, thereby expanding policy space for less-industrialized nations (Chang 2005). Furthermore, Oxfam can recommend procedural safeguards in international agreements to ensure that less-industrialized countries can maintain domestic regulatory autonomy, suggesting that reforms should include transparency, broad representation, and scientific input to protect these countries' policy space (Rodrik 2011).

Finally, Oxfam can advocate for the creation of formal consultation structures within the World Bank, IMF, and WTO, akin to those in the OECD, to enhance transparency, accountability, and participation of less-industrialized countries, thereby increasing their policy space (WCSDG 2004). Collectively, these reforms aim to establish a more equitable and inclusive global governance system that empowers less-industrialized countries to pursue their own development strategies effectively.

### Human Rights and Social Equity
Integrating human rights frameworks into trade and investment policies is crucial for advancing social equity and ensuring that economic growth aligns with human rights and gender equality goals. Oxfam can recommend that affluent nations conduct comprehensive human rights impact assessments (HRIAs) for trade and investment agreements. These assessments are vital for identifying and mitigating the human costs of trade agreements, ensuring that trade liberalization supports rather than undermines human rights, particularly for marginalized groups such as women (Potter et al. 2012; UNCTAD 2014). HRIAs also serve as a mobilization tool for women's organizations and civil society to engage in trade policy debates (Potter et al. 2012). Equity considerations in international law underscore the shared value of greater global equity, supporting the integration of human rights frameworks into trade policies (World Bank 2006).

Oxfam should advocate for reforms in transnational institutions to address governance asymmetries where investment protection often takes precedence over state rights and human rights obligations (Aidt et al. 2002; Watkins et al. 2002). Strengthening dispute settlement mechanisms for human rights and environmental concerns is crucial to expanding policy space for less-industrialized countries (UNCTAD 2014; AWID 2013). Rich countries should ensure that international financial institutions align fiscal policies with human rights standards, promoting transparency and accountability (AWID 2013). The Stockholm Statement emphasizes the responsibility of advanced countries to ensure global policies are equitable and pro-development (Stiglitz 2018).

Incorporating gender-sensitive assessments into trade policies is another critical recommendation. This includes ex-ante and ex-post gender impact assessments to address potential discriminatory effects and ensure that trade agreements do not exacerbate gender inequalities (Potter et al. 2012). The WTO's Aid for Trade Initiative, which highlights the gender implications of trade deals, exemplifies the growing awareness of these issues (Potter et al. 2012).

Oxfam can suggest that rich countries support reforms to ensure financial resources are allocated towards human development sectors rather than primarily supporting the private sector (Milanovic 2020). This would help increase the policy space for less-industrialized countries to pursue their development strategies without restrictive conditionalities (Alpízar et al. 2010). Additionally, advocating for increased aid flows, particularly aiming for the UN target of 0.7% of GNP, can support growth and reduce disparities, thereby expanding the policy space for less-industrialized countries (World Bank 1980; Stiglitz 2002).

Furthermore, Oxfam can recommend that rich countries facilitate investments in distribution channels that support local food systems and enhance women's productive capacity, while ensuring indigenous communities have the right to free informed prior consent regarding land use (CWGL 2011). Proposals for international financial transaction taxes (FTT) are also welcome as they provide alternatives for capital control and tax justice at the international level (AWID 2013).

Overall, aligning trade and investment policies with human rights laws, particularly those supporting the right to food and women's rights, and ensuring coherence between trade policies and human rights obligations, are pivotal strategies for promoting human rights and social equity in global economic governance (Stiglitz 2018; UNCTAD 2018; WCSDG 2004). Oxfam can advocate for reforms that ensure less-industrialized countries have comparable policy space to industrialized nations by promoting equitable economic growth, including access to education and health services (World Bank 1992).
