Advanced economies often focus on high-tech sectors and innovation, while emerging economies leverage state-led strategies to drive industrial growth. The integration of industrial policy into national strategies underscores the recognition of the state's role as an entrepreneurial agent capable of shaping markets and driving innovation. The need for international cooperation and policy coherence is critical to ensure that industrial policies support global development without constraining opportunities for emerging economies.

Overall, the contemporary resurgence of industrial policy reflects a complex interplay of economic, political, and environmental factors, highlighting the need for strategic state intervention to guide structural economic transformation and foster sustainable and inclusive growth. ## What drivers account for the resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrial and industrializing countries?
## Question Overview
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is driven by several key factors. State intervention and strategic alliances play a crucial role, with governments using tariffs, subsidies, and public procurement to stimulate growth and competitiveness. Geopolitical and economic pressures, including strategic competition and economic nationalism, have prompted countries to adopt policies enhancing national security and economic resilience. Technological advancements and digitalization are reshaping industrial strategies, emphasizing the need for innovation and digital infrastructure. Environmental sustainability and green policies are increasingly prioritized, driven by international commitments and the economic opportunities of green technologies. Historical and structural economic factors, such as lessons from past industrialization successes and the need to address market failures, further underscore the importance of government intervention in fostering sustainable economic development.

### State Intervention and Strategic Alliances
The revival of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing nations is predominantly fueled by state intervention and strategic alliances between governments and businesses. This revival is marked by the implementation of tariffs, subsidies, and public procurement to stimulate growth in pivotal industries, alongside the formation of alliances aimed at enhancing competitiveness and technological advancement (Mazzucato 2013; Wade 2014; Lin 2012; Maltais & Suljada 2025). Governments are increasingly engaging in strategic planning and intervention to rectify market failures, foster technological innovation, and ensure economic resilience (Mazzucato 2013; Wade 2014; Lin 2012; Bam & De Bruyne 2019).

In advanced economies, industrial policy is often a cornerstone of national strategies, with governments actively participating in road-mapping exercises to identify future societal needs and emerging technologies (Chang and Andreoni 2020). For instance, the United States has revitalized its manufacturing sector through federal policies and initiatives such as the Department of Energy's support for clean energy research (Armstrong and Wu 2024; Berasaluce 2024). Similarly, the European Union has underscored the significance of industry for job creation and economic growth, setting ambitious targets for industrial GDP share (UNIDO 2014). Strategic competitiveness concerns further drive this resurgence, with advanced economies increasingly employing subsidies and export promotion measures (Evenett et al. 2024; Fernandes and Reed 2026; Juhász, Lane, Rodrik 2023).

Emerging economies like China and Brazil have harnessed state-led strategies to propel industrial policy, concentrating on sectors such as renewable energy and electric vehicles through substantial subsidies and public investment (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Mazzucato 2013; UNIDO 2024). Brazil's hosting of the World Cup and Olympic Games has catalyzed significant infrastructure investments, further advancing industrial policy (Gereffi 2014). These nations utilize development banks to provide countercyclical lending and support for high-risk innovations, particularly in green technologies (Mazzucato 2013). The political climate in many countries has become more conducive to development objectives, bolstering the resurgence of industrial policy (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Rodrik 2011).

The COVID-19 pandemic has further highlighted the strategic importance of certain sectors, prompting governments to integrate industrial policy into recovery strategies to address supply chain resilience and national security concerns (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Evenett et al. 2024; Mardia 2025). This has led to increased government intervention in sectors such as health, logistics, and ICT, underscoring the necessity for coordinated policy efforts to foster sustainable economic development (UNCTAD 2018). The pandemic also presents an opportunity to reconsider government roles, proposing a new framework for intervention to serve the public interest (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Overall, the resurgence of industrial policy reflects a broader acknowledgment of the state's role as an entrepreneurial agent, capable of shaping markets and driving innovation through strategic interventions and alliances with the private sector (Mazzucato 2013; Wade 2014; Lin 2012; Stiglitz et al. 2013). This resurgence is driven by a confluence of historical, political, and economic factors, including the need to respond to international competition, the role of state intervention in creating price distortions to direct economic activity, and the formation of alliances between business and government (Amsden 1989; Wade 1990; Rodrik 2015).

### Geopolitical and Economic Pressures
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing nations is significantly shaped by geopolitical and economic pressures. Geopolitical tensions, particularly the rise of China and the competitive dynamics it introduces, have prompted countries like the United States to adopt industrial policies aimed at bolstering national security and economic resilience (Irwin 2023; Manak 2024; Comin and Hobijn 2010). This strategic competition, especially between the U.S. and China, has led to initiatives such as the U.S. CHIPS Act and China's Made in China 2025, highlighting the geopolitical motivations underpinning industrial policy (Irwin 2023; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020; Rodrik 2011). The increasing Chinese presence in Europe and acquisitions of European firms have further spurred calls for a reassessment of competition rules (Aiginger and Rodrik 2020).

Economic nationalism and protectionism have been pivotal in this resurgence. The global financial crisis of 2008 exposed vulnerabilities within the neoliberal economic model, prompting a shift towards policies that emphasize domestic industries and strategic autonomy (Lin 2012; Berasaluce 2024; Chang and Andreoni 2020). This shift is further driven by the need to address trade imbalances and competitive pressures from emerging economies, which have become significant players in global value chains (Gereffi 2014; Yülek 2018; Amsden 1989). The rise of global value chains (GVCs) during a period of declining trade barriers and the emergence of the WTO has reinvigorated industrial policy debates, as GVCs transmit global competition pressures to domestic markets (Gereffi 2014).

The COVID-19 pandemic and other global shocks have underscored the fragility of global supply chains, leading to a renewed focus on supply chain resilience and the reconfiguration of global value chains towards regional or domestic networks (UNIDO 2024; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Mardia 2025). This reconfiguration is driven by the desire to mitigate risks associated with geopolitical tensions and enhance economic self-sufficiency (UNIDO 2024). The increasing frequency of natural disasters and the emergence of new infectious diseases have further motivated industrial policy to secure against risks (Fernandes and Reed 2026).

The rise of economic nationalism is also evident in the increasing use of trade interventions, such as export bans and import restrictions, to protect domestic industries and secure critical resources (Fernandes and Reed 2026). These measures reflect a broader trend towards economic policies that prioritize national interests over global integration, driven by both economic and geopolitical considerations (Evenett et al. 2024; Cafruny and Ryner 2007). The political environment in many countries has become more supportive of development objectives, contributing to the resurgence of industrial policy (Fernandes and Reed 2026).

In Latin America, the resurgence of industrial policy has been driven by the opportunity to leverage the commodity price super cycle and financial support from BRICS countries, particularly China (Páez 2018). In Brazil, motivations included limiting oil imports following the 1970s oil price shocks and mitigating macroeconomic impacts (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). The resurgence is also fueled by the expanded talent available to governments due to rising education levels globally and a more conducive political environment (Fernandes and Reed 2026).

Overall, the interplay of geopolitical threats, economic nationalism, and global economic shifts has catalyzed a resurgence of industrial policy as countries seek to navigate the complexities of the modern global economy while safeguarding their strategic interests. This resurgence is further influenced by the need to address ecological sustainability, human rights, and equality, while responding to competitive pressures from advanced economies that leverage significant financial resources (World Bank 2009; UNCTAD 2018; Galitsky et al. 2004).

### Technological Advancements and Digitalization
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is significantly propelled by technological advancements and digitalization, which are reshaping industrial strategies on a global scale. Governments are increasingly acknowledging the imperative of fostering technological innovation to sustain competitiveness amidst rapid digital transformation and the acceleration of technological cycles (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Bogers et al. 2019). This recognition is manifest in the strategic prioritization of digital infrastructure and advanced manufacturing technologies, including the Internet of Things, artificial intelligence, and robotics, which are integral to the Fourth Industrial Revolution (UNIDO 2024; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020; Nkosi et al. 2023).

In advanced economies, industrial policies are being recalibrated to bolster the development and application of cutting-edge technologies, frequently through public-private partnerships and government-led R&D initiatives (Mazzucato 2013; UNCTAD 2018; World Bank 2009). The U.S. government's historical investment in foundational technologies such as the internet and semiconductors exemplifies the catalytic role of state intervention in technological progress (Chang and Andreoni 2020; UNCTAD 2014). Similarly, China's strategic emphasis on electric vehicles underscores dual objectives of technological leapfrogging and environmental sustainability (Altenburg and Assmann 2017).

The COVID-19 pandemic has further accelerated digital transformation, highlighting the necessity for agile and flexible production systems and supply chains (Ferrannini et al. 2021). This has led to a reevaluation of industrial policies to incorporate digital-first strategies and enhance resilience against global disruptions (UNIDO 2024). The pandemic has also underscored the importance of leveraging science, technology, and innovation to address social needs and environmental challenges, which has gained prominence in policy and academic discourse (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Moreover, the integration of digital technologies into industrial policy extends beyond developed nations. Emerging economies are also capitalizing on digitalization to upgrade their industrial capabilities and integrate into global value chains, thereby enhancing their competitive positions (UNIDO 2024; World Bank 1991; Amsden 1989). The strategic adoption of digital technologies is deemed essential for fostering innovation, economic diversification, and sustainable growth across various economic contexts (Lin 2012; UNCTAD 2018). In advanced industrial nations, governments collaborate with the private sector in road-mapping exercises to identify future societal needs and global challenges, as well as the emerging technologies that will address them (Chang and Andreoni 2020).

Technological advances such as automation have diminished the competitive advantage of low-wage labor in global manufacturing, contributing to the resurgence of industrial policy (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Rodrik 2011). This shift necessitates a focus on developing dynamic comparative advantages and sectors with potential for learning and spillovers (Stiglitz 2018). The transition to electric vehicles is poised to disrupt industries, facilitating competition from newcomers as much of the incumbents' accumulated knowledge and physical assets lose value (Altenburg and Assmann 2017).

The resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by the need for productivity-enhancing investments in technology, physical, human, and organizational capital, which have a stronger multiplier effect compared to other investments like housing (Mazzucato 2013). The USA has established Advanced Manufacturing Institutes as a direct support program to foster local clusters of firms and expertise around specific technologies (Aiginger and Rodrik 2020). In France and the EU, the resurgence is marked by measures such as reducing corporate taxes, extending innovation incentives, and improving vocational training (Aiginger and Rodrik 2020).

Overall, technological advancements and digitalization are central to the contemporary resurgence of industrial policy, shaping the future of global industrial landscapes. This resurgence is characterized by the adoption of complex and diverse policies that integrate multiple instruments such as trade, education, and foreign investment, and is further influenced by national security concerns and the desire to control strategic assets and technologies (UNCTAD 2018; Evenett et al. 2024).

### Environmental Sustainability and Green Policies
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is significantly propelled by the imperative to address environmental sustainability and transition to green economies. This shift is largely influenced by international agreements such as the Paris Agreement, which have catalyzed a global commitment to limit temperature increases and promote decarbonization (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Turan & Sivrikaya 2025). Governments are increasingly prioritizing environmental sustainability within industrial policy frameworks, fostering the development of clean energy technologies and ultra-low carbon technologies to enhance energy efficiency (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Chang and Andreoni 2020; Kemp and Never 2017). The transition to renewable energy sources, such as solar and wind power, is a central component of this policy shift, with countries like China and Germany leading through substantial investments and supportive policy frameworks (Mazzucato 2013; Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Government procurement has played a crucial role in the development of green energy technologies in several countries (Chang and Andreoni 2020).

The strategic deployment of green technologies is perceived as a dual pathway to achieving economic growth and environmental objectives, with green industrial policies emerging as a complement to carbon pricing strategies (Juhász and Lane 2024; Maltais & Suljada 2025). These policies are driven not only by environmental concerns but also by the economic opportunities they present, such as job creation and technological innovation (Rodrik 2014; Fernandes and Reed 2026; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020). The decreasing cost of renewable energy and the increasing demand for green technologies from developed countries further incentivize this transition (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Moreover, the COVID-19 pandemic has underscored the necessity for a sustainable and equitable global development paradigm, reinforcing the role of science, technology, and innovation in addressing environmental challenges (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

As countries strive to align economic development with planetary boundaries, the role of the state in steering private sector responses and fostering systemic changes in energy and transport sectors becomes increasingly critical (Wade 2010; Mazzucato 2013; Maltais & Suljada 2025). The need for massive and coordinated investments in energy systems, production practices, and mobility to tackle long-term challenges like climate change is a key driver of this policy resurgence (Chang and Andreoni 2020). The failure of markets to bring about simultaneous large-scale investments in complementary fields is one of the key reasons for adopting industrial policy, particularly when change is system-wide and transformative, as in the greening of economies (Altenburg and Assmann 2017).

Environmental regulations can stimulate economic growth and activity in certain sectors by spurring demand, as solving environmental problems has become a business opportunity for companies supplying pollution-control technologies (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). The resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by the need to address broader national objectives such as national security and reduced air pollution (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Evenett et al. 2024). The strategic focus on integrating environmental sustainability into economic frameworks is driving the global shift towards a low-carbon, resource-efficient economy, with green industrial policies gaining momentum as a driver of industrial policy resurgence (Fernandes and Reed 2026; UNIDO 2024).

### Historical and Structural Economic Factors
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is intricately linked to historical and structural economic factors. Historically, industrial policy has been pivotal for economic development, as evidenced by the industrialization strategies of East Asian economies such as Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan. These nations effectively employed state intervention to stimulate industrial growth and technological advancement, combining market mechanisms with strategic government actions to overcome the challenges of late industrialization and establish competitive industries (Amsden 1989; Wade 1990; Lin 2012; Chang 2007). This resurgence is further driven by the need to address productivity growth differentials and technology adoption, historically accelerated by postwar U.S. economic aid and technical assistance programs (Comin & Hobijn 2010).

The global economic landscape has undergone significant transformations, with globalization and the rise of global value chains (GVCs) reshaping production and trade dynamics. This has intensified international competition, necessitating a reevaluation of industrial strategies to maintain economic resilience and competitiveness (Gereffi 2014). The transition from import substitution industrialization (ISI) to export-oriented industrialization (EOI) in the 1980s, facilitated by transnational corporations and GVCs, has further influenced the resurgence of industrial policy (Gereffi 2014). Emerging economies have become key players in global trade, exporting intermediate and final manufactured goods, prompting a renewed focus on industrial policy to sustain growth and avoid the middle-income trap (Chang and Andreoni 2020). Additionally, the resurgence is influenced by the need to address challenges posed by de-industrialization and capitalize on the commodity price super cycle, supported by financial backing from emerging economies like China (Páez 2018).

The 2008 financial crisis and subsequent economic downturns exposed the limitations of market-fundamentalist approaches, leading to a reconsideration of industrial policy as a means to achieve structural transformation and economic stability (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Wade 2010). This shift has been accompanied by a move towards mission-oriented policies that address grand challenges such as sustainable development and economic inequality (Aiginger and Rodrik 2020). The COVID-19 pandemic further accelerated this shift, emphasizing the need for public action and government intervention to tackle economic and social problems (Ferrannini et al. 2021). The resurgence is also driven by the need to address the generalized problem of degraded workers' bargaining power and promote equitable growth (Bivens 2023).

Moreover, the resurgence of industrial policy is motivated by the need to diversify economies, particularly in resource-dependent regions like the Gulf, and to address structural challenges such as premature deindustrialization (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Governments are increasingly adopting industrial policies to manage transitions effectively, as demonstrated by Morocco's development of sectors like automotive and aeronautics following trade agreements (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Additionally, the rise of the BRICs and the globalization process have spurred investment and sourcing operations in dynamic emerging economies, reinforcing the role of industrial policy (Gereffi 2014). The resurgence is further influenced by the need to address the complex interplay between trade liberalization, migration, and labor market outcomes, significantly impacted by globalization (Adewale & Bank-Ola 2025).

Overall, the resurgence of industrial policy is a response to both historical lessons and contemporary economic challenges, underscoring the necessity for strategic state intervention to guide structural economic transformation and foster sustainable and inclusive growth (Stiglitz, Lin, Monga 2013; UNCTAD 2014). The renewed interest in industrial policy is not a transient phenomenon and will continue to be relevant in addressing both traditional economic development goals and newer concerns like ecological sustainability and equality (Aiginger and Rodrik 2020; Fernandes and Reed 2026). This resurgence is also driven by the recognition that economic growth and development are achievable only through the accumulation of capabilities over time, in areas ranging from skills and technologies to public institutions (Rodrik 2011).

### Market Failures and Government Intervention
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is fundamentally driven by the imperative to address market failures and the essential role of government intervention. Market failures, including externalities, coordination problems, and information asymmetries, create inefficiencies that private markets are ill-equipped to resolve independently (Stiglitz et al. 2013; Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Rodrik 2004). Recognizing these limitations, governments are increasingly stepping in to correct these failures, thereby fostering innovation, structural transformation, and economic resilience (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Mazzucato 2013; Bivens 2023). The global financial crisis of 2008-2009 underscored the inadequacies of market forces in achieving Pareto-efficient outcomes, prompting a reevaluation of industrial policy as a tool for economic stabilization and growth (Stiglitz et al. 2013; Warwick 2013; Stiglitz, Lin, Monga 2013). This resurgence is also influenced by the shortcomings of the Washington Consensus and the understanding that economic growth necessitates the accumulation of capabilities over time, including skills, technologies, and robust public institutions (Rodrik 2011).

State intervention plays a critical role in internalizing externalities, such as environmental costs and learning spillovers, which are not adequately priced by the market (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Bivens 2023). Governments are also addressing coordination failures that impede large-scale investments in complementary fields, particularly in the realm of green technologies and sustainable development (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Fernandes and Reed 2026). The strategic involvement of the state in fostering innovation and technological advancement is highlighted by its participation in high-risk investments and the creation of new markets, as evidenced by the historical successes of East Asian economies and contemporary examples like the U.S. government's support for technology sectors (Mazzucato 2013; World Bank 1993; Amsden 1989).

Furthermore, industrial policy is increasingly perceived as a vehicle to achieve broader socio-economic objectives, such as social inclusion, national security, and ecological sustainability, which are often not captured in market prices (Armstrong and Wu 2024; Fernandes and Reed 2026). The COVID-19 pandemic has further underscored the necessity for resilient supply chains and strategic government intervention to mitigate systemic risks (Mardia 2025; Ferrannini et al. 2021). The resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by the need to reduce uncertainty for firms, thereby encouraging productivity-enhancing investments and steering innovation (Chang and Andreoni 2020). Market-pull dynamics, such as the demand for intermediate goods, facilitate market expansion and additional investments (Chang and Andreoni 2020).

This resurgence is further motivated by the need to address global market dysfunctions, such as those in the steel market, and to select activities deserving of public goods based on specific producer needs (Juhász, Lane, Rodrik 2023). The productivity slowdown in advanced economies and increased regional inequalities have led to calls for innovation policies that extend beyond marginal incentive alterations (Juhász, Lane, Rodrik 2023). Additionally, the necessity for long-term, committed finance, often provided by state agencies, is crucial for supporting high-risk innovation (Mazzucato 2013). The resurgence reflects a growing consensus on the importance of government action in addressing market imperfections and guiding economic development towards sustainable and inclusive growth (Stiglitz et al. 2013; Lin 2012; Adewale and Bank-Ola 2025).

## How have the definitions and approaches to industrial policy shifted from the post-World War II period to present day? And, more specifically, how have these shifts considered (if at all) inclusive and sustainable growth, and respect of human rights and gender equality?
## Question Overview
The evolution of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day is marked by a transition from state-led, protectionist strategies to market-oriented approaches, with a growing emphasis on inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality. Initially characterized by import substitution and state intervention, industrial policy shifted towards export orientation and integration into global markets, influenced by neoliberal policies and structural adjustments. Contemporary policies increasingly incorporate social and environmental objectives, reflecting a broader understanding of industrial policy as a tool for equitable growth and sustainability. The role of the state has evolved from direct intervention to strategic facilitation, emphasizing innovation, technological advancement, and public-private partnerships. This transformation highlights the integration of global sustainability goals and the importance of aligning industrial policy with broader societal objectives, including gender equality and human rights.

### State-Led to Market-Oriented Transition
The trajectory of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day illustrates a profound transformation from state-led, protectionist frameworks to market-oriented strategies. Initially, many nations embraced import substitution industrialization (ISI), characterized by high tariffs and significant state intervention to foster domestic industries (Gereffi 2014; World Bank 1991). However, the inherent limitations of ISI, such as trade deficits and inefficiencies, prompted a shift towards export-oriented industrialization (EOI) in the 1980s, particularly in East Asia (Wade 2010; Gereffi 2014). This transition emphasized integration into global markets, technological innovation, and the utilization of global value chains (GVCs) (Gereffi 2014; Juhász, Lane, Rodrik 2023 2023; Gajinov 2023). The international division of labor, with low-wage countries focusing on labor-intensive production and high-wage countries on other industries, significantly influenced these strategies (Amsden 1989).

The shift was further shaped by global economic changes, notably the rise of neoliberal policies in the 1980s, which prioritized deregulation, trade liberalization, and reduced state intervention (Chang 2007; Rodrik 2011). Structural adjustment programs by the IMF and World Bank reinforced these trends, advocating fiscal austerity and privatization (Chang and Andreoni 2020). Despite these global shifts, some countries, especially in East Asia, retained strategic state involvement to guide industrial development, balancing market forces with government intervention to achieve rapid industrialization and technological progress (Wade 2010; World Bank 1993; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020).

In contemporary contexts, industrial policies increasingly incorporate considerations of inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality. This reflects a broader understanding of industrial policy as a tool for not only economic growth but also social and environmental objectives (UNCTAD 2018; World Bank 1991). The focus has shifted towards creating competitive advantages in high-tech sectors, fostering innovation, and ensuring equitable growth through mechanisms like universal education and shared growth principles (World Bank 1993; Lin 2012). The transition from 'producer-driven' to 'buyer-driven' commodity chains in the 1970s and 1980s marked a change in industrial policy approaches, highlighting the role of global lead firms and suppliers in shaping industrial strategies (Gereffi 2014). This shift to GVC-oriented policies necessitates a sophisticated understanding of global industrial organization, targeting specialized niches rather than building vertically integrated domestic industries (Gereffi 2014). Additionally, the collapse of the Soviet bloc and the rise of free market ideologies further entrenched neoliberal economic policies globally (Chang and Andreoni 2020).

Recently, industrial policy has evolved to include 'open economy' strategies that blend import replacement with export orientation, employing reciprocal control mechanisms where state assistance is contingent on performance (Wade 2010). Countries like Brazil have pivoted towards renewable energy sources for economic and security reasons, reflecting a broader trend towards sustainable industrial policies (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Moreover, the shift from social democratic capitalism to liberal meritocratic capitalism has led to weakened labor unions and increased inequality, influencing industrial policy approaches (Milanovic 2020). The post-World War II order, underpinned by Fordist regulation and U.S. hegemony, provided stability and liquidity through the dollar/gold standard, shaping industrial policy frameworks (Cafruny and Ryner 2007).

Overall, the evolution of industrial policy underscores a complex interplay between state intervention and market mechanisms, adapting to global economic dynamics while addressing domestic socio-economic goals. This includes a shift towards more collaborative approaches involving the private sector, as exemplified by the East Asian developmental state model, which emphasizes market mechanisms and private property rights while still allowing for strategic state intervention (Shiraishi, Sonobe 2019). The transition in industrial policy from the post-World War II period to the present day also reflects a move from focusing primarily on economic recovery and capital transfers to emphasizing technology transfer and productivity enhancement (Comin and Hobijn 2010).

### Inclusive and Sustainable Growth
The transformation of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day underscores a significant shift towards integrating inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality into its framework. Initially, industrial policies were predominantly focused on enhancing productivity and competitiveness through state-led initiatives and protectionist measures. Over time, however, there has been an increasing recognition of the necessity to address broader societal goals, such as ecological sustainability and social equity, alongside traditional economic objectives (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Wade 2010; Maltais and Suljada 2025).

Contemporary industrial policy now frequently incorporates environmental objectives, such as decarbonization and the adoption of renewable energy technologies, as part of a structural transformation towards a low-carbon economy. This shift is evident in the integration of green industrial policies, which aim to balance economic, social, and environmental objectives, marking a departure from traditional industrial policies that prioritized economic growth and productivity (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Mazzucato 2013; Maltais and Suljada 2025).

Moreover, the focus on inclusive growth has led to the incorporation of social objectives, such as reducing inequality and supporting women's entrepreneurship, into industrial policy frameworks. Initiatives like the Women Entrepreneurship Strategy in Canada and gender-balance certifications in Costa Rica aim to promote gender equality and empower marginalized groups (OECD 2021; UNIDO 2024; Suarez and Santiago Rodriguez ND). The inclusiveness of industrial policy also depends on constructing 'productive coalitions' that invest in collective productive capabilities and engage in equitable value distribution (Chang and Andreoni 2020).

The integration of human rights and gender equality into industrial policy is further supported by international frameworks like the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), which emphasize the importance of inclusive and sustainable industrialization. These frameworks encourage the adoption of policies that promote equitable growth and ensure that the benefits of industrial development are shared more broadly across society (UNIDO 2024; Ferrannini et al. 2021; Fernandes and Reed 2026).

Recent shifts in industrial policy approaches have been influenced by the expansion of talent available to governments due to rising education levels and a more supportive political environment for development objectives, contributing to stronger macroeconomic management and service delivery (Fernandes and Reed 2026). The United States, for example, has shifted from conducting industrial policy under the radar to explicitly articulating it as an essential component of federal government policy, with new investment priorities like the Inflation Reduction Act aimed at speeding up the green transition (Armstrong and Wu 2024; Bivens 2023).

The role of the state in industrial policy has transformed from a passive facilitator to an active participant, with governments in late-industrializing countries like Korea taking on a visionary role in industrialization, making pivotal investment decisions, and imposing performance standards on firms, which has contributed to more structured and performance-based industrial policies that consider broader social and environmental goals (Amsden 1989). This evolution highlights the importance of aligning industrial policy with global sustainability goals and ensuring that it contributes to the creation of more equitable and inclusive societies (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Stiglitz et al. 2013).

The shift in industrial policy from the post-World War II period to the present day involves a transition from state-led, sector-specific interventions aimed at correcting market failures and promoting heavy industries, to more nuanced approaches that emphasize export orientation, technological advancement, and the creation of competitive advantages in high-tech sectors, reflecting a broader consideration of inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality (Wade 1990; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020). This transformation is also characterized by a move from 'picking winners' to 'making winners,' where governments create environments conducive to the viability of new industries by shaping the social structure of investment and guiding resources into strategic sectors (Wade 1990).

The evolution of industrial policy has increasingly incorporated considerations of ecological sustainability, human rights, and gender equality, as evidenced by the shift towards non-energy-intensive and technology-intensive activities, and the establishment of policies that promote inclusive growth and respect for human rights (Wade 1990; Maltais and Suljada 2025). The integration of SDGs into industrial policy has led to the development of mission-oriented strategies that are transformation-oriented, focusing on the direction of innovation rather than its level, and requiring coordination across policy domains and stakeholders to address societal challenges (OECD 2021).

Overall, the shift in industrial policy approaches over time reflects a broader understanding of development as a multidimensional process that encompasses economic, social, and environmental dimensions. This evolution highlights the importance of aligning industrial policy with global sustainability goals and ensuring that it contributes to the creation of more equitable and inclusive societies (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Stiglitz et al. 2013; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020).

### Role of the State
The evolution of the state's role in industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day reflects a dynamic interplay between changing economic paradigms and global challenges. Initially, industrial policy was heavily characterized by direct state intervention and protectionism, with governments actively promoting industrialization through import substitution and state ownership of key industries (World Bank 1991; Amsden 1989). This approach was prevalent across both developed and developing nations, where the state played a pivotal role in directing economic activities and managing market failures (Wade 1990; Lin 2012).

However, the 1980s marked a significant shift towards market-oriented approaches, influenced by neoliberal policies that emphasized deregulation, privatization, and reduced state intervention (Chang 2007; Warwick 2013). The limitations of this neoliberal approach, particularly highlighted by the 2008 financial crisis, led to a renewed interest in industrial policy that focuses on strategic facilitation and partnerships with the private sector (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021). This modern approach underscores the state's role in fostering innovation, supporting technological advancement, and addressing market failures through strategic interventions rather than direct control (Mazzucato 2013; Rodrik 2004).

Contemporary industrial policy now integrates considerations of inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality, reflecting a broader understanding of development that extends beyond mere economic growth to encompass social and environmental objectives (Stiglitz et al. 2013; UNCTAD 2014). This is evident in the adoption of mission-oriented strategies that align industrial policy with broader societal goals, such as the Sustainable Development Goals (OECD 2021). The shift from 'government' to 'governance' involves participatory governance and collective appraisal, moving away from simplistic hierarchical models (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Recent conceptualizations of industrial policy have expanded to include 'horizontal' policies affecting the entire national economy, 'selective' policies targeted at specific industries, and GVC-oriented policies leveraging international supply chain linkages (Gereffi 2014). The economic rationale for industrial policy has shifted to focus on internalizing externalities and providing public goods, such as workforce training and infrastructure, essential for addressing market failures and promoting economic performance (Armstrong and Wu 2024). Successful industrial policy requires clear objectives, as pursuing multiple goals simultaneously can lead to missed targets (Armstrong and Wu 2024).

The evolution of industrial policy highlights a transition from state-led, protectionist strategies to a more nuanced role for the state as a facilitator and partner in economic development, balancing market forces with strategic interventions to achieve inclusive and sustainable growth (World Bank 1997; Lin 2012). This includes a systemic approach that coordinates innovation, regional policy, and trade policy, with manufacturing at its core, affecting upstream and downstream industries, sectoral change, clusters, and networks (Aiginger and Rodrik 2020). The modern approach also emphasizes the importance of governance mechanisms and implementation processes shaped by a normative societal vision and a new understanding of industrial policy economics, political economy, and management (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

In Korea, the evolution of industrial policy reflects a shift from foreign-controlled economic policies to state-driven industrial diversification, with the government playing a visionary role in industrialization. This approach emphasizes state coordination and supervisory guidance over economic strength, rather than relying solely on market forces, and includes considerations for inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality (Amsden 1989). The Korean model illustrates a reciprocal relationship between the state and firms, where the state provides subsidies in exchange for performance standards, indicating a move towards more structured and performance-based industrial policy (Amsden 1989).

The transformation in industrial policy approaches has also been influenced by global economic governance changes, such as WTO accession and trade agreements, which have constrained traditional policy measures but also prompted countries like China and Brazil to adapt by focusing on domestic capabilities and strategic sectors (UNCTAD 2014). The shift in industrial policy approaches over time has been influenced by the need to balance national policy autonomy with international economic integration, as seen in the historical context of the Bretton Woods system and the Havana Charter (UNCTAD 2014).

Contemporary green industrial policies are designed to address global environmental failures and are characterized by a more interventionist role of the state, contrasting with earlier industrial policies that were primarily focused on national competitiveness and economic growth without explicit consideration of global fairness or inclusivity (Maltais and Suljada 2025).

### Innovation and Technological Advancement
The evolution of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day underscores a pivotal shift towards prioritizing innovation and technological advancement as core elements of industrial strategies. Initially, industrial policies were predominantly centered on manufacturing and protectionist measures. Over time, however, there has been a pronounced transition towards fostering knowledge generation, research and development (R&D), and high-tech industries (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021). This shift is marked by the integration of learning processes and absorptive capacities, which serve to bridge the gap between scientific discovery and practical application (Ferrannini et al. 2021). Nonetheless, the disconnection between learning and production remains a noted shortcoming in current innovation paradigms (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Amsden 1989).

Modern industrial policies emphasize the role of innovation systems, which involve interactive processes among scientific, entrepreneurial, and governmental actors to foster technological advancement and economic growth (World Bank 2007). The focus has expanded beyond R&D to include the development of innovation ecosystems that support sustainable and inclusive growth (UNCTAD 2018; Khorsheed 2015). This includes strategic public-private partnerships, investment in education and training, and the promotion of green technologies (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Mazzucato 2013; OECD 2021). Successful countries have integrated technological innovation with environmental and social objectives, reflecting a broader commitment to sustainable development (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; UNIDO 2024; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020).

The role of the state has evolved from merely correcting market failures to actively shaping markets and driving technological revolutions through mission-oriented policies and long-term investments (Mazzucato 2013; Lin 2012). This approach underscores the importance of government intervention in fostering innovation and ensuring that technological advancements contribute to inclusive and sustainable growth (Stiglitz et al. 2013; Lin and Chang 2009). The shift towards innovation and technological advancement in industrial policy reflects a broader understanding of the need to integrate economic, social, and environmental objectives in pursuit of long-term prosperity.

Countries like Germany and China have implemented policies that integrate technological innovation with environmental and social objectives, demonstrating a broader commitment to sustainable development (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; UNIDO 2024). Germany's Energiewende and China's New Energy Vehicle Industry Development Plan exemplify how industrial policies are being used to drive technological advancement while addressing environmental challenges (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; UNIDO 2024).

The shift in industrial policy approaches has been accompanied by a change in the investment policy toolkit, moving from restrictive measures to more open and facilitative policies that aim to attract foreign direct investment and integrate domestic industries into global value chains, while also addressing new challenges posed by technological advancements and the need for sustainable growth (UNCTAD 2018; Watkins and Fowler 2002). The evolution of industrial policy has seen a shift towards integrating inclusive and sustainable growth, as current green industrial policies emphasize technology transfer, equitable integration into green value chains, and increased international climate finance to support emerging markets and developing economies, reflecting a broader commitment to global equity and human rights (Maltais and Suljada 2025; Xian 2024).

The transition towards post-industrial economies is driven by technological progress in manufacturing, shifts in demand towards services, and increased knowledge-intensity in manufacturing (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Yülek 2018). Germany's renewable energy law (Erneuerbare-Energien-Gesetz) established principles such as energy source-specific feed-in tariffs, grid connection priority, and burden sharing of costs, which have shifted the approach to industrial policy by promoting renewable energy industries while safeguarding competitiveness (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). The renewable energy amendment of 2014 in Germany introduced technology-specific, yearly caps for additional capacity and changed the determination method for feed-in tariffs from pre-determination to tendering, reflecting a shift towards more efficient policies (Altenburg and Assmann 2017).

The definitions and approaches to industrial policy have shifted to prioritize digitalization and energy transition, with a focus on improving productivity, enhancing technological spinoffs, and promoting global public goods like renewable energy to avoid climate change (Berasaluce 2024; Wade 1990). The shift in industrial policy from the post-World War II period to the present day involves a transition from traditional import substitution strategies to a new model that emphasizes a mixed, market-based approach with strategic public-private partnerships, focusing on innovation and the role of non-market institutions, which aligns with contemporary goals of inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality (UNCTAD 2006; Warwick 2013).

## What challenges and constraints do less-industrialized countries face in realizing effective industrial policy?
## Question Overview
Less-industrialized countries face multifaceted challenges in implementing effective industrial policies, primarily due to external constraints such as protectionist measures by industrialized nations and restrictive global trade agreements. These external pressures limit market access and policy autonomy, exacerbating internal issues like weak governance, corruption, and inadequate institutional capacity. Technological and innovation barriers further hinder progress, as limited access to advanced technologies and restrictive intellectual property regimes prevent these countries from moving up the value chain. Financial constraints, including high interest rates and dependency on external financial flows, compound these challenges, while environmental and sustainability concerns add another layer of complexity. The interplay of these factors creates a challenging environment for less-industrialized countries, requiring nuanced strategies that balance economic growth with sustainable development and navigate the intricate landscape of global economic policies.

### Protectionist Measures and Market Access
Less-industrialized countries encounter formidable obstacles in implementing effective industrial policies, primarily due to protectionist measures enforced by industrialized nations. These measures, including high tariffs, quotas, and subsidies, restrict market access and exacerbate economic vulnerabilities, particularly in sectors where developing countries hold competitive advantages, such as agriculture and labor-intensive manufacturing (World Bank 2010; Watkins and Fowler 2002; Gereffi 2014; Maltais & Suljada 2025). The trade policies of wealthier nations often involve tariff escalation, imposing higher duties on processed goods, which discourages value addition and limits export diversification in less-industrialized countries (World Bank 2005; Watkins and Fowler 2002; Gereffi 2014; Walleri 1978).

The revival of industrial policy in advanced economies further threatens the industrial opportunities available to less-industrialized countries, narrowing their development pathways. This threat is intensified by the financial and technological advantages that wealthier countries can exploit, potentially dominating sectors critical for the industrialization of poorer nations (Fernandes and Reed 2026; UNCTAD 2018; Mazzucato 2013; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020). Competitive pressures from advanced economies, along with restrictive international trade agreements, further constrain the policy space of these countries, hindering their ability to implement effective industrial strategies (Wade 1990; World Bank 1991; Rodrik 2014; Irwin 2023).

Internally, less-industrialized countries struggle with inadequate infrastructure, poor governance, and limited access to finance, which impede their global competitiveness and the development of robust industrial sectors (World Bank 2010; Watkins and Fowler 2002). These internal challenges are compounded by external economic pressures, such as high transport costs and volatile global commodity markets, which disproportionately affect landlocked and low-income economies (World Bank 2010; Páez 2018). Additionally, the lack of technical capacity and resources to meet stringent international standards and non-tariff barriers further restricts their market access and competitiveness (Fliess and Lejarraga 2005; UNIDO 2024; Maltais & Suljada 2025).

Reliance on transnational corporations can lead to import-intensive or deficit-prone industrialization, as evidenced in Latin America, where imports of capital and intermediate goods have outpaced exports, marginalizing small and medium enterprises (Wade 2010). The low tradability of services in developing countries also weakens their export capabilities, resulting in slower growth (Páez 2018). The wider dispersion of tariff rates across products in developing economies compared to advanced ones suggests selective protection of certain industries (Fernandes and Reed 2026).

In sum, the combination of protectionist measures by industrialized countries and internal constraints within less-industrialized nations creates a complex environment that significantly impedes their industrial policy effectiveness and economic development prospects. These challenges are further complicated by the need to balance protectionist policies with attracting foreign investment, as illustrated by Taiwan's experience in the automobile industry (Wade 1990). Moreover, the resurgence of green industrial policies in major economies presents a dual challenge: less-industrialized countries risk being confined to low-value extractive roles without capturing higher-value production stages, while market distortions from domestic subsidies and trade barriers in wealthier countries limit their competitiveness and inclusion in global green value chains (Maltais & Suljada 2025).

### Global Trade Structures and Agreements
Less-industrialized countries face formidable challenges in crafting effective industrial policies, largely due to the constraints imposed by global trade structures and agreements. The dominance of multinational corporations within global value chains significantly limits the policy space and opportunities available to local firms, thereby hindering their industrial competitiveness (Gereffi 2014; Chang and Andreoni 2020). Bilateral and multilateral trade agreements, particularly those under the WTO, often prioritize the interests of developed countries, restricting traditional industrial policy tools such as subsidies and local content requirements, and consequently reducing policy autonomy for less-industrialized nations (Rodrik 2004; Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Fernandes and Reed 2026). Historical legacies of colonialism and unequal treaties further exacerbate these structural constraints, impeding upward mobility within the value chain (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Amsden 1989).

The resurgence of industrial policy in developed countries poses additional risks by potentially pre-empting entry-level industrial opportunities, thus narrowing development pathways for less-industrialized countries (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; UNIDO 2024; World Bank 2006). International investment agreements (IIAs) and other commitments further constrain policy space by regulating subsidies and performance requirements, creating administrative complexity and uncertainty (UNCTAD 2018). The expansion of global supply chains, particularly in East Asia, presents additional challenges as less-industrialized countries struggle to regulate their links to the global economy, including trade, foreign direct investment, and exchange rates (Gereffi 2014).

Power dynamics within global economic arrangements further affect policy space, with less-industrialized countries facing challenges from transnational corporations that create entry barriers and squeeze suppliers in oligopolistic markets (Chang and Andreoni 2020). The increasing financialization of the world economy and changes in global economic rules further complicate industrial policy-making (Chang and Andreoni 2020). Moreover, the structure of the global economic system, which is not open-ended, limits opportunities for upward mobility and industrial development (Wade 2010; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020).

Environmental policies and international trade and investment laws also pose challenges, as they may restrict green industrial policies and impose conditions that are difficult for developing countries to meet without compensatory transfers from developed nations (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). The interaction between international investment policies and industrial policies creates additional hurdles, as evidenced by Ethiopia's industrial parks struggling after losing trade preferences (Fernandes and Reed 2026).

The expiration of trade agreements like AGOA and shifts in global manufacturing dynamics, such as China's retreat from labor-intensive industries, create both opportunities and constraints for less-industrialized countries. These nations must navigate competitive pressures from other developing countries and the potential for automation to close traditional development pathways (Sandefur and Subramanian 2024; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020). The declining global demand for manufactures as a share of GDP suggests that manufacturing- and export-based growth strategies may no longer be suitable for today's low-income countries, although some African economies still have scope to expand their share of manufacturing exports (Sandefur and Subramanian 2024).

The constraints faced by less-industrialized countries are further exacerbated by globalization's adverse effects, such as the erosion of worker rights, exclusion from global economic opportunities due to informal economies, and insufficient investment in education and technological capabilities, which are crucial for global economic participation (WCSDG 2004; Adewale and Bank-Ola 2025). The global financial system's dominance by industrialized countries limits policy autonomy and strategic intervention capabilities, while agricultural protectionism and high tariffs on textiles and clothing undermine comparative advantages (WCSDG 2004). Additionally, the lack of a coherent global framework addressing socio-economic security and the imbalanced outcomes of multilateral agreements restrict policy options and market access (WCSDG 2004).

The interplay between global governance structures and domestic institutional weaknesses exacerbates challenges, as less-industrialized countries often lack the power and resources to influence international decision-making processes that affect them (World Bank 2006). The inequitable distribution of power and resources in global markets and institutions limits their ability to implement effective industrial policies, as they face systemic disadvantages in negotiations and rule-setting processes (World Bank 2006). The resurgence of industrial policy in highly industrialized countries poses a risk by potentially pre-empting entry-level industrial opportunities, thereby narrowing development pathways and exacerbating existing inequalities in global markets (World Bank 2006; Bivens 2023).

Overall, the intricate web of global trade structures and agreements, compounded by historical legacies, the dominance of multinational corporations, and the resurgence of industrial policy in developed countries, collectively narrows the development pathways available to less-industrialized countries. These challenges are further complicated by the need to navigate diverse economic and social characteristics, geopolitical relationships, and the shifting organization of global production (Gereffi 2014; Chang and Andreoni 2020; Amsden 1989).

### Governance and Corruption Challenges
The implementation of industrial policy in less-industrialized countries is severely hampered by weak governance structures and pervasive corruption, which collectively undermine policy effectiveness and economic development (World Bank 2005; Gereffi 2014; Rodrik 2011). Corruption functions as a de facto tax on entrepreneurial activities, distorting policymaking processes and eroding governmental credibility (World Bank 2005). This environment encourages rent-seeking behaviors, where influential interest groups manipulate policies for personal gain, often to the detriment of public welfare (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Stiglitz et al. 2013; Amsden 1989). These practices are further exacerbated by information asymmetries between lobbyists and the public sector, facilitating the justification of subsidies that may not align with national interests (Altenburg and Assmann 2017).

Political capture and cronyism further complicate the landscape of industrial policy, as decisions are frequently swayed by vested interests rather than empirical evidence or considerations of public welfare (Rodrik 2014; Stiglitz et al. 2013). This results in policies that entrench existing power structures and inhibit structural change, as exemplified by the Philippines, where landed elites have historically used personalized rules to maintain rent sources and resist industrial transformation (Wade 2010). Additionally, the lack of bureaucratic capacity and institutional coordination undermines policy effectiveness, as demonstrated by Colombia's failure to boost employment or productivity through tariffs due to evasion and fraud (Fernandes and Reed 2026).

The political economy of industrial policy is further complicated by the distribution of power across organizations, which influences institutional change and policy outcomes (Ferrannini et al. 2021). Industrial policies are often subject to political modifications and reversals, deterring investment and undermining long-term policy commitments (Juhász and Lane 2024). Moreover, the presence of 'limited access social orders' in developing countries contrasts with the 'open social orders' of developed nations, leading to regulatory capture and inefficiencies (Wade 2010; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020).

The 'Brazil cost,' characterized by poor infrastructure, excessive bureaucracy, corruption, and high interest rates, exemplifies the challenges faced by less-industrialized countries in realizing effective industrial policy (Gereffi 2014). In Brazil, government policies have generated significant uncertainty and unpredictability, adversely affecting sectors such as ethanol (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Similarly, China's industrial policies have been marked by cycles of excessive subsidy competition among provincial governments, necessitating national intervention to consolidate industries and address overinvestment (Armstrong and Wu 2024).

Without an appropriate analytical framework, policymakers risk exacerbating government failures, particularly during times requiring extraordinary interventions to ensure social resilience and protect people, households, ecological systems, and businesses (Ferrannini et al. 2021). The challenge is compounded by the inability of certain social groups to organize and influence policy-making processes, which is crucial for effective industrial policy (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Adewale and Bank-Ola 2025).

Less-industrialized countries also face external constraints such as trade barriers imposed by high-income countries, which, when combined with internal issues like limited access to finance and policy uncertainty, undermine their investment climate and economic development potential (World Bank 2007; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020). These countries must balance market-friendly interventions with political and social constraints, often resulting in inefficient policies and practices that hinder development (World Bank 1991). Internal factors such as weak administrative capacity, entrenched special interests, and inadequate infrastructure are compounded by external pressures like trade barriers and limited access to technology (World Bank 1991).

In summary, the challenges of governance and corruption in less-industrialized countries create a complex environment where industrial policy is frequently subverted by political and economic interests, leading to ineffective outcomes and hindering sustainable development. The necessity for tailored strategies that operate within existing political environments is critical, as illustrated by Thailand's eventual success with export promotion following initial setbacks (Juhász and Lane 2024).

### Institutional Capacity and Policy Frameworks
The implementation of effective industrial policy in less-industrialized countries is significantly hindered by a confluence of internal and external constraints. Internally, these nations often contend with weak institutional capacity, inadequate infrastructure, and limited fiscal resources, which collectively impede their ability to design and execute policies that foster growth and development (World Bank 1991; Lin 2012; Gereffi 2014). The absence of reliable data and statistical capacity further complicates policy formulation and evaluation, obstructing the assessment of economic performance and informed decision-making (World Bank 2010; Armstrong and Wu 2024). Additionally, the instability of industrial policy regimes, characterized by rapidly changing local production incentives and import tariff levels, poses significant challenges to growth, particularly affecting small and medium-sized enterprises (Gereffi 2014; Ayyagari et al. 2011).

Externally, global economic structures and international trade agreements constrain the policy space of less-industrialized countries, limiting their ability to implement tailored industrial strategies. The influence of international financial institutions like the IMF and World Bank often imposes policy conditions that prioritize liberalization and fiscal austerity over developmental needs, thereby restricting these countries' autonomy to pursue context-specific industrial policies (Stiglitz 2002; Rodrik 2001; Fernandes and Reed 2026; AWID 2013). High tariffs and trade barriers imposed by high-income countries further restrict market access and competitiveness, exacerbating industrialization challenges (World Bank 2005). Moreover, the presence of global contract manufacturers, while offering immediate advantages such as job creation, underscores the dependency on imports for critical inputs, as illustrated by Brazil's reliance on imported aircraft-grade aluminum despite its bauxite abundance (Gereffi 2014).

The interplay of these factors creates a complex environment where less-industrialized countries must navigate both domestic and international pressures to realize effective industrial policy. Strategic government intervention is crucial to address market failures, such as information asymmetries and coordination problems, but is often hampered by limited administrative capacity and political constraints (Lin 2012; Wade 1990; Ferrannini et al. 2021). The historical legacy of inappropriate development strategies, such as import-substitution industrialization, has led to distorted industrial structures misaligned with comparative advantages, complicating efforts to achieve sustainable industrial growth (Lin 2012; Ferrannini et al. 2021).

To overcome these challenges, less-industrialized countries require a nuanced approach that balances liberalization with strategic government intervention, tailored to their unique economic and institutional contexts. This involves strengthening institutional capacity, improving infrastructure, and fostering government-business cooperation within a pragmatic developmental state framework (UNCTAD 2006; Stiglitz, Lin, Monga 2013; Ferrannini et al. 2021). Additionally, reforms in global governance structures are necessary to expand the policy space available to these countries, enabling them to pursue industrialization strategies not overshadowed by the industrial policies of more developed nations (WCSDG 2004; Whitsel et al. 2024). The industrial policy debate has historically had a supply-side bias, often overlooking the influence of demand management, which is crucial for effective policy implementation (Chang and Andreoni 2020). Moreover, the neo-classical economics approach operates at a level of abstraction too high to capture key factors responsible for uneven development, posing challenges for less-industrialized countries (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Furthermore, less-industrialized countries face specific challenges such as identifying industries with latent comparative advantages, which requires reviewing imports to target simple manufacturing goods for industrial upgrading (Lin 2012). The barriers preventing the emergence of new industries are less binding for nearby industries, which only require slight adaptations of existing inputs (Lin 2012). However, the discipline of time-bound withdrawal of concessions and timely exit from unsuccessful policies is difficult to obtain (Lin 2012). Additionally, rigid labor regulations pose constraints, as seen in Mauritius, which addressed this by allowing employment flexibility in export zones while maintaining domestic regulations (Lin 2012). The market mechanism may not be sufficient to enable firms to overcome problems of information, coordination, and externality, necessitating government intervention (Lin 2012). Finally, the political economy and institutional frameworks significantly influence the types of industrial policies pursued and their effectiveness, as seen in the varied success of countries like Thailand and Nigeria (Das ND; Juhász and Lane 2024; Ikonne and Nwogwugwu 2020).

The effectiveness of industrial policy is further constrained by the difficulty in identifying and compensating for information externalities and coordination failures, which are critical for fostering industrial upgrading and diversification (Lin 2012). Less-industrialized countries also struggle with the dual challenge of needing to upgrade their industrial structures while simultaneously addressing the constraints of limited human capital and infrastructure, which are necessary for supporting industrial upgrading and diversification (Lin 2012). The failure to align industrial strategies with comparative advantages often results in inefficient resource allocation and economic instability (Lin 2012). Moreover, the transition management approach, which involves strategic planning, stakeholder engagement, and policy learning, is crucial for overcoming challenges of lock-in and market failures, but requires significant adaptation to local contexts and capabilities (Kemp and Never 2017; Altenburg and Assmann 2017).

### Technological and Innovation Barriers
Less-industrialized countries encounter formidable technological and innovation barriers that significantly impede the implementation of effective industrial policies. These nations often struggle with limited access to advanced technologies and inadequate research and development (R&D) capabilities, which are further exacerbated by restrictive international intellectual property regimes. Such constraints hinder their ability to ascend the value chain within global value chains (GVCs), leaving them entrenched in low-value-added activities with minimal opportunities for learning and advancement (Gereffi 2014; Chang and Andreoni 2020). The dominance of foreign firms and the lack of domestic innovation capacity further compound these challenges, as evidenced by Malaysia's stagnating export value added and limited technological progress (Wade 2010). Similarly, large emerging economies like Brazil face difficulties in enhancing the technological content of their exports, particularly in trade with China, underscoring the challenges of moving up the value chain (Gereffi 2014; Bogers et al. 2019).

International intellectual property regimes, such as the TRIPS agreement, impose additional constraints by limiting technology transfer and innovation. These regimes make it challenging for less-industrialized countries to engage in reverse engineering and imitation, historically crucial for technological catch-up (Rodrik 2004; Chang 2007; Akyüz 2008). Such frameworks often favor developed countries, increasing the cost of technology acquisition and restricting the ability of developing nations to adapt and build on advanced technologies (Watkins and Fowler 2002; World Bank 2010). The persistence of intellectual property rights-related institutions in post-colonial societies continues to impact economic development, posing constraints on inclusive industrial policies (Sappa 2026).

The rapid advancement of automation and digital technologies further erodes the competitive advantage of low-wage labor, leading to premature de-industrialization and limiting the potential for manufacturing to absorb labor and drive economic growth (Páez 2018; Fernandes and Reed 2026). This shift necessitates significant investment in human capital and infrastructure to support the adoption of new technologies, which many less-industrialized countries lack (UNIDO 2024; Lin 2012). Moreover, manufacturing has become more capital- and skill-intensive, reducing its ability to absorb large amounts of non-educated labor (Páez 2018; Miketa 2001).

The global emphasis on green technologies presents both opportunities and challenges. While there is potential for developing countries to engage in green industrial value chains, they often lack the necessary infrastructure, skills, and policy frameworks to capitalize on these opportunities (Maltais & Suljada 2025; Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Market failures and the lock-in of existing technologies hinder the adoption of cleaner technologies, requiring government intervention and international support to overcome these barriers (Kemp and Never 2017; Apeaning and Thollander 2013). Additionally, the transition to a service-oriented economy is fraught with challenges, including potential increases in inequality and the dominance of tech giants, which may inhibit these countries' ability to participate meaningfully in the global marketplace (Stiglitz 2018).

Financial constraints, exacerbated by the COVID-19 crisis, regulatory and policy uncertainty, and disparities in R&D spending collectively hinder the ability of less-industrialized countries to innovate and compete globally (OECD 2021). These countries also face the dual challenge of needing to defy their current comparative advantage to achieve industrial upgrading while dealing with limited factor mobility and technological capabilities (Lin and Chang 2009). The resurgence of industrial policy in highly industrialized countries poses a risk by potentially pre-empting or displacing entry-level industrial opportunities crucial for development (World Bank 1992; Mazzucato).

Overall, less-industrialized countries must navigate a complex landscape of technological constraints, international policy regimes, and rapidly changing global markets to realize effective industrial policy and foster sustainable economic development. This requires a strategic focus on building technological capabilities, enhancing human capital, and fostering innovation ecosystems that can support industrial upgrading and economic diversification (Ferrannini et al. 2021; UNCTAD 2018). Government intervention is necessary to address market failures in technology development, manpower training, and credit allocation, which are critical for competing with more developed countries (Wade 1990; Amsden 1989).

### Financial and Economic Constraints
Less-industrialized countries grapple with a complex web of financial and economic constraints that severely limit the efficacy of their industrial policies. These constraints include fiscal limitations, high interest rates, underdeveloped financial systems, and a dependency on external financial flows (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Páez 2018; Akyüz 2008). High real interest rates, notably in Brazil and South Africa, deter investment in the manufacturing sector, which is capital-intensive and reliant on substantial borrowing (Chang and Andreoni 2020). Overvalued currencies further compound these issues by adversely affecting export industries, particularly manufacturing, although industrial policies such as subsidized credit can offer partial relief (Chang and Andreoni 2020).

The financialization of the global economy and the absence of robust global regulations have diminished governments' capacities to sustain favorable macroeconomic conditions and execute effective industrial policies (Chang and Andreoni 2020). This situation is exacerbated by the international financial architecture's failure to efficiently channel financial resources, leaving countries vulnerable to financial capital flow instability and macroeconomic shocks (Chang and Andreoni 2020). In regions like Latin America, reliance on commodity cycles and external financial support leads to vulnerability when these cycles end, resulting in missed opportunities for structural transformation (Páez 2018).

Fiscal constraints are further intensified by high debt levels and limited fiscal space, restricting the ability of less-industrialized countries to implement effective industrial policies. For example, Latin America faces a combined general government gross debt of 61.8% of GDP, which limits fiscal maneuverability (Páez 2018). Dependency on export commodities and foreign capital inflows temporarily alleviates fiscal restrictions but leaves countries susceptible to shocks when commodity prices collapse (Páez 2018).

Moreover, financial systems in these countries are often underdeveloped, with limited access to credit for small and medium-sized enterprises, which are crucial for industrial growth (World Bank 2010). The lack of financial institutions capable of supporting short-term investments that yield long-term benefits hinders the implementation of green industrial policies (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Additionally, the high cost of capital and fiscal constraints make it challenging for these countries to attract green investment, essential for sustainable industrial development (Maltais and Suljada 2025).

The global financial cycle, influenced by the economic conditions and monetary policy decisions of developed countries, creates macroeconomic instability and financial effects that do not align with the needs of less-industrialized countries, further constraining their ability to implement effective industrial policies (UNCTAD 2014). This is compounded by the erosion of fiscal space due to globalization-induced tax competition and transfer pricing abuses by transnational corporations, which undermine domestic revenue generation and fiscal stability (UNCTAD 2014).

Increased inequality within countries, partly a product of globalization, poses additional challenges, as economic gains from participating in global supply chains do not necessarily translate into good jobs or stable employment, potentially leading to social downgrading (Milanovic 2020; Gereffi 2014). The 'Brazil cost,' characterized by poor infrastructure, excessive bureaucracy, corruption, and high interest rates, further complicates the realization of effective industrial policy in less-industrialized countries (Gereffi 2014).

Small countries face unique challenges due to their limited market size, which is insufficient to attract significant foreign direct investment (FDI), and their domestic firms tend to be small-scale and less advanced (Gereffi 2014). This is further complicated by the concentration of FDI in a small number of developing countries, leaving the vast majority with very little (Wade 2010).

The private financial sector's focus on short-term finance instead of the long-term finance needed for infrastructural and industrial projects poses a challenge for less-industrialized countries in realizing effective industrial policy (UNCTAD 2014). Foreign capital flows to developing and transition economies may generate macroeconomic instability, external imbalances, and boom-and-bust credit episodes, which are highly dependent on their amount, composition, and use (UNCTAD 2014).

Tax havens, secrecy jurisdictions, and illicit financial flows erode the tax base, undermine the fairness of the tax system, and distort trade and investment patterns, posing challenges to less-industrialized countries (UNCTAD 2014). Inadequate tax rules and difficulties in enforcing them prevent developing countries from fully exploiting the potential fiscal revenues from extractive industries (UNCTAD 2014). The problem of 'Dutch disease', whereby an expanding mining sector triggers a real currency appreciation and a fall in output and employment in other tradable sectors, can introduce serious macroeconomic imbalances and increase exchange rate volatility and economic vulnerability (UNCTAD 2014).

Overall, the financial and economic constraints faced by less-industrialized countries are multifaceted and deeply interconnected, requiring comprehensive policy responses that address both internal structural weaknesses and external economic pressures to enable effective industrial policy implementation. Policymakers must navigate these challenges while considering the increased inequality and social costs associated with globalization and financialization (Stiglitz et al. 2013; Ferrannini et al. 2021). Additionally, these countries must contend with policy uncertainty, inadequate infrastructure, and external pressures such as trade distortions from developed countries and international rules that may not align with local conditions (World Bank 2005). The credibility of government policies is often compromised by political instability and frequent policy shifts, which exacerbate policy uncertainty and deter investment (World Bank 2005).

### Environmental and Sustainability Challenges
Less-industrialized countries encounter substantial challenges in aligning their industrial policies with environmental sustainability, a task complicated by a web of economic, institutional, and international constraints. These nations must balance the dual imperatives of reducing poverty and improving livelihoods while adhering to international environmental agreements, which often restrict their industrial policy options (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). The Agenda 2030 for Sustainable Development highlights the importance of leveraging industry for enduring prosperity, yet industrial development is not neutral in its impact on human development, affecting the expansion of human capabilities (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

The transition to green industrial policies is impeded by financial, technological, and institutional deficits, necessitating significant international support for technology transfers and capacity development (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). International trade and investment laws further constrain the implementation of effective green industrial policies by restricting performance requirements and subsidies that could benefit domestic industries (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Developing countries also face the risk of technological and infrastructural lock-in, which could obstruct future green transformations, as they are often pressured to adopt renewable energy technologies and increase resource efficiency to align with global decarbonization trends (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). The absence of a single role model for sustainable development and the historical responsibility of developed nations for environmental degradation complicate the implementation of sound environmental policies (Altenburg and Assmann 2017).

Moreover, balancing short-term economic gains from natural resources with long-term sustainable development requires strategic government intervention to facilitate industrial diversification and address demographic pressures (Stiglitz et al. 2013). The resurgence of green industrial policies in major economies poses additional challenges, as these policies often prioritize domestic benefits and may exclude or hinder lower-income and emerging economies from participating in green industrial transitions (Maltais and Suljada 2025). Less-industrialized countries face limited policy options in response to environmental measures like the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), which could impose tariffs on goods linked to carbon emissions or deforestation (Fernandes and Reed 2026). Additionally, the challenge of increasing energy intensity as industries develop can hinder the ability to implement effective industrial policies aimed at reducing energy consumption and emissions (Miketa 2001).

Economic latecomers have the advantage of not being deeply locked into existing unsustainable infrastructures, allowing them to build in more sustainable ways (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). However, the current organization of economic transactions largely ignores the social cost of resource depletion and pollution, undermining the basis for future economic development and jeopardizing progress on social welfare (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Developing countries can gain economically from pursuing environmental integrity and have policy options available to accelerate transformation in ways that enhance well-being and environmental sustainability (Altenburg and Assmann 2017).

Overall, less-industrialized countries must navigate the compounded challenges of financial constraints, inadequate institutional mechanisms, and the need for international cooperation to address environmental issues, which are exacerbated by the pressures of meeting basic needs and managing scarce resources (World Bank 1992). The lack of resources and proper institutions limits citizens' capacity to react and adapt to environmental degradation, making them more vulnerable to it (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). The impact of manufacturing activities on environment-related SDGs needs to be closely monitored, and policies must accompany the transition to cleaner industrial production (OECD 2021). Additionally, these countries face the challenge of balancing industrialization with environmental sustainability, as industrialization often leads to increased CO2 emissions due to factors like population growth, GDP per capita, and energy intensity (Ghazali and Ali 2019).

### Conflict
The discourse surrounding industrial policy in less-industrialized countries is fraught with conflicts, particularly between the forces of trade liberalization and protectionism, as well as the tension between economic growth and environmental sustainability. Advocates of trade openness, often influenced by the Washington Consensus, assert that market-driven strategies can stimulate growth (Gereffi 2014). However, critics contend that such policies frequently exacerbate inequalities and social unrest due to insufficient state intervention to manage the resulting conflicts (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021). As previously noted, increased trade openness can lead to a decline in manufacturing value (Páez 2018), and the IMF highlights that industrial policies often involve trade-offs between competing objectives (Fernandes and Reed 2026). Trade liberalization has been shown to negatively impact manufacturing employment in developing regions, such as sub-Saharan Africa and Latin America, contributing to deindustrialization (Adewale and Bank-Ola 2025).

Selective industrial policies, which involve prioritizing certain sectors and technologies, are contentious due to their potential to generate conflicts (Chang and Andreoni 2020). These policies necessitate effective conflict management to shape the political economy of industrialization (Chang and Andreoni 2020). The rise of protectionism and economic nationalism in advanced economies further complicates matters, as these countries become less inclined to import from developing economies, thereby constraining traditional trade-led growth strategies (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Irwin 2023). As previously discussed, the resurgence of industrial policy in wealthy nations poses a threat to less-industrialized countries by potentially pre-empting entry-level industrial opportunities, thus narrowing development pathways (Wade 1990; UNCTAD 2018; World Bank 2012).

The dual challenge of pursuing economic development while adhering to environmental sustainability presents another significant conflict. Some advocate for prioritizing economic growth, with the intention of using the income generated for environmental remediation later (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). However, this approach is criticized for its short-term focus, which could lead to long-term environmental degradation and social unrest (Ferrannini et al. 2021). The additionality criterion of the Clean Development Mechanism has been criticized for creating perverse incentives, rewarding the absence of climate mitigation policies (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Green industrial policies in major economies risk leaving emerging markets and developing economies (EMDEs) with lower capacity for industrial policy measures behind, as they often focus too narrowly on domestic benefits and are insufficiently aligned with principles of global fairness (Maltais and Suljada 2025).

Furthermore, political constraints and disputes over climate policies illustrate the challenges in implementing green industrial policies, which are seen as complements rather than substitutes for carbon pricing (Juhász and Lane 2024). Less-industrialized countries also face difficulties in garnering public support for disruptive policies, especially when these involve conflicts with powerful vested interests (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). The politics of transitioning to sustainable technologies, such as the influence of incumbents from the gas and oil sector, further complicate these efforts (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Additionally, the framework for industrial policy becomes more complex when considering societal priorities beyond economic growth, as highlighted by the Covid-19 pandemic (Ferrannini et al. 2021). These conflicts underscore the intricate interplay of global economic policies and the unique challenges faced by less-industrialized countries in crafting effective industrial policies.

## What key recommendations can Oxfam make to more industrialized countries so that their industrial policies do less harm to less-industrialized countries?
## Question Overview
Oxfam's recommendations for industrialized countries to mitigate the negative impacts of their industrial policies on less-industrialized nations encompass several key themes. Firstly, trade liberalization and market access should be prioritized by reducing trade barriers and subsidies, ensuring compliance with international trade agreements, and supporting fair global trade rules. Secondly, industrial policy design should avoid pre-empting industrial opportunities in developing countries, incorporate transparency and accountability, and align with sustainable development goals. Thirdly, technology transfer and capacity building are crucial, with a focus on fostering international cooperation, supporting technological capabilities, and facilitating innovation. Environmental sustainability should be integrated into industrial policies, promoting green technologies and sustainable practices while avoiding policies that harm developing countries. Social equity and inclusion must be embedded in policy frameworks to ensure human rights and gender equality are upheld. Finally, conflict management strategies should be employed to address the adverse effects of protectionist policies and promote global cooperation, avoiding measures that could lead to economic inefficiencies and hinder development in less-industrialized countries.

### Trade Liberalization and Market Access
Oxfam advocates for industrialized countries to alleviate the negative effects of their industrial policies on less-industrialized nations by reducing trade barriers and subsidies, particularly in agriculture, textiles, and steel sectors, which are vital for the economic growth of developing countries (World Bank 1987; Watkins and Fowler 2002; Diao et al. 2005). Trade liberalization should be structured to enhance market access for these countries, facilitating increased export opportunities and economic development (World Bank 1980; Stiglitz 2002; Adewale and Bank-Ola 2025). This necessitates the removal of protectionist measures such as high tariffs and non-tariff barriers that have historically limited market access for less-industrialized countries (World Bank 1987; Watkins and Fowler 2002).

Industrialized nations are encouraged to implement WTO-compliant industrial policies to prevent retaliatory tariffs that could further restrict market access for developing countries (Fernandes and Reed 2026). They should also refrain from large-scale subsidies and trade restrictions that distort global markets and impede the development of less-industrialized countries (Aiginger and Rodrik 2020; Irwin 2023; Evenett et al. 2024). Instead, policies should focus on providing market access assistance and quality infrastructure to help businesses in less-industrialized countries connect with international buyers and establish product standards (Fernandes and Reed 2026). Market access assistance, including export promotion and supplier development programs, directly addresses information gaps and reduces costs (Fernandes and Reed 2026).

Oxfam further recommends that industrialized countries support the creation of fair global trade rules that offer equitable opportunities for all nations, acknowledging the diversity in national capacities and developmental needs (WCSDG 2004). This includes backing regional integration efforts in less-industrialized countries and ensuring that trade policies do not exacerbate existing inequalities (WCSDG 2004; Watkins and Fowler 2002). Additionally, industrialized countries should consider ending the chronic overvaluation of their currencies by actively managing their value relative to key trading partners to support the manufacturing sector (Bivens 2023).

Moreover, industrialized countries should avoid imposing rapid financial and capital market liberalization on less-industrialized countries, as such policies have historically led to economic instability (Stiglitz 2002). A gradual approach to trade liberalization is recommended, providing temporary protection to industries in less-industrialized countries, allowing them to develop competitiveness before facing full market exposure (Lin 2010). By aligning their industrial policies with international trade agreements and avoiding protectionist measures, industrialized countries can foster a more equitable global economic environment that supports the development of less-industrialized countries (Berasaluce 2024; Chang 2007).

Oxfam suggests that industrialized countries adopt 'open economy' industrial policies that include import replacement and use reciprocal control mechanisms where state assistance is contingent on performance, rather than merely granting trade protection (Wade 2010). Trade-neutral production subsidies, where governments support both domestic and export production, should be preferred when feasible (Fernandes and Reed 2026). Avoiding tariffs on capital equipment or raw materials is crucial, as they increase costs for downstream industries that may also be developing (Fernandes and Reed 2026).

To promote the expansion of African manufacturing exports, the U.S. could offer positive incentives to import specific goods from specific countries, such as through negative tariff rates or subsidies (Sandefur and Subramanian 2024). Strengthened AGOA trade preferences could serve as a pull mechanism to create bankable projects for DFC to lend to and invest in, reorienting its work towards job creation and industrialization in Africa (Sandefur and Subramanian 2024). Oxfam can recommend reinforcing enhanced market access through complementary actions that would create an ecosphere for sustained manufacturing activity in Africa (Sandefur and Subramanian 2024).

Finally, Oxfam advises that industrial policies in more industrialized countries should avoid creating unfair competition for less-industrialized countries, such as through subsidized agricultural over-production and export dumping, which can annex vital resources and foreclose development opportunities for poorer nations (Watkins and Fowler 2002). Industrialized countries should ensure their industrial policies are globally consistent, avoiding competitive reductions of costs and imports, and instead fostering robust domestic activity to support global trade recovery (UNCTAD 2014). To mitigate the risks posed by green industrial policies of more industrialized countries to less-industrialized countries, Oxfam recommends avoiding excessive localization requirements and protectionist measures, instead fostering partnerships that emphasize diverse regional competitive advantages (Maltais & Suljada 2025).

### Industrial Policy Design
The design of industrial policies in more industrialized countries must be carefully crafted to avoid undermining the industrial opportunities essential for the development of less-industrialized nations. These opportunities are pivotal for their economic growth and poverty alleviation (Gereffi 2014; Chang and Andreoni 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021; World Bank 1993; Amsden 1989). It is imperative that such policies do not restrict the policy autonomy of less-industrialized countries, thereby allowing them to pursue their own industrialization strategies, which are crucial for their economic advancement (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Wade 2010; World Bank 1992;  Ghazali and Ali 2019). A balanced approach that combines top-down and bottom-up strategies is recommended, ensuring that policies are adaptable to the needs of entrepreneurs while avoiding narrowly focused market incentives (Armstrong and Wu 2024; Lin 2012; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020).

To minimize adverse impacts, industrial policies should be transparent, accountable, and strategically visionary, ensuring they do not obstruct development pathways for less-industrialized countries (Rodrik 2014; Ferrannini et al. 2021). This involves incorporating mechanisms for policy learning and adaptation to global shifts, such as those prompted by the Covid-19 pandemic, to prevent exacerbating inequalities and to support a balanced global development framework (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Kemp and Never 2017). Moreover, policies should include participatory governance and collective evaluation to mitigate regulatory capture and information asymmetries (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Lin 2012).

Industrial policies should also focus on establishing enabling institutions and providing low-cost public inputs not supplied by the market, such as skills development programs and quality infrastructure, to prevent adverse effects on less-industrialized countries (Fernandes and Reed 2026; World Bank 1993). It is crucial that these policies align with sustainable development goals, promoting ecological sustainability, gender equality, and human rights, while avoiding the appropriation of development pathways vital to less-industrialized countries (OECD 2021; UNIDO 2024; World Bank 2010; Maltais and Suljada 2025).

Finally, fostering international cooperation and policy coherence is essential to avoid beggar-thy-neighbor strategies and ensure that investment policies do not become entangled with national security measures, which could deter foreign investment and harm less-industrialized countries (UNCTAD 2018; World Bank 1993). By adopting a comprehensive approach that integrates economic, social, and environmental objectives, more industrialized countries can ensure their industrial policies support global development without constraining opportunities for less-industrialized countries (World Bank 1991; UNIDO 2024). Additionally, policies should be designed to address market failures as a primary solution and include sunset clauses to ensure that support is phased out appropriately, avoiding premature termination that could undermine long-term benefits (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Lin 2012).

### Technology Transfer and Capacity Building
Oxfam proposes a strategic focus on technology transfer and capacity building to mitigate the detrimental effects of industrial policies from more industrialized countries on less-industrialized nations. Central to this strategy is the promotion of international technology cooperation, which is essential for the rapid dissemination of technological solutions, particularly in the management of global commons and the advancement of clean technologies (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Maltais and Suljada 2025). This involves enhancing mechanisms for funding international research and development, facilitating knowledge sharing, and providing technical assistance (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Industrialized countries are encouraged to bolster the technological capabilities of less-industrialized nations by investing in education, technological training, and infrastructure, thereby improving their technological absorptive capacity (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; World Bank 2010). Aligning educational and vocational training policies with industrial needs is crucial for effective integration into global value chains (UNIDO 2024; Let et al. 2018).

Oxfam advocates for industrial policies that promote technology transfer under favorable conditions, such as flexible intellectual property rights and licensing agreements, enabling less-industrialized countries to adapt and utilize technologies (Chang 2007; Sappa 2026). Public procurement can be a tool to support innovation and capacity building, as demonstrated by successful models in countries like South Korea (Yülek 2018; Amsden 1989). Additionally, governments can mitigate risks associated with scaling up emerging technologies by subsidizing or directly providing technology-related public goods (Chang and Andreoni 2020).

Moreover, Oxfam suggests that industrialized countries design policies to support the development of local innovation hubs and technology incubators, especially in green technologies, to facilitate inclusive and cooperative global energy transitions (Maltais and Suljada 2025). This includes committing to long-term research and development and market deployment to foster leadership in clean technologies (Mazzucato 2013). Industrial policies should not hinder less-industrialized countries from accessing technological advancements; instead, they should promote the international diffusion of suitable technologies by aligning with the comparative advantages of these countries, thus accelerating economic growth and reducing inequality (Lin 2012; World Bank 1991).

Oxfam recommends that industrialized countries recognize the government's role in fostering technological innovations and avoid advocating purely free-market solutions while engaging in covert industrial policy (Wade). Policies should support innovation networks and decentralized approaches to prevent adverse impacts on less-industrialized countries (Wade). Furthermore, industrialized countries should design policies that maximize learning spillovers from foreign investment, potentially through joint ventures or local content and employment requirements (Stiglitz 2018).

To further assist less-industrialized countries, Oxfam suggests exploring technical, financial, and other cooperative mechanisms to establish mutually beneficial partnerships (Maltais and Suljada 2025). Training programs should be developed to enhance technical skills, management practices, and adherence to environmental standards (Maltais and Suljada 2025). Additionally, Oxfam recommends that industrialized countries support the development of local versions of successful institutional frameworks, akin to those in Korea and Taiwan, to bolster industrialization efforts (Rodrick 1993; Amsden 1989).

Finally, Oxfam advises that industrialized countries invest in capacity development by enhancing skills, strengthening organizational tools, and promoting policy learning to improve the formulation and implementation of industrial policies (UNIDO 2024). This includes fostering productive capacities through capital accumulation, technological progress, and structural change, ensuring these efforts are tailored to specific contexts rather than adopting a one-size-fits-all approach (UNCTAD 2006). Additionally, Oxfam suggests providing technical and financial support to enhance the human capital and physical infrastructure of less-industrialized countries, which is vital for their effective participation in production networks and to reap the benefits of globalization (Kuroiwa 2009).

### Environmental Sustainability
Aligning industrial policies in more industrialized countries with global climate objectives necessitates a comprehensive integration of ecological sustainability. Such policies should prioritize sustainable structural transformations that harmonize development with environmental imperatives (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Altenburg and Assmann 2017). This involves the adoption of green industrial policy frameworks that not only accelerate structural transformation and enhance productivity but also minimize market failures and political capture (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). It is crucial for more industrialized countries to phase out environmentally harmful sectors while promoting the growth of environmentally superior industries, ensuring these policies do not obstruct development pathways for less-industrialized countries (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Fernandes and Reed 2026; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020).

Environmental fiscal reforms should incorporate true environmental costs into pricing, eliminate fossil fuel subsidies, and utilize the resulting revenues to support poverty reduction and green infrastructure, thereby indirectly benefiting less-industrialized countries (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Industrial policies should facilitate the adoption of clean technologies and efficient processes in developing countries, leveraging their potential to avoid unsustainable infrastructure investments (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). A mission-oriented approach to industrial strategy, guided by Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), can provide a clear framework for aligning industrial policies with global sustainability objectives (UNIDO 2024; Mazzucato 2013). This includes fostering public-private partnerships, promoting renewable energy, and ensuring regional cooperation to establish sustainable industries (UNIDO 2024).

Furthermore, industrial policies must integrate ecological sustainability, gender equality, and human rights, ensuring they do not restrict the policy space of less-industrialized countries (OECD 2021; Wade 1990; Allwood 2020). This involves employing a diverse array of policy instruments, such as emission pricing and subsidies for low-carbon technology, to promote sustainable practices without disadvantaging less-industrialized countries (OECD 2021; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020). International cooperation is essential for scaling up financing and technology transfer to support green industrialization in less-industrialized countries, ensuring an inclusive global transition (Maltais and Suljada 2025; World Bank 1992).

The restructuring of value chains will have significant implications for inclusion and sustainability, necessitating a societal vision that reconciles sustainability with development (Ferrannini et al. 2021). More industrialized countries should avoid implementing inefficient incentives that lead to environmental degradation, as eliminating these can enhance both environmental quality and economic efficiency (Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Governments must understand the synergies and trade-offs involved in designing green industrial policies to maximize benefits and minimize costs, acknowledging the challenges posed by situation-specific factors (Altenburg and Assmann 2017).

Ultimately, Oxfam can advocate for more industrialized countries to adopt a comprehensive approach to industrial policy that integrates both demand-side and supply-side measures. These policies should support the development of green industries and technological maturity without hindering the development pathways of less-industrialized countries (Mazzucato 2013; UNCTAD 2018; Galitsky et al. 2004). By implementing these strategies, more industrialized countries can design industrial policies that promote ecological sustainability and equitable global development.

### Social Equity and Inclusion
Oxfam proposes that industrialized countries integrate social equity, gender equality, and human rights into their industrial policy frameworks to foster inclusive development. Industrial policies should incorporate conflict management and social insurance mechanisms to mitigate resistance to socially beneficial changes that might disadvantage certain groups (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Wade 2010). Furthermore, fiscal policies reminiscent of Structural Adjustment Programs (SAPs) should be avoided, as they conflict with human rights frameworks. Instead, reforms in International Financial Institutions (IFIs) should align fiscal policies with human rights standards (AWID 2013).

As previously discussed, it is crucial that industrial policies from more industrialized countries do not exacerbate inequalities or hinder development opportunities for less-industrialized nations. Policies should support ecological sustainability, gender equality, and human rights, avoiding the appropriation of development opportunities vital to less-industrialized countries (OECD 2021; Altenburg and Assmann 2017; UNCTAD 2018; Ghazali and Ghulam 2019). Compensation measures for vulnerable groups, particularly women and youth, should be embedded in policy reforms to secure necessary buy-in and minimize harm (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Potter, Gibb and Cedillo 2012).

### Conflict
The discourse surrounding the industrial policies of more industrialized countries reveals significant conflicts regarding their impact on less-industrialized nations. A key perspective emphasizes the necessity of anticipatory conflict management strategies, such as clear policy announcements and performance targets, to mitigate conflicts and accusations of favoritism, thereby reducing harm to less-industrialized countries (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Ferrannini et al. 2021). This approach includes adopting social insurance and temporary protection measures to address resistance to socially beneficial changes (Chang and Andreoni 2020).

Conversely, protectionist policies, particularly those implemented by the United States, are criticized for diminishing global welfare and impeding the energy transition by increasing costs and stifling innovation (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Berasaluce 2024). Trade-distorting measures, which form a substantial part of new industrial policies, adversely affect global trade and economic outcomes (Evenett et al. 2024). The Stockholm Statement underscores that the policies of large, affluent countries have significant externalities on the rest of the world, often unaccounted for (Stiglitz 2018). Furthermore, the proliferation of subsidies without clear policy goals could trigger a global subsidies race, complicating international trade dynamics (Manak 2024).

Historically, it is argued that more industrialized countries should acknowledge their own past use of protectionist policies and allow less-industrialized countries to employ similar strategies for development, rather than imposing free-market policies that may hinder growth (Chang 2007; Rodrik 2011). Reforming international economic governance to prevent the 'kicking away the ladder' approach is crucial (Chang 2007). However, excessive protectionism and rent-seeking behaviors can lead to inefficient resource allocation and hinder development in less-industrialized countries (Lin 2012; Aiginger and Rodrik 2020; Lin and Chang 2009).

From a global perspective, it is argued that national competitiveness concerns should lead to a subsidy war rather than a tariff war, as the former expands the global supply of clean technologies while the latter restricts it (Rodrik 2014). Policymakers are encouraged to adopt a collaborative approach, open to international productive-capacity cooperation, and avoid beggar-thy-neighbor outcomes (UNCTAD 2018; Maltais & Suljada 2025). As discussed in earlier sections, Oxfam can recommend that more industrialized countries avoid excessive localization requirements that sideline emerging markets and developing economies (EMDEs) and prioritize partnerships over protectionism to scale up climate solutions globally (Maltais & Suljada 2025; Stiglitz 2002). Additionally, they should avoid creating large rents through protection and subsidies, as these can become targets of political capture and governance issues in less-industrialized countries (Lin 2012).

Overall, the debate centers on whether industrial policies should prioritize anticipatory conflict management and historical context recognition or focus on avoiding protectionism and promoting global cooperation. These conflicting views underscore the complexity of designing industrial policies that are equitable and beneficial for both more and less-industrialized countries. More industrialized countries should consider the impact of their industrial policies on less-industrialized countries, as illustrated by the case where Chinese access to credit led to bankruptcies of solar companies in the US and Europe (Mazzucato 2013).

Oxfam can make several key recommendations to rich countries to reform transnational institutions, thereby increasing the policy space for less-industrialized countries. Prioritizing green industrial policies is essential, as they address sustainability challenges while fostering industrial growth. Reforms should permit environmentally motivated subsidies and harmonize environmental taxation on an international scale to mitigate competitiveness effects (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Maltais and Suljada 2025). Oxfam can further recommend that affluent nations support the development of Quality Infrastructure (QI) systems and national standards bodies to enhance the industrial capabilities of less-industrialized countries (Fernandes and Reed 2026). ### Trade and Investment Agreement Reforms
Reforming trade and investment agreements is pivotal for expanding the policy space available to less-industrialized countries, thereby enabling them to pursue tailored industrialization strategies. A critical aspect of this reform involves revising World Trade Organization (WTO) rules to permit selective industrial policies and clarifying the application of GATT Article XVIII, which provides exceptions for industrial policy purposes to economies with low living standards (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Chang and Andreoni 2020). Although current WTO provisions, such as special and differential treatment (SDT), offer some flexibility, they fall short of addressing the constraints imposed by trade agreements on performance requirements, technology transfer, and local content rules (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Wade 2010). The existing restrictions on subsidies and performance requirements limit policy space, underscoring the need for reforms that empower less-industrialized countries to protect nascent industries and implement green industrial policies (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Furthermore, the prohibition of export-contingent subsidies, except for the least developed countries, restricts policy options for developing economies (Fernandes and Reed 2026).

Reforms should also prioritize enhancing investment facilitation, transparency, and administrative procedures to bolster industrialization efforts (UNCTAD 2018). Expanding preferential trade agreements, particularly South-South agreements, can create larger duty-free markets and promote technology transfer (Fernandes and Reed 2026). Additionally, addressing non-tariff barriers and harmonizing international standards can alleviate burdens on less-industrialized countries, facilitating their integration into global value chains (OECD 2021; Maltais and Suljada 2025). The current WTO framework often favors industrialized nations, necessitating a shift towards a more development-friendly regime that allows for greater policy autonomy and experimentation with institutional arrangements (Rodrik 2001; Stiglitz 2018).

Oxfam can recommend that affluent nations reform transnational institutions to ensure that trade agreements and policies are designed to reduce barriers and tariffs on exports from less-industrialized countries. Initiatives by the European Union and the United States exemplify how such reforms could significantly enhance policy space for these countries (World Bank 2008). Additionally, investing in infrastructure projects with private participation can alleviate fiscal constraints and improve infrastructure efficiency, supporting industrialization strategies (World Bank 2008). Reforms should ensure that trade agreements do not disproportionately favor industrialized nations, allowing less-industrialized countries to adopt international obligations selectively and balance the benefits of joining international 'clubs' like the WTO with the need for policy flexibility (World Bank 2005; Stiglitz, Lin, Monga 2013).

Moreover, Oxfam can advocate for the removal of trade protection and related distortions in developed countries, which could provide significant economic gains to developing countries, thereby increasing their policy space and enabling them to pursue their own industrialization strategies (World Bank 2005). Rich countries should support the development of international agreements that facilitate the removal of trade barriers and subsidies on climate-smart technologies, thereby enhancing the policy space for less-industrialized countries to engage in clean technology markets and benefit from increased trade volumes (World Bank 2010). These changes are essential for creating a fairer global trade environment that supports sustainable development and poverty reduction in less-industrialized countries. Oxfam can recommend that rich countries support these reforms to transnational institutions, ensuring that less-industrialized countries have comparable policy space to industrialized nations (Bivens 2023; Juhász, Lane, Rodrik 2023). Mutual recognition agreements and harmonization of standards can play a pivotal role in reducing burdens on emerging markets and developing economies (EMDEs) to integrate into green trade and value chains (Maltais and Suljada 2025).

### Technology Transfer and Innovation
To enhance technology transfer and innovation in less-industrialized countries, Oxfam should advocate for significant reforms in transnational institutions, particularly concerning intellectual property rights (IPR) and local entrepreneurship support. Reforming the TRIPS agreement to allow more flexible IPR regimes is essential, as it would enable these countries to access and adapt new technologies at a lower cost (Chang 2007; Watkins and Fowler 2002; Wade 2003). This includes advocating for exceptions in IPR protection specifically for climate-smart technologies to facilitate their transfer and adaptation (World Bank 2010; Akyüz 2008).

Oxfam should also promote the creation of international co-innovation and green technology diffusion funds, supported by climate finance instruments and multilateral development banks, to aid technology transfer and green industrial development (Maltais and Suljada 2025). Encouraging joint ventures and strategic partnerships can lead to knowledge spillovers and attract foreign direct investment, as evidenced by China's success in utilizing such mechanisms (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Khorsheed 2015). Moreover, existing WTO regulations on joint ventures and technology transfer can still be leveraged to expand policy space (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Nemlioglu 2019).

Reforms should also aim to enable less-industrialized countries to adopt cleaner technologies and efficient processes developed in more advanced economies. This involves facilitating technology transfers through licensing agreements and ensuring that global environmental agreements incorporate climate finance provisions to support these initiatives (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; World Bank 1992). Additionally, Oxfam can recommend that affluent nations support the expansion of higher education and skilled labor to attract multinational R&D activities, thereby enhancing local innovation capacities (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Amsden 1989).

As previously discussed, Oxfam should advocate for the removal of trade barriers and subsidies on climate-smart technologies, which would facilitate technology transfer and innovation. It is crucial that international treaties address IPR concerns to allow for the exploitation of patented inventions for climate mitigation and adaptation (World Bank 2010; Stiglitz 2002). Furthermore, reforms should tackle prohibitions in international investment agreements that restrict performance requirements, such as technology transfer, to increase policy space for less-industrialized countries (Altenburg and Assmann 2017; Nguyen 2019).

To further bolster innovation, Oxfam can recommend that wealthy nations advance reforms recognizing the importance of state intervention in technology, innovation, and entrepreneurship, as highlighted by Mazzucato's work. This includes ensuring that market conditions foster innovation and that sufficient investment is available for innovators (Mazzucato 2013; Lin 2012). Additionally, developing precise indicators of upgrading relevant to supplier firms and countries can guide these efforts (Gereffi 2014).

Finally, Oxfam should advocate for reforms that allow less-industrialized countries to maintain flexible IPR systems, enabling them to access new technologies and compete more effectively in global markets, thus expanding their policy space (UNCTAD 2014; Sappa 2026). By promoting these reforms, Oxfam can help increase the policy space available to less-industrialized countries, enabling them to pursue industrialization strategies aligned with their developmental goals.

### Financial System and Debt Reform
To enhance the fiscal autonomy and policy space of less-industrialized countries, Oxfam should advocate for comprehensive reforms in the global financial system and debt structures. A critical aspect of these reforms involves addressing the pervasive issue of financialization, which constrains the economic policy options available to developing nations (Chang and Andreoni 2020). This necessitates a coordinated international effort to reform transnational institutions, such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank, to permit more flexible economic policies, including the strategic use of multiple exchange and interest rates (Amsden 1989; Wade 1990). Additionally, the establishment of a global reserve system could enhance the purchasing power of developing countries without diminishing that of developed nations (Stiglitz 2018).

Expanding debt relief initiatives, such as the Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative, is crucial for alleviating the financial burdens on heavily indebted poor countries, thereby enabling them to prioritize national development goals (World Bank 2007; AWID 2013). Furthermore, reforms should legitimize the use of capital controls as a policy tool to manage financial inflows and outflows, thus bolstering macroeconomic stability (UNCTAD 2014; Stiglitz 2002; Wade 2003).

Oxfam should also recommend that affluent nations support the development of financial mechanisms that provide long-term, affordable financing for development, such as development banks and publicly funded venture funds (Rodrik 2004; Mazzucato 2013). These mechanisms should be complemented by increased official development assistance, aiming for the United Nations target of 0.7% of Gross National Product (GNP), to support infrastructure development and trade facilitation in less-industrialized countries (World Bank 1980; World Bank 2008). Highlighting cooperation between the European Union and partner countries on the development of national carbon markets can further increase policy space for these nations (Maltais and Suljada 2025).

Moreover, reforms should focus on enhancing transparency and governance in international financial systems to prevent illicit financial flows and tax avoidance, which deplete resources from developing countries (UNCTAD 2014; Fernandes and Reed 2026). Integrating sustainability criteria into monetary policy frameworks can also help mitigate environmental impacts and promote sustainable growth (Turan and Sivrikaya 2025; Altenburg and Assmann 2017).

Finally, Oxfam should advocate for the removal of trade barriers, particularly in agriculture, and promote good governance in international financial and trading systems to enable developing countries to effectively pursue their industrialization strategies (World Bank 2010; Wade 2010). These comprehensive reforms are essential for expanding the policy space available to less-industrialized countries, allowing them to achieve sustainable development and economic growth. Strategies must also include optimizing the productive utilization of remittances from migrant workers to ensure sustained long-term economic growth (Adewale and Bank-Ola 2025).

### Human Rights and Trade Policy
The integration of human rights frameworks into trade and investment policies is essential to prevent the erosion of human rights and the exacerbation of inequalities. Recent international investment agreements (IIAs) have begun to incorporate corporate social responsibility (CSR) standards, including labor, environmental, and human rights standards, although the specificity and enforceability of these references vary (UNCTAD 2018). Some IIAs align with global standards such as the Sustainable Development Goals and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UNCTAD 2018). However, the ability of private parties to bring direct international claims under these agreements can affect the policy space available to less-industrialized countries (UNCTAD 2018). To advance reform, Oxfam can recommend that affluent nations undertake mandatory human rights impact assessments (HRIAs) for trade and investment agreements. These assessments can democratize debate and ensure that human rights and gender equality goals are addressed, preventing the erosion of rights and promoting inclusive development (Potter, Gibb, Cedillo 2012; CWGL 2011).

Reforming the governance of International Financial Institutions (IFIs) to align fiscal policies with human rights standards can increase policy space for less-industrialized countries (AWID 2013). Additionally, strengthening the role of human rights frameworks in fiscal policy can ensure transparency, accountability, and participatory mechanisms in policy design (AWID 2013). Trade policies should support inclusion by tailoring standards to the contexts of emerging markets and developing economies (EMDEs), promoting mutual recognition of standards, and providing technical assistance to reduce regulatory hurdles (Maltais and Suljada 2025). Furthermore, Oxfam can advocate for reforms that democratize the WTO, enhance the ILO's capacity to enforce labor standards, and establish new intellectual-property rules to protect agricultural systems in less-industrialized countries (Watkins and Fowler 2002). Transnational institutions should be reformed to strengthen dispute settlement and implementation mechanisms for human rights and environmental concerns, which are currently weaker than those in the investment treaty framework (UNCTAD 2014).

The integration of human rights into trade agreements can benefit more people and produce sustainable growth (Potter, Gibb, Cedillo 2012). Trade policies and standards should enable broad participation, ensuring that green industrial policies align with inclusive development and climate goals (Maltais and Suljada 2025). Labor standards should be a concern of the international community, not just individual governments (Aidt and Tzannatos 2002). There is a need to increase coherence between trade and human rights, as highlighted by the Director General of the WTO in 2010 (Potter, Gibb, Cedillo 2012). International donor states should ensure that the policies and decisions of multilateral organizations, including the WTO, IMF, and World Bank, conform to the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR) (Potter, Gibb, Cedillo 2012).

Oxfam can recommend that rich countries align trade and investment policies with human rights laws, particularly those supporting the right to food and women's rights, while ensuring these policies empower local communities and support sustainable growth (CWGL 2011). The establishment of a legally binding international protocol to govern the trade of natural resources from conflict areas can promote transparency and human-rights protection, expanding policy space for less-industrialized countries (Watkins and Fowler 2002). By aligning trade and investment policies with human rights laws, rich countries can ensure that these policies empower local communities and support sustainable growth (CWGL 2011).

Finally, Oxfam can suggest that rich countries push for reforms that enhance the transparency, accountability, and coherence of global institutions, ensuring that these institutions respect human rights and empower local communities, thereby increasing the policy space available to less-industrialized countries (WCSDG 2004). Good governance at all levels of society, in terms of the rule of law, democracy, human rights, and social equity, is essential for a fair and productive process of globalization (WCSDG 2004). It would be legitimate to apply more stringent rules to authoritarian regimes in certain instances, such as imposing restrictions on a non-democratic country's trade when that trade causes problems in an importing country (Rodrik 2011).

### Multilateral Governance and Inclusivity
To foster equitable governance within global institutions and expand the policy space for less-industrialized countries, Oxfam should advocate for comprehensive reforms in multilateral governance structures. These reforms must prioritize inclusivity and representation, ensuring that the voices and developmental needs of less-industrialized countries are adequately addressed (Stiglitz 2002; WCSDG 2004; Gereffi 2014). This involves democratizing institutions such as the World Trade Organization (WTO) and the International Monetary Fund (IMF), where current governance frameworks disproportionately favor industrialized nations (Chang 2007; Watkins and Fowler 2002).

Reforms should enhance transparency and accountability within transnational institutions, aligning international rules with the developmental objectives of less-industrialized countries (Rodrik 2014; World Bank 2006). Establishing procedural safeguards that ensure broad representation and scientific input in regulatory processes can protect the policy space of these countries (Rodrik 2011; Ferrannini et al. 2021). Oxfam can propose that affluent nations support the development of a new quota formula in international financial institutions, giving greater weight to GDP measured in purchasing power parity terms, and increase basic votes to restore their original share of total voting power (World Bank 2006).

Furthermore, Oxfam can recommend the creation of a balanced multilateral framework for Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) that reduces incentive competition and ensures equitable benefits for less-industrialized countries (WCSDG 2004; UNCTAD 2014). Promoting regional integration and cooperation can empower these countries to better manage global economic forces and enhance their negotiating power (UNCTAD 2014; Gereffi 2014). Reforms should also consider the historical context and current challenges of labor markets in regions like the Middle East and North Africa (MENA), where state intervention and social contracts have significantly influenced economic policies and labor relations (World Bank 2004).

Addressing the asymmetry in governance, where investment protection is prioritized over state rights, is crucial. Strengthening dispute settlement mechanisms for human rights and environmental concerns can help rectify this imbalance (UNCTAD 2014; Altenburg and Assmann 2017). Reforms should encourage the selective adoption of international obligations, allowing less-industrialized countries to balance the benefits of global integration with the need for policy flexibility (World Bank 2005; Wade 2010). Rich countries should be advised to support governance reforms in less-industrialized countries, focusing on enhancing accountability and inclusiveness, which are essential for managing economic transitions and integrating into the global economy (World Bank 2004).

Oxfam should advocate for reforms that enhance the capacity of less-industrialized countries to pursue their own industrialization strategies by establishing robust institutions, such as high-quality civil services, to monitor performance and insulate policies from political interference (World Bank 1993; Nguyen 2019). This includes supporting the development of participatory governance mechanisms and multi-stakeholder initiatives to address collective action problems (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Gereffi 2014). Reforms should also focus on building greater human capacities, redistributing access to land or infrastructure services, and promoting fairness in markets (World Bank 2006).

Finally, Oxfam can recommend that rich countries advance reforms in transnational institutions to integrate development concerns into climate regimes, ensuring that climate actions do not impose undue constraints on the growth of developing countries (World Bank 2010; Maltais and Suljada 2025). These reforms collectively aim to create a more inclusive and equitable global governance system that supports sustainable development and poverty reduction in less-industrialized countries. Additionally, Oxfam can suggest that rich countries reform transnational institutions to ensure that trade agreements consistently advocate for sustainable development and genuine market liberalization, addressing root causes of displacement and migration, thereby increasing policy space for less-industrialized countries (Adewale & Bank-Ola 2025).
