## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is driven by strategic government intervention, historical and geopolitical influences, technological advancements, environmental sustainability, and the need to address market failures. Governments are increasingly collaborating with the private sector to identify future societal needs and global challenges, a trend accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic. This collaboration underscores the strategic importance of sectors like health and information and communication technology (ICT). National security concerns and the need for structural transformation in response to globalization, climate change, and demographic shifts further motivate the resurgence of industrial policy.

Geopolitical shifts, such as the rise of China and the success of East Asian economies, have prompted countries like the United States and those in Europe to adopt industrial policies aimed at boosting domestic production and ensuring national security. The historical success of export-led growth models in East Asia highlights the strategic importance of industrial policy. However, the resurgence of industrial policy is not without debate, as it often revisits old issues with new terminologies, potentially leading to diminishing returns.

Technological advancements associated with the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) necessitate government intervention to foster innovation and maintain competitive positioning in the global market. The shift towards clean energy and green technologies is a pivotal aspect of the current industrial policy landscape, driven by the urgency of achieving a green transition and addressing climate change. Countries like China are leveraging state-led initiatives to dominate sectors such as electric vehicles, demonstrating the strategic use of industrial policy to foster technological leadership.

The resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by the need to address market failures and promote economic development through structural transformation. Developing countries view industrial policy as a critical tool to escape the 'middle-income trap' and stimulate economic upgrading. In advanced economies, the resurgence is partly driven by the need to counteract deindustrialization and maintain competitiveness in the face of global economic shifts.

Less-industrialized countries face significant challenges in realizing effective industrial policy due to constraints related to economies of scale, technological capabilities, regulatory environments, and international trade agreements. These countries often struggle with limited access to advanced technologies, inefficient regulatory environments, and constraints imposed by international trade agreements. Financial constraints, environmental challenges, and conflicts over the role of the state further complicate the implementation of effective industrial policies.

To support less-industrialized countries, Oxfam recommends that more industrialized countries preserve policy space, reform transnational institutions, avoid harmful industrial policies, and support sustainable development. This includes advocating for flexibility in international agreements, reforming investment protection and dispute resolution mechanisms, and promoting inclusive industrial policies. By aligning industrial policies with global development goals, industrialized countries can minimize harm to less-industrialized countries and foster a more equitable global economic environment.

## What drivers account for the resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrial and industrializing countries?
## Question Overview
The resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is driven by several key factors. Strategic government intervention plays a crucial role, as nations seek to foster economic growth, technological advancement, and resilience in response to challenges such as the COVID-19 pandemic and geopolitical tensions. Historical and geopolitical influences, including the success of East Asian economies and the rise of China, have prompted a reevaluation of industrial strategies. Technological advancements, particularly those associated with the Fourth Industrial Revolution, necessitate policies that support innovation and digital transformation. Environmental sustainability and the transition to green technologies are also significant drivers, as countries aim to address climate change and promote sustainable growth. Additionally, the need to correct market failures and adapt to global economic shifts, including the restructuring of global value chains, further underscores the importance of industrial policy in contemporary economic development.

### Strategic Government Intervention
The most prominent narrative identified across the corpus is the pivotal role of strategic government intervention in the resurgence of industrial policy within both highly industrialized and industrializing nations. This resurgence is largely attributed to the recognition of industrial policy's potential to stimulate economic growth, drive technological advancement, and enhance sectoral resilience, particularly in the aftermath of the 2008 financial crisis. Leading economies such as the USA and Germany have exemplified this trend through proactive measures, as documented by Andreoni (2016) and Mazzucato (2013) in Chang and Andreoni (2020). Theoretical advancements have further reinvigorated the discourse on industrial policy, underscoring its capacity to mitigate uncertainty for firms and guide innovation (Chang & Andreoni, 2020). Historical precedents, such as the US government's investment in the development of foundational technologies like computers and the internet via public R&D funding, illustrate the enduring impact of such interventions (Berger, 2013; Block & Keller, 2011; Mazzucato, 2013).

Moreover, governments are increasingly forging strategic partnerships with the private sector to anticipate future societal needs and global challenges, a trend that has catalyzed the resurgence of industrial policy (Chang & Andreoni, 2020). The COVID-19 pandemic has further accelerated this movement by underscoring the necessity of government intervention in production dynamics and highlighting the strategic importance of sectors such as health and ICT (Ferrannini et al., 2021). The pandemic has also emphasized the critical role of public action in recovery strategies, as noted by Mazzuccato (2020), the UN (2020), the IMF (2020a), the OECD (2020a), and the WEF (2020a). It has exacerbated pre-existing economic and social issues, such as inequality and multidimensional poverty, which are key drivers for the renewed focus on industrial policy (Anand et al., 2020; Fleurbaey, 2020; Mazzuccato, 2020; Sen, 2020; WEF, 2020a).

National security concerns have emerged as a significant driver, with governments striving to enhance domestic production and self-sufficiency in strategic sectors to shield against geopolitical shocks (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). The increasing incidence of natural disasters and emerging infectious diseases has further motivated the adoption of industrial policy as a safeguard against such risks (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). Additionally, the need for structural transformation in response to technological advancements, globalization, climate change, and shifting demographics has been a compelling factor (Stiglitz, 2018).

Technological progress, the rise of protectionism, economic nationalism, and new climate-related regulations have constrained traditional trade-led growth, necessitating strategic public investments across various sectors (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). Such interventions are crucial for addressing contemporary challenges, including the green transition, supply chain resilience, and geopolitical competition, particularly with China (Juhász, Lane, & Rodrik, 2023). The resurgence of interest in industrial policy has reestablished its prominence in academic and policy-making debates worldwide, as highlighted by Aiginger & Rodrik (2020), Bailey et al. (2015), Chang & Andreoni (2020), Cimoli et al. (2009), and Oqubay et al. (2020). However, this debate risks entering a phase of diminishing returns, often revisiting old issues with new terminologies (Andreoni & Chang, 2019).

Furthermore, the resurgence of industrial policy is driven by the imperative for governments to actively facilitate structural change and upgrade infrastructure to support industrial growth, while addressing market failures such as information spillovers and coordination deficits (Lin, 2012). The recognition that successful economies have historically achieved structural change through shifts in endowment structures and comparative advantages suggests a proactive role for the state in facilitating such transformations (Lin, 2012). In advanced countries, the need for technological innovation and industrial upgrading necessitates substantial and risky investments in research and development, further driving the resurgence of industrial policy (Lin, 2012).

### Historical and Geopolitical Influences
A primary pillar of the analysis concerns the historical and geopolitical influences that have significantly shaped the resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries. As previously noted, the success of East Asian economies, particularly the Asian Tigers and China, has underscored the efficacy of state-led industrial strategies, prompting a global reconsideration of industrial policy as a viable tool for economic development (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Juhász, Réka; Lane, Nathan; Rodrik, Dani 2023). The ascent of China as a formidable economic power has heightened geopolitical tensions, compelling nations such as the United States and European countries to adopt industrial policies aimed at bolstering domestic production and safeguarding national security (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan 2026; Bofinger 2019, Altmaier 2019). This geopolitical rivalry is further intensified by the imperative to counterbalance China's influence and rectify global economic distortions (Inu Manak 2024).

Historically, industrial policy has been integral to economic success in regions like East Asia, where export-led growth models have been pivotal (Rodrik 2006; Stiglitz and Yusuf 2001). The historical linkage between economic growth and manufacturing expansion highlights the strategic significance of industrial policy (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017). Additionally, the disintegration of the Bretton Woods system, oil shocks, and the rise of finance-driven globalization have historically necessitated a reevaluation of industrial strategies (Bruno and Sachs, 1985; United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014).

Geopolitical transformations, such as the conclusion of the Cold War and the collapse of command-and-control economies, have also reshaped the global economic landscape, prompting a renewed focus on industrial policy (The World Bank 1997). The success of rapidly growing economies in East and South-East Asia has exerted pressure on developed countries to respond to intensified competition in trade, investment, and technology (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). These historical and geopolitical influences collectively drive the resurgence of industrial policy as nations strive to secure economic growth, enhance competitiveness, and address national security concerns in a rapidly evolving global environment.

The resurgence is further propelled by strategic competition, job creation, climate change mitigation, and shifts in economic strategies, such as those observed under the Biden administration in the U.S., marking a departure from traditional skepticism towards government intervention (Inu Manak 2024). The historical misinterpretation that protectionist policies were key to the success of wealthier countries, despite evidence that open economies with competitive domestic markets were more successful, also plays a role (Douglas Irwin 2023). Moreover, the resurgence is influenced by the need for supply chain resilience, geopolitical concerns, climate change, national security, and domestic competitiveness (March 2025 survey, World Bank).

The resurgence of industrial policy is partly driven by the historical success of countries like Korea, which have demonstrated the importance of education and foreign technical assistance in driving industrialization and economic development (Amsden 1989). The historical context of financial systems, including the role of universal banking and government intervention in credit allocation, has influenced the current resurgence as countries aim to balance regulation with market efficiency (World Bank Group 1989). Additionally, the resurgence is driven by the need to address rapid demographic transitions and the associated economic challenges, as seen in the East Asian economies (World Bank 1993). The resurgence is also motivated by the need to address the limitations and negative impacts of free trade and foreign direct investment, as evidenced by the experiences of countries like Mexico (Ha-Joon Chang 2007).

### Technological Advancements and Innovation
The material most densely clusters around the pivotal role of technological advancements and innovation in driving the resurgence of industrial policy across both highly industrialized and industrializing nations. This resurgence is intricately linked to the transformative impacts of the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR), characterized by the integration of advanced manufacturing technologies, digital transformation, and the proliferation of information and communication technologies (ICTs). These developments necessitate strategic government intervention to foster innovation and maintain competitive positioning in the global market, as highlighted by the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (2018) and UNCTAD (2017e). Consistent with historical precedents, the US government's role in financing foundational technologies such as the internet and semiconductors underscores the critical importance of state intervention in technological development (Berger, 2013; Mazzucato, 2013).

Technological change has accelerated the need for industrial policies that support digitalization and automation, evidenced by the increased adoption of AI, IoT, and cloud computing, which are transforming business operations and enhancing efficiencies (Rasiah 2024). The COVID-19 pandemic further underscored the necessity of advanced technologies for agile production systems, emphasizing the importance of a digital-first mindset (Ferrannini et al. 2021). Moreover, the shift towards clean energy and green technologies is a pivotal aspect of the current industrial policy landscape, driven by the urgency of achieving a green transition and addressing climate change (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2024). Countries like China exemplify the strategic use of industrial policy to foster technological leadership, particularly in sectors such as electric vehicles (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

As noted in previous sections, the resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by structural changes such as technological advances, the rise of protectionism, economic nationalism, and new policy responses to climate change, which have constrained traditional trade-led growth (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). Additionally, the need for technological diffusion and industrial upgrading, which involves making knowledge widely available and fostering constant learning, is widely accepted as a new rationale for industrial policy (Stiglitz, Lin, Monga, & Patel, 2013).

Labour-saving technological progress and globalization are significant factors in understanding current trends in industrial policy, as they substantially reduce demand for routine labor activities in manufacturing and services (Rodrik 2014; Brynjolfsson & McAfee 2014; Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017). The paradigm shift to electric driving is likely to cause a major industry shake-up, facilitating competition for newcomers (Perez 1988 in Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

The role of the state as a catalyst for innovation is further emphasized by the need for mission-oriented funding and procurement, which has historically driven significant technological advancements, as seen in the development of the internet and green technologies (Mazzucato). This proactive state involvement is crucial for overcoming market failures and ensuring that technological advancements translate into broad economic benefits (Block 2008; Fuchs 2010). The resurgence of industrial policy is intricately linked to the strategic deployment of technological advancements to drive economic growth and competitiveness, with countries like Korea and the US exemplifying the use of government initiatives and public-private collaborations to advance high-tech industries (Amsden 1989; Irwin & Klenow 1996).

### Environmental Sustainability and Green Technologies
At the core of the discussion lies the imperative to address climate change and transition to green economies, which significantly drives the resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries. This shift is characterized by the development and deployment of green technologies, renewable energy, and sustainable growth models, with governments placing environmental sustainability at the forefront of industrial policy (Rodrik, 2014; Mathews, 2020; Ferrannini et al., 2021). As noted in earlier sections, the urgency to act swiftly due to approaching global thresholds and the need for quantum leaps in resource efficiency provides a compelling rationale for green industrial policy (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C., 2017). International agreements such as the Paris Agreement and the Montreal Protocol further commit governments to decarbonize their economies, influencing national policy agendas (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C., 2017).

Government interventions, including subsidies and incentives, are pivotal in promoting low-pollution technologies and enhancing global competitiveness (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan, 2026). Countries like China and Brazil have leveraged state investment banks to fund clean technology and renewable energy, demonstrating the critical role of public finance in supporting green industrial growth (Mazzucato). The strategic focus on renewable energy technologies is seen as a pathway to inclusive and green growth, fostering job creation and private sector development (UNEP 2011; World Bank 2012; IRENA 2013).

The political economy of climate policy has also shifted, with green industrial policies emerging as politically feasible alternatives to carbon pricing, helping to shift political equilibria in favor of climate action (Meckling and Karplus, 2023). The recognition of ecological system boundaries as a game-changer for economic development has prompted countries to adopt policies that support sustainable growth and address environmental challenges (Altenburg et al., 2017). This trend is further supported by the decreasing cost of renewable energy generation and the increasing demand for environmentally conscious products, driven by both consumers and institutional investors (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C., 2017).

### Market Failures and Economic Development
A recurring point of emphasis across the record is the critical role of addressing market failures in the resurgence of industrial policy, particularly as a mechanism for promoting economic development through structural transformation. As previously discussed, this resurgence is notably pronounced in developing countries that aim to transcend the limitations of market-driven approaches, which have historically struggled to deliver sustainable growth and diversification (Chang and Andreoni 2020; Rodrik 2004). Market failures, including coordination failures, information asymmetries, and the inability of markets to internalize environmental costs, necessitate strategic government intervention to foster economic diversification and structural change (Altenburg and Lütkenhorst 2015; Rodrik 2004).

Developing regions, notably Africa and Latin America, perceive industrial policy as a vital instrument to escape the 'middle-income trap' and stimulate economic upgrading post-commodity boom (Felipe 2015; Noman and Stiglitz 2016). Middle-income countries in Asia and parts of Latin America similarly view industrial policy as a pathway to overcome this trap (Chang, Ha-Joon; Andreoni, Antonio 2020). Additionally, oil-dependent economies in the Gulf are pivoting towards industrial policy to diversify their economic bases away from oil reliance (Cherif and Hasanov 2014; Chang, Ha-Joon; Andreoni, Antonio 2020). African economies are also engaging in discussions around industrial policy as a strategy to alleviate poverty (Chang, Ha-Joon; Andreoni, Antonio 2020). The recognition of industrialization as a cornerstone for economic transformation has led to a renewed focus on proactive industrial policies to drive structural changes (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

In advanced economies, the resurgence is partly motivated by the need to counteract deindustrialization and maintain competitiveness amid global economic shifts. The European Commission and other bodies have underscored the significance of industry for job creation and economic growth, advocating for an 'industrial renaissance' (EC Communication 2014). The global financial crisis and subsequent economic challenges have further exposed the limitations of market-fundamentalist approaches, prompting a shift towards state intervention to achieve national and global policy objectives such as financial stability and environmental protection (Stiglitz et al. 2013). This resurgence is also driven by the historical correlation between economic growth and the expansion of the manufacturing sector, particularly through export-led growth in manufactures (Rodrik 2006; Stiglitz and Yusuf 2001; Commission on Growth and Development 2008; Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

The resurgence of industrial policy reflects a broader recognition of the necessity for strategic government intervention to correct market failures, support technological innovation, and promote sustainable economic development. This approach is deemed essential for achieving long-term growth and addressing the socio-economic challenges posed by globalization and technological change (Fernandes and Reed 2026; Stiglitz 2018). There is an increasing understanding that industrial policy is not exclusive to advanced economies and is selectively employed in developing economies to steer economic activity and protect certain industries (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan 2026). The resurgence is also driven by the imperative to address global challenges such as climate change, conflicts, poverty, and socioeconomic disparities, which require coordinated public interventions (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2024). Furthermore, the resurgence is influenced by the need to balance trade-offs between labor-absorbing and high-wage activities, deciding when to subsidize labor rather than capital, and assisting workers displaced by trade or technological change (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan 2026).

### Global Economic Shifts and Value Chains
Fundamental to this body of work is the recognition that the resurgence of industrial policy in both highly industrialized and industrializing countries is intricately linked to the restructuring of global value chains (GVCs) and broader global economic shifts. As previously discussed, the rise of newly industrializing economies, particularly the BRICs, has catalyzed a transition from import-substituting industrialization (ISI) to export-oriented industrialization (EOI). This shift has prompted transnational companies to outsource to lower-cost locations, necessitating a reevaluation of industrial policies to focus on global supply chain linkages and specialization rather than building vertically integrated domestic industries (Gereffi, 2014; Gereffi & Sturgeon, 2014).

The organization of industries into GVCs has reinvigorated industrial policy debates, as countries seek to strategically position themselves within these chains to enhance competitiveness and adapt to globalization processes (Gereffi, 2014). Emerging economies like China and Brazil are actively seeking to move to the forefront of GVCs, encouraging global suppliers to establish facilities within their borders (Gereffi & Sturgeon, 2014). This strategic positioning is further driven by technological advances, cost-reduction opportunities from offshoring, and increased openness to trade and investments (Gereffi, 2014, 2018; Milberg and Winkler, 2013).

Moreover, globalization creates new opportunities for industrial development by facilitating access to previously inaccessible technologies and markets (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). However, it also introduces competitive pressures, as seen with China's ability to outcompete manufacturers across a wide range of products suitable for low-income economies, partly due to an undervalued exchange rate (Wade, 2010). This has put manufacturing sectors in the developing world under intense competitive pressure.

The increasing popularity of the GVC approach is linked to its ability to connect economic growth with poverty reduction and transmit global competition pressures to domestic markets, prompting a resurgence in industrial policy (Gereffi, 2014). The necessity for national and subnational industrial systems to address globalization processes and disruptive technological changes further drives this resurgence (Mehrotra & Biggeri, 2007; Pietrobelli & Rabellotti, 2011).

Additionally, the restructuring of value chains has crucial implications for inclusion and sustainability concerns, as countries navigate the challenges and opportunities presented by these shifts (Oldekop, 2020). The strategic choice of countries like Brazil to attract investments from global suppliers for domestic production and export underscores the importance of serving multiple customers and engaging in global sourcing (Gereffi & Sturgeon, 2014). These dynamics highlight the critical role of industrial policy in adapting to and capitalizing on global economic shifts and value chain restructuring.

The resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by the need for diversification of production, as seen in the 'China plus one,' 'onshoring,' and 'reshoring' strategies (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). These strategies are influenced by geopolitical concerns and the need for supply chain resilience. Furthermore, the promotion of low-pollution technologies through grants and tax incentives enhances global competitiveness (Rosenow & Mealy, 2024; Fernandes & Reed, 2026).

Least Developed Countries have identified productive capacity-building as a high priority, including the importance of their industrialization and integration in global value chains (UN OHRLLS, 2013). However, recent policy shifts, including rising protectionism and economic nationalism, may limit developing economies’ access to large export markets in higher-income economies (Fernandes & Reed, 2026).

The resurgence of industrial policy is also driven by strategic competitiveness, climate change, supply chain resilience, and geopolitical concerns, with advanced economies being the most frequent users (Evenett et al., 2024). The need to address the fragility of supply chains, as demonstrated by the disruptions during the pandemic, has highlighted the risks of excessively fragile supply chains as an externality not fully accounted for by market participants (Bivens, 2023). The growth of EPAs in recent decades has been partly driven by governments’ interest in promoting exports while remaining consistent with WTO rules.

Overall, the resurgence of industrial policy is a complex phenomenon influenced by a multitude of factors, including globalization, technological advancements, geopolitical tensions, and the need for sustainable development. These drivers reflect the ongoing efforts of countries to strategically position themselves in a rapidly changing global economic landscape.

### Conflicts
The analysis highlights conflicting accounts concerning the resurgence of industrial policy, revealing a complex interplay between state-led intervention and market-driven approaches, as well as between protectionism and free trade. As previously noted, the global financial crisis and subsequent economic challenges have eroded confidence in market mechanisms, prompting a shift towards state intervention (Wade, 2010; Fernandes & Reed, 2026). This shift is further necessitated by structural changes such as technological advances, protectionism, and climate change, which have constrained traditional trade-led growth (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). The crisis has also sparked debates over the efficacy of market mechanisms in directing investments towards the 'right' sectors and restoring economic growth (Warwick, 2013).

In contrast, another perspective emphasizes the political economy of industrial policy, where political motivations and constraints significantly shape policy choices. This includes the influence of political equilibria, the desire to maintain power, and the emergence of green industrial policies as alternatives to carbon pricing (Acemoglu & Robinson, 2013; Juhász et al., 2024). The political allure of protectionist measures, despite their inherent risks, also contributes to the resurgence of industrial policy (Das, Prasun Kumar). This resurgence is partly driven by shifts in the political landscape, where those in power advocate for policy choices or broader reforms (Juhász et al., 2024), and by concerns over a potential return to Trump-era policies, leading to intensified industrial policy initiatives (Inu Manak 2024).

Further complicating the discourse are the geopolitical and international trade dynamics that have fueled protectionist sentiments. Heightened competition from developing countries, economic instability, and demands for 'fair trade' rather than free trade have led to protectionist policies in industrialized nations (World Bank Group, 1987). This trend is exacerbated by the perception that protectionist policies were historically pivotal to the success of wealthier countries, despite evidence suggesting otherwise (Douglas Irwin, 2023). The resurgence is also driven by a confluence of domestic and international factors, including currency crises, oil crises, debt crises, global recession, and high unemployment, which have significantly increased demands for protection (World Bank Group, 1987).

Finally, the resurgence is influenced by ideological shifts, where the debate over industrial policy has become less ideologically charged and more pragmatic, reflecting a retreat from the trade fundamentalism of the 1990s (Rodrik, 1997; Dejana M. Gajinov, 2023). This pragmatic approach acknowledges the necessity of balancing market forces with strategic government roles, allowing for a more nuanced economic policy (Rodrik, 2004). The resurgence is driven by the need to balance trade-offs between labor-absorbing and high-wage activities, deciding when to subsidize labor rather than capital, and assisting workers displaced by trade or technological change (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). Additionally, there is a growing recognition that liberalizing markets, attracting FDI, and promoting good governance are not sufficient conditions for long-term economic growth and development in low-income countries (Wade, 2010). These conflicting perspectives underscore the intricate interplay of economic, political, and ideological factors driving the resurgence of industrial policy.

## How have the definitions and approaches to industrial policy shifted from the post-World War II period to present day? And, more specifically, how have these shifts considered (if at all) inclusive and sustainable growth, and respect of human rights and gender equality?
## Question Overview
The evolution of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day is characterized by a transition from state-led to market-oriented approaches, with a significant shift towards integrating inclusive and sustainable growth, human rights, and gender equality. Initially dominated by import substitution industrialization, policies evolved towards export-oriented strategies, particularly in East Asia, emphasizing global market integration and technological advancement. Modern industrial policy now incorporates strategic government intervention, focusing on innovation and market creation, while addressing market failures and fostering sustainable development. Globalization has imposed constraints through trade agreements and global value chains, necessitating policies that balance international competitiveness with domestic policy autonomy. Green industrial policy has emerged as a transformative approach, integrating environmental objectives with economic goals to foster sustainable development. The discourse on industrial policy is marked by conflicts over ideological framing, environmental integration, and the balance between efficiency and growth, reflecting a complex evolution in policy definitions and approaches.

### State-Led to Market-Oriented Transition
The analysis suggests that a central driver of the transition from state-led to market-oriented industrial policies is the evolving global economic landscape from the post-World War II era to the present day. Initially, many nations embraced import substitution industrialization (ISI) strategies, characterized by high tariffs and significant government intervention aimed at nurturing nascent industries. This approach was particularly prevalent among newly independent countries striving to dismantle colonial economic legacies (World Bank Group 1987). However, by the 1980s, the inherent inefficiencies and rent-seeking behaviors associated with ISI became evident, catalyzing a shift towards export-oriented industrialization (EOI) (Gereffi 2014). This transition also mirrored a broader shift from producer-driven to buyer-driven commodity chains, indicative of changing global production dynamics (Gereffi 2014).

The EOI model, as exemplified by the economic strategies of East Asian countries such as South Korea and Taiwan, prioritized integration into global markets through export promotion and the reduction of trade barriers (Wade 2010). These nations adeptly combined state intervention with market mechanisms, employing strategic subsidies and performance-based incentives to cultivate competitive industries (Wade 2010; World Bank 1993). This approach marked a departure from earlier protectionist policies, underscoring the necessity of aligning industrial policy with comparative advantage and global market demands (Lin 2012). East Asian governments implemented 'reciprocal control mechanisms,' requiring firms to meet performance targets in exchange for special favors, such as export quotas or local content requirements (Amsden 2001 in Wade 2010).

In recent decades, industrial policy has further evolved to incorporate elements of globalization and technological advancement. Modern strategies emphasize participation in global value chains, digital development, and ICT diffusion, reflecting a broader understanding of industrial policy that includes sustainable development and inclusiveness (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). This evolution is evident in China's strategic focus on electric powertrains and technological innovation to bolster national competitiveness (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). Additionally, the transition towards post-industrial economies is shaped by technological progress in manufacturing, rising incomes that shift demand towards services, and the increasing knowledge intensity of manufacturing industries (OECD 2015; Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

Despite the shift towards market-oriented policies, the state's role remains pivotal in facilitating industrial transformation. Successful examples, such as those in East Asia, illustrate the efficacy of blending market forces with strategic state intervention to achieve economic growth and structural change (Rodrik 1997). However, the challenge persists in balancing these approaches with considerations of human rights, gender equality, and sustainable growth, which are increasingly recognized as integral to contemporary industrial policy frameworks (Takashi Shiraishi & Tetsushi Sonobe 2022). The evolution of industrial policy also signifies a move from protectionist measures like import substitution to more open, inclusive, and sustainable approaches that integrate horizontal policies with strategic industry development plans, focusing on technological upgrading, ecological sustainability, and gender equality (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). Furthermore, the return to ISI and EOI policies of the past is improbable, as domestic industries are now inextricably linked within the globalized economy (Gereffi 2014).

### Inclusive and Sustainable Growth
The most prominent narrative identified across the corpus is the transformative shift in industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present, which increasingly emphasizes inclusive and sustainable growth. This evolution reflects a broader integration of ecological sustainability, human rights, and gender equality into the core objectives of industrial policy. Initially, the focus was predominantly on productivity and competitiveness. However, as articulated in the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, there is now a concerted effort to address environmental catastrophes, poverty, inequity, exclusion, and conflict (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). This shift is driven by the recognition that traditional economic growth models, often reliant on market forces and trickle-down economics, have inadequately addressed systemic inequalities and environmental degradation (Stiglitz et al., 2013).

Contemporary industrial policy has thus expanded to incorporate social and environmental goals, moving beyond mere economic metrics to embrace broader societal objectives. This includes promoting ecological sustainability, advancing gender equality, and upholding human rights, which are now seen as integral to sustainable development (UNCTAD, 2018). The post-Covid19 era has further accelerated this shift, underscoring the necessity for policies that enhance social resilience and address societal challenges beyond productivity and competitiveness (Ferrannini et al., 2021).

Modern industrial policy is characterized by systemic coordination with other policy areas, such as competition, regional, and trade policies, to maximize synergies and support structural change sustainably (Aiginger & Rodrik, 2020). This involves fostering innovation and technological advancement while ensuring that economic growth does not exacerbate social inequalities or environmental harm (Mazzucato). The inclusiveness of industrial policy is contingent upon constructing new or steering existing productive coalitions that are willing to invest in collective productive capabilities and engage in a more equitable distribution of value (Chang & Andreoni, 2020).

Moreover, there is a growing emphasis on participatory and inclusive policy-making processes, involving diverse stakeholders in the design and implementation of industrial policies. This participatory approach is deemed essential for ensuring that policies are responsive to the needs of all societal groups, particularly marginalized communities, and for preventing regulatory capture and information asymmetries (Ferrannini et al., 2021). Civil society and citizens are increasingly recognized as sources of new ideas and solutions, indicating a shift towards more inclusive approaches in industrial policy (Ferrannini et al., 2021).

The current international debate on industrial policy is increasingly focused on the relationship between goals, targets, and tools, which shapes the government's ability to effectively translate general objectives into concrete and specific industrial policy programs (Ferrannini et al., 2021). The governance of production organizations and systems is critical in ensuring their reproduction, inclusiveness, and sustainability (Andreoni et al., 2019). The Capability Approach and Human Development paradigm challenge the mainstream vision of development by emphasizing the expansion of people's capabilities to lead the kind of life they value, rather than merely providing the means of living (Ferrannini et al., 2021).

Overall, the shift in industrial policy reflects a broader understanding of development that balances economic growth with social equity and environmental sustainability, aligning with global commitments such as the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) (OECD, 2021). This evolution underscores the importance of integrating inclusive and sustainable growth considerations into the core of industrial policy frameworks, ensuring that they contribute to a more equitable and resilient global economy. The sustainable human development paradigm offers a robust foundation for a normative perspective on industrial policy, composed of equity, participation and empowerment, sustainability, and productivity (Haq, 1995 in Ferrannini et al., 2021).

### Strategic Government Intervention
A primary pillar of the analysis concerns the evolution of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day, highlighting a significant shift towards strategic government intervention. This transformation underscores the importance of innovation, market creation, and addressing market failures. Initially, industrial policy was often ideologically sidelined, but it has since gained recognition for its critical role in facilitating economic growth and structural transformation (Chang and Andreoni 2020). Prominent economists such as Joseph Stiglitz and Dani Rodrik have championed a neoclassical framework that supports strategic intervention to exploit comparative advantages and foster innovation (Stiglitz, Lin, and Monga 2013). 

Modern industrial policy now emphasizes the state's role in creating and shaping markets, rather than merely correcting market failures. This approach aligns with Mariana Mazzucato's concept of the 'entrepreneurial state,' which actively engages in risk-taking and market shaping to drive technological advancements and address societal challenges (Mazzucato). As noted in earlier sections, the transition from 'government' to 'governance' underscores the importance of collaboration between public and private sectors, fostering a participatory approach to policy formulation and implementation (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Strategic government intervention today encompasses a broader set of tools beyond traditional tariffs and subsidies, incorporating public inputs, market incentives, and macroeconomic interventions to support industrial development (Fernandes and Reed 2026). The focus has expanded from manufacturing to include services and technology sectors, reflecting a more holistic view of industrial policy that integrates with other policy strands such as competition, education, and environmental policies (Aiginger & Rodrik 2020).

The systemic approach to industrial policy involves coordinating multiple policy fields to address upstream and downstream industries, sectoral change, and network development (Aiginger & Rodrik 2020). This approach is evident in the success of East Asian economies, where strategic orchestration of industrial and macroeconomic policies facilitated rapid industrialization and economic growth (Chang, 2010; Stiglitz, 1996). Overall, contemporary industrial policy is characterized by its adaptability, inclusiveness, and focus on sustainable and equitable growth, aligning with global development goals.

Recent shifts in industrial policy have also emphasized the importance of reducing demand uncertainty through tools like infant industry protection and government procurement preferences, which support domestic firms (Chang, Ha-Joon; Andreoni, Antonio 2020). Additionally, there is a growing recognition of the need to focus on 'learning in production' as a driver of innovation dynamics, contrasting with the dominant innovation policy paradigms that prioritize R&D units (Chang, Ha-Joon; Andreoni, Antonio 2020). The current debate also highlights the importance of production in sustaining learning and innovation, even in advanced economies (Chang, Ha-Joon; Andreoni, Antonio 2020).

Furthermore, industrial policy now requires mechanisms for discussing societal priority challenges and goals with key actors to ensure a systemic and coalition-building approach (Ferrannini et al. 2021). Participatory formulation of industrial policy vision and objectives can limit regulatory capture and information asymmetries, enhancing government effectiveness in driving sustainable structural change (Ferrannini et al. 2021). The role of industrial policy has expanded to include addressing market failures and proposing a taxonomy that links country characteristics to the choice of industrial policy tools, while recognizing associated risks (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan 2026). This includes technology transfer quid pro quo policies and local content regulations to ensure technology transfer and use of domestically produced inputs (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan 2026).

The evolution of industrial policy also reflects a shift towards 'smart' industrial policies that encourage knowledge generation, contrasting with traditional policies that provide protection and subsidies (Chang, Ha-Joon; Andreoni, Antonio 2020). This shift is accompanied by a focus on building state capacity, particularly bureaucratic capacity and autonomy, to effectively implement policies, which is seen as critical for policy success (Juhász, Réka; Lane, Nathan J. 2024). Embeddedness, or the connection between bureaucracies and the private sector, is now considered a crucial component of industrial policy, facilitating information exchange and addressing informational asymmetries (Juhász, Réka; Lane, Nathan J. 2024).

### Globalization and Policy Constraints
The material most densely clusters around the profound impact of globalization on the evolution of industrial policy from the post-World War II era to the present day. Initially, industrial policies were dominated by import substitution industrialization (ISI), which focused on nurturing domestic industries through protectionist measures. However, the 1980s heralded a pivotal shift towards export-oriented industrialization (EOI), aligning with the global trend of integrating into global value chains (GVCs) (Gereffi 2014). This transition reflects a broader move from producer-driven to buyer-driven commodity chains, emphasizing the role of global lead firms and suppliers (Gereffi & Sturgeon 2014).

Contemporary industrial policies are increasingly oriented towards GVCs, emphasizing international competitiveness and leveraging global supply chain linkages to enhance a country's role within these chains (Gereffi & Sturgeon 2014). This approach contrasts sharply with earlier strategies that prioritized domestic market protection and vertical integration. The shift towards GVCs necessitates policies that consider the interests of international business networks and respond to pressures from non-governmental organizations (Gereffi & Sturgeon 2014). As noted in previous sections, there are now three distinguishable types of industrial policies: 'horizontal' policies affecting the entire national economy, 'selective' policies targeted at particular industries, and GVC-oriented policies that leverage international supply chain dynamics (Gereffi & Sturgeon, supra note 4, at 342–43).

Globalization has also blurred traditional sectoral boundaries, driven by technological advancements and offshoring practices, challenging the conventional taxonomy of industrial policy (Chang & Andreoni 2020). The rise of digitalization and decarbonization further underscores the need for policies that integrate sustainable development goals (UNIDO 2024). Sustainability is becoming a major emphasis of the standards required for participation in GVCs (UNCTAD, 2017d), and modern industrial policies are increasingly diverse and complex, addressing new themes such as GVC integration, strategic positioning for the new industrial revolution (NIR), and support for the achievement of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018).

International trade agreements, such as those under the World Trade Organization (WTO), have constrained the policy space available to developing countries, limiting their ability to use industrial strategies that were once pivotal for the development of now-advanced economies (Wade 2003). These agreements often prioritize market liberalization and intellectual property protection, which can hinder inclusive and sustainable growth in developing regions (Wade 2003). The WTO's governance structure, despite being more democratic in form than the International Monetary Fund (IMF) or World Bank, still suffers from a democratic deficit that allows industrialized countries to shape rules to their advantage, highlighting a shift in industrial policy that may not fully consider inclusive growth and equality (Oxfam 2002).

The shift towards hyperglobalization, marked by the creation of the WTO, has reversed the Bretton Woods priorities, making domestic economic management subservient to international trade and finance (Rodrik 1997). This has influenced contemporary industrial policy, which now emphasizes international competitiveness and integration into global markets, often at the expense of domestic policy autonomy and inclusive growth (Rodrik 1997). The transition from import-substituting industrialization to export-oriented strategies in some regions highlights a shift towards integrating into global markets, which has been associated with varying degrees of success in fostering economic growth (Dani Rodrik 1997).

The practice of industrial policy has evolved alongside globalization, with examples like South Korea's 1960s export activity promotion under Park Chung-hee (Juhász, Réka; Lane, Nathan; Rodrik, Dani 2023). China adapted South Korea and Japan's strategies to a more globalized world, using incentives and controls on foreign direct investment (FDI) as tools of industrial policy in the post-WTO world (Juhász, Réka; Lane, Nathan; Rodrik, Dani 2023). FDI is an essential feature of both Chinese and Vietnamese industrial policy, distinct from East Asian predecessors, and offers generalizable insights and policy lessons for developing economies (Juhász, Réka; Lane, Nathan; Rodrik, Dani 2023).

The experience of East Asia shows the importance of trade and export policy in contemporary industrial development conversations, highlighting a pragmatic approach to industrial policy that adapts to global institutions (Juhász, Réka; Lane, Nathan; Rodrik, Dani 2023). The international division of labor, established post-World War II, has influenced industrial policy by delineating roles for low-wage and high-wage countries, impacting how industrial policies are designed to consider inclusive growth and equality (Amsden 1989).

Modern industrial policies are increasingly geared towards international competitiveness, designed to maximize benefits from external know-how and technology, and focused on promoting sectors that support higher participation in global value chains, while balancing horizontal and vertical measures with smart regulation and effective governance (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). The shift in industrial policy from the post-World War II period to the present day has seen a move from protectionist measures like import substitution to more open, inclusive, and sustainable approaches that integrate digital development, ICT diffusion, and participation in global production networks (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018).

The resurgence of industrial policy in high-income countries poses risks to development outcomes in less-industrialized countries by potentially pre-empting or displacing entry-level industrial opportunities, which were historically accessible due to lower labor costs (Fitoussi et al. 2000). There is a growing recognition that industrial policies should avoid appropriating or foreclosing development pathways for less-industrialized countries, which marks a shift from earlier approaches that did not consider the global implications of industrial policy (World Bank 2004).

### Green Industrial Policy
At the core of the discussion lies the transformative shift towards green industrial policy, which integrates environmental objectives with economic and social goals to foster sustainable development and transition to low-carbon economies. This approach harmonizes productivity-enhancing structural change with environmental sustainability, aligning national interests with the protection of global commons (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). The European Green Deal exemplifies this shift, aiming to transform the EU's economy for a sustainable future (Ferrannini et al., 2021).

As noted in earlier sections, the evolution of industrial policy now includes anticipating long-term trends in technology and market development, with environmental considerations becoming central due to climate change and ecological challenges (World Bank 2012; Hallegatte et al., 2013). Green industrial policy frameworks are increasingly seen as valuable tools for shaping inclusive, sustainable economies, marking a departure from traditional industrial policies focused solely on productivity and competitiveness (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). This shift is also reflected in the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, which emphasizes addressing environmental catastrophes, poverty, inequity, exclusion, and conflict (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

Countries like Germany and China illustrate the application of green industrial policies. Germany's Energiewende aims to steer the energy sector towards sustainability by meeting targets for greenhouse gas reduction, renewable energy, and energy efficiency (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). Similarly, China's shift to electric powertrains is seen as an opportunity to boost national competitiveness by leapfrogging into new technologies (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). Morocco encourages foreign direct investment in solar and wind energy projects and supports skills development and domestic supplier industries (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

Green industrial policy requires a comprehensive approach, including environmental taxes, cap-and-trade systems, and innovation subsidies to foster clean technologies (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017). It also involves managing structural change to enhance productivity while replacing environmentally unsustainable activities, reflecting a shift from traditional industrial policy approaches (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017). Environmental costs need to be better reflected in prices, regulations must be tightened, and subsidies for fossil fuels and other unsustainable goods and practices need to be phased out (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

The transition to green industrial policy involves a collaborative process with public and private actors continuously negotiating and adapting contributions to sustainable development (Altenburg et al., 2008). This approach emphasizes the need for transformative change in production systems to decouple economic development from resource depletion and waste production, aligning with goals of inclusive and sustainable growth and respect for human rights and gender equality (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). Radically new techno-institutional systems are needed to achieve this decoupling (IPCC 2014; UNEP 2011).

Green industrial policy differs from traditional industrial policies by focusing on solving collective action problems at an international scale and creating positive externalities for the global environment (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017). It involves providing incentives to adapt the national economy's structure to take advantage of changes driven by climate change mitigation and ecological challenges (World Bank 2012; Hallegatte et al. 2013; Lütkenhorst et al. 2014; Pegels 2014). Policymakers need to integrate industrial and environmental policies, rethinking industrial policy from an environmental perspective to contribute to greater competitiveness and more and better jobs (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

In the long term, there is no trade-off among social, economic, and environmental objectives, but in the short term, trade-offs must be considered, such as pricing environmental goods which may jeopardize competitiveness and employment capacity (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017). Moving to greener economies holds many benefits even from a purely economic perspective, as greater resource efficiency lowers costs, early movers may develop new markets, asset-stranding is avoided, and new job opportunities open (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

### Conflicts
Consensus is noticeably absent on the question of how industrial policy should be defined and implemented, reflecting a landscape fraught with ideological and practical conflicts. A significant debate revolves around the ideological framing of industrial policy itself. Chang and Andreoni (2020) advocate for moving beyond the traditional East Asian 'miracle' narrative, suggesting a more pragmatic approach that includes conflict management and macroeconomic considerations. This perspective contrasts with the historical ideological battles that constrained policy development until the 2008 financial crisis (Andreoni and Chang, 2019). The recent shift towards a less ideologically charged discourse has facilitated a more nuanced understanding of industrial policy (Chang & Andreoni, 2020).

Another critical area of conflict involves the integration of environmental objectives into industrial policy. Altenburg and Assmann (2017) underscore the tension between traditional productivity goals and the need for green industrial policies that align national interests with global environmental protection. However, Maltais and Suljada (2025) caution that current green policies often prioritize domestic benefits, potentially excluding emerging markets from green value chains. This exclusion challenges the inclusivity of these policies, necessitating a reimagining of green industrial policies as a cooperative global endeavor. Such an approach would avoid a two-speed transition and ensure equitable climate goals, incorporating credible financial support, meaningful technology partnerships, and inclusive trade frameworks (Maltais & Suljada, 2025).

The discourse also grapples with balancing static efficiency and long-term growth. Stiglitz (2018) highlights the conflict between policies that enhance immediate efficiency and those fostering learning and growth, a balance that modern policies strive to achieve. The distinction between vertical and horizontal policies has blurred, leading to a more integrated perspective on industrial policy (Bailey & Tomlinson, 2017; Peneder, 2017 in Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Furthermore, the resurgence of industrial policy in high-income countries raises concerns about its impact on less-industrialized nations. Fitoussi et al. (2000) and others argue that wealthy countries' policies could pre-empt opportunities for industrialization in developing economies, marking a shift from earlier approaches that did not fully consider global implications (Bhattacharyya, 2019; World Bank Group, 1985). There is a growing recognition that industrial policies should avoid appropriating or foreclosing development pathways for less-industrialized countries, reflecting a shift from earlier approaches that did not consider the global implications of industrial policy (World Bank 2004).

The lack of a uniform definition of 'industrial policy' itself is a point of contention, as noted by Altenburg and Assmann (2017), complicating the discourse and implementation of coherent strategies. This definitional ambiguity underscores the broader conflict in aligning national policies with global economic and environmental goals, reflecting a complex evolution in industrial policy discourse. The political space of interests, power relations, and conflicts underlying industrial policies should become the key focus, and the management of conflicts should be central to its successful design and implementation to avoid reinforcing horizontal inequalities (Chang & Andreoni, 2020).

The shift from the neo-liberal economic model to a different way of thinking about economic and industrial policy recognizes the limitations of neo-classical economics in addressing uneven development and inequality (Reinert, 2006; Andreoni and Chang, 2018 in Ferrannini et al. 2021). This shift is accompanied by debates on the effectiveness of free trade versus government intervention, with some arguing that East Asian success was due to market liberalization, while others highlight the role of strategic government interventions (Wade 1990). The persistence of protectionist measures, such as tariffs and non-tariff barriers, despite trade liberalization efforts, indicates a complex evolution in industrial policy where protectionism adapts rather than disappears (World Bank Group 1987).

In Europe, there is a tension between industrial policy objectives and competition policy goals, reflecting a shift in approach (Bofinger 2019, Altmaier 2019). Under the umbrella of this EU-wide framework, many EU members have continued to design and implement their own national industrial strategies, in part because 'the EU was less and less regarded as having solutions and progressively seen as an impediment to industrial restructuring' (Cohen, 2007: 222–223) (Cohen 2007 in United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014). The current approach to industrial policy in the U.S. resembles past policies, focusing on political rents and investments in battleground states, raising questions about its true goals and effectiveness (Inu Manak 2024). The new industrial policy is about saving the environment, creating jobs, and competing with China, revealing a lack of strategy about what industrial policy is supposed to achieve (Inu Manak 2024).

## What challenges and constraints do less-industrialized countries face in realizing effective industrial policy?
## Question Overview
Less-industrialized countries encounter a range of challenges in implementing effective industrial policies, primarily due to constraints related to economies of scale, technological capabilities, regulatory environments, international trade agreements, financial limitations, environmental sustainability, and internal conflicts. Small market sizes and the dominance of multinational corporations hinder local industrial growth and technological upgrading. Technological constraints are exacerbated by limited access to advanced technologies and skilled labor, while regulatory inefficiencies and corruption further complicate policy implementation. International trade agreements restrict policy space, limiting the use of traditional industrial policy tools. Financial constraints, including high debt burdens and limited fiscal space, impede necessary investments. Environmental challenges require balancing industrial growth with sustainability, while internal conflicts over the role of the state and market forces add complexity to policy design. These multifaceted issues necessitate strategic interventions and international cooperation to enable sustainable industrial development.

### Economies of Scale and Market Size
Fundamental to this body of work is the recognition that less-industrialized countries encounter formidable obstacles in crafting effective industrial policies, primarily due to limitations associated with economies of scale and market size. The constraints of small domestic markets significantly impede the attraction of foreign direct investment (FDI) and the support of large-scale industrial operations, as evidenced by the restricted industrial policy options available to smaller nations (Gereffi, 2014). This challenge is exacerbated by the predominance of large multinational corporations, which overshadow local enterprises and confine these countries to low-value-added activities (Sturgeon et al., 2014). The absence of scale further hampers domestic firms' capacity to supply reliable intermediate products, thereby obstructing technological advancement and the establishment of backward and forward linkages (Gereffi, 2018; Salazar-Xirinachs et al., 2014). Moreover, firms engaged in routine assembly tasks within global value chains (GVCs) experience diminished earnings, offer lower wages, and are more susceptible to economic fluctuations, thereby constraining both economic performance and social welfare (Sturgeon, Gereffi, Guinn & Zylberberg, supra note 93, at 33).

The limited market size also restricts the efficacy of industrial policy instruments. Nations with constrained fiscal resources and governmental capacity often resort to suboptimal policy measures such as tariffs, which are less effective and can incur additional costs (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). High tariffs on intermediate inputs and the limited potential for scale in small markets diminish incentives for quality enhancement and innovation (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). Furthermore, the absence of expansive domestic markets, akin to those in Brazil or India, complicates import substitution efforts, thereby reducing the advantages of tariffs and local content mandates (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). The dependency on imports of semi-processed raw materials and capital goods further restricts the formation of backward and forward linkages (Gereffi, 2018; Gereffi and Sturgeon, 2013; Salazar-Xirinachs, et al., 2014).

Additionally, the focus on low-value-added segments within GVCs curtails future learning opportunities and economic progression. Foreign-owned enterprises frequently establish minimal linkages due to the limited capacities of local suppliers, further entrenching less-industrialized countries in unprofitable segments of the value chain (Gereffi, 2018; Salazar-Xirinachs et al., 2014). The issues of premature deindustrialization and the imperative for structural transformation and enhancement of manufacturing sectors are crucial for alleviating poverty and elevating income levels (Rodrik, 2016; Aiginger & Rodrik, 2020). Low total-factor productivity is a pivotal factor in the persistence of poverty in developing nations, underscoring the necessity for productivity growth to mitigate poverty (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017). Without addressing these constraints, less-industrialized countries face significant challenges in implementing effective industrial policies that can foster sustainable economic growth and development.

### Technological Capabilities and Innovation
A highly consistent pattern emerges regarding the technological challenges faced by less-industrialized countries in their pursuit of effective industrial policy. Central to these challenges is the limited access to advanced technologies and the requisite skills for their effective utilization. Many developing nations are constrained by reliance on outdated, inefficient technologies, which significantly impairs their global competitiveness (Fernandes & Reed 2026). This technological lag is further exacerbated by a shortage of skilled labor and inadequate educational systems, which fail to produce a workforce equipped for modern industrial demands (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2024). Particularly in Africa, educational deficits, characterized by limited access to primary education and high dropout rates, severely restrict the capacity to formulate and implement effective industrial policies (Oxfam 2002). 

As previously discussed, integration into global value chains (GVCs) often relegates these countries to low-skill activities, thereby stifling opportunities for technological learning and advancement (Chang & Andreoni 2020). This situation is further compounded by the erosion of competitive advantages due to technological advancements such as automation, which diminish the reliance on low-wage labor (Fernandes & Reed 2026). The transition to green technologies is similarly hindered by financial and institutional constraints, as well as market failures like information asymmetries (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). Developing countries face significant hurdles in adopting green technologies due to limited financial, institutional, and professional resources, coupled with a lack of experience in state-driven technology development (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

Intellectual property rights (IPR) regimes, particularly under the TRIPS agreement, present additional barriers by restricting reverse engineering and imitation—strategies historically employed by nations such as South Korea and Taiwan to achieve technological advancement (Oxfam 2002). This restriction increases the costs of technology transfer, thereby widening the technology gap and exacerbating income inequalities in developing countries, which in turn constrains their ability to implement effective industrial policies (Oxfam 2002).

The advent of the New Industrial Revolution (NIR) intensifies competition for high-tech investments, which less-industrialized countries find challenging to attract due to inadequate infrastructure and technological readiness (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). The absence of robust domestic innovation systems and a dependency on foreign technology providers further limits the effectiveness of their industrial policies (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2024). The NIR also influences investor behavior, with firms increasingly opting for non-equity modes of operations and services trade, which impacts the readiness to engage in joint ventures or build local supplier capacity (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018).

Emerging economies face the daunting task of advancing to the forefront of GVCs and encouraging global suppliers to establish facilities within their borders to reduce risks and lower entry barriers for local firms (Gereffi & Sturgeon, supra note 4, at 353). Large emerging economies that supply primary products to China encounter the significant challenge of enhancing the technological content of their exports to transition into higher-value activities (Gereffi, Gary 2014). The middle-income trap presents a formidable obstacle for emerging states, as they struggle to compete with both low-wage economies and technologically advanced high-wage economies, complicating efforts to sustain economic growth (Takashi Shiraishi; Tetsushi Sonobe 2022).

Collectively, these constraints underscore the imperative for strategic policy interventions aimed at building local technological capabilities, enhancing education and skills training, and fostering environments conducive to innovation and technology transfer. Developing countries must create conditions that facilitate the flow of technologies and stimulate growth, even amidst sub-optimal microeconomic policies, weak institutions, and the absence of comprehensive private property rights (Lin, Justin Yifu 2012).

### Regulatory and Bureaucratic Challenges
The analysis suggests that a central driver of the regulatory and bureaucratic challenges faced by less-industrialized countries in implementing effective industrial policy is the complex and unstable regulatory environments that prevail in these regions. These environments are often characterized by bureaucratic inefficiencies, corruption, and political instability, which collectively create uncertainty and hinder economic development (Gereffi 2014; World Bank Group 1991). For instance, the 'Brazil cost'—a term that encapsulates the barriers posed by poor infrastructure, excessive bureaucracy, and high interest rates—illustrates the formidable obstacles to policy implementation in Brazil (Gereffi 2014). Furthermore, the inconsistency of policies, such as Brazil's ethanol policy, exacerbates these challenges by fostering uncertainty that deters investment and innovation (Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) are particularly susceptible to these regulatory challenges due to their limited influence over policymakers, which restricts their ability to navigate the intricate bureaucratic systems (Gereffi 2014). As noted in previous sections, the lack of state capacity to coordinate economic agents and stabilize confidence in government actions often substitutes for the formalization of rules, further complicating the policy landscape (Wade 2010). This is consistent with earlier findings that highlight the limitations of market-based resource allocation in the presence of coordination failures, a significant challenge for less-industrialized countries in realizing effective industrial policy (Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

The prevalence of corruption and rent-seeking behavior further undermines the efficiency of government functions and distorts policy outcomes, complicating the implementation of industrial policies (World Bank Group 1991). This issue is compounded by the lack of bureaucratic autonomy and capacity, which results in lower-quality policy outcomes and increases the risk of political capture (Juhász, Lane, & Rodrik 2024). As previously discussed, less-industrialized countries face challenges such as political capture and information asymmetries between lobbyists and the public sector, which can lead to the misuse of industrial policy by interest groups (Rodrik 2004).

Moreover, the administrative complexity and high costs associated with regulatory compliance deter participation and skew government support towards businesses with more administrative effectiveness, as evidenced by Nepal's cash rebate scheme for exporters (Fernandes & Reed 2026). The lack of coordination across public policies and regulations often limits the impact of industrial policies, as seen in Latin America and the Caribbean, where policy silos hinder effective implementation (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2024). Coordination failure is particularly problematic when change is system-wide and transformative, such as in the greening of economies (Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

Transformations invariably create winners and losers, necessitating governments to build consensus on the direction of change and facilitate compromises among stakeholders (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). Some green industrial policies require robust governance of institutions to avoid rent-seeking and political capture by vested interests (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). As noted earlier, the task of enhancing the state's capacity to coordinate economic agents is more feasible than the across-the-board formalization and enforcement of rules, which demands high fixed costs and many decades (Wade 2010).

In conclusion, the regulatory and bureaucratic challenges in less-industrialized countries present a formidable barrier to the realization of effective industrial policy. Addressing these challenges necessitates significant improvements in governance, institutional capacity, and policy coherence to create a more stable and predictable environment for industrial development. As highlighted in previous discussions, industrial policy involves strategic collaboration between the private sector and governments to jointly identify barriers that require public-private coordination for their removal (Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

### International Trade and Investment Constraints
The most prominent narrative identified across the corpus highlights the formidable challenges less-industrialized countries face in implementing effective industrial policies, primarily due to constraints imposed by international trade agreements and investment treaties. These agreements often curtail the policy space available to these nations by prohibiting the use of traditional industrial policy tools such as tariffs, subsidies, and domestic content requirements. For instance, the WTO's Agreement on Trade-Related Investment Measures (TRIMs) and the Agreement on Subsidies and Countervailing Measures (SCM) impose stringent limitations on performance requirements and subsidies, which have historically been pivotal for industrial development (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017; WTO 2011b). The ambiguity surrounding GATT Article XVIII further complicates the use of industrial policy measures, as its exceptions for industrial policy purposes remain underutilized (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

The proliferation of bilateral and regional trade agreements exacerbates these constraints by extending WTO disciplines and introducing new obligations that limit policy autonomy. These agreements often encompass stringent rules on intellectual property rights and investment-related measures, which can hinder technological capacity and industrial policy effectiveness (Rodrik, 2004; United Nations Conference on Trade and Development, 2014). The 'single undertaking' provision of the WTO and the spread of investment treaties have significantly restricted the policy space for developing countries, complicating their ability to employ industrial policies that were once standard (Chang & Andreoni, 2020). Furthermore, the 'lock-in' effect of international investment agreements (IIAs) binds countries to commitments once an industry is opened to foreign investment, limiting their ability to adjust policies over time (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018).

As noted in earlier sections, the global integration of trade and production through global value chains (GVCs) transmits the pressures of global competition to domestic markets in developing economies, leaving less space for local firms to design, produce, and market independently (Gereffi, 2014). This integration often results in developing countries being locked into low-value-added activities, as lead firms in GVCs impose high standards and control over production processes, limiting the ability of these countries to move up the value chain (Milberg & Winkler, 2013). GVC-oriented industrial policies, which focus on integrating into these chains, contrast with ISI-style policies that attempt to recreate entire supply chains domestically (Gereffi & Sturgeon, supra note 4, at 342–43).

The dominance of multinational enterprises and the concentration of foreign direct investment (FDI) in a few countries further constrain the industrial policy space of less-industrialized countries. These countries often struggle to attract FDI outside the natural resource sector, which limits their ability to leverage FDI for broader industrial development (Wade, 2010; Stiglitz et al., 2013). Additionally, the investor-state dispute settlement mechanisms in international investment agreements pose financial risks and reduce the regulatory space of host countries, further complicating the implementation of effective industrial policies (Brauch, 2014). The financial costs of bilateral investment treaties can be exorbitant and difficult to justify, presenting constraints for less-industrialized countries (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014).

Environmental standards and trade-related aspects of green industrial policies are also increasingly regulated through international agreements, posing additional challenges for less-industrialized countries. These countries often find it difficult to compete if they adhere to traditional products and processes, as developed countries impose higher environmental standards and trade sanctions (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). The uncertainty of trade and investment law creates a 'grey zone' for less-industrialized countries, discouraging them from pursuing green industrial policy due to the high costs and resource scarcity associated with managing potential disputes (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

Overall, the constraints imposed by international trade and investment agreements, coupled with the structural power dynamics in global markets, significantly limit the ability of less-industrialized countries to pursue effective industrial policies. These constraints necessitate reforms in transnational institutions to expand policy space and enable these countries to implement strategies that align with their developmental needs (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development, 2014). The resurgence of industrial policy in wealthy countries poses additional risks to development outcomes in less-industrialized countries, as these wealthier nations can leverage significant financial resources to compete in global markets, potentially foreclosing entry-level industrial opportunities essential for less-industrialized countries (OECD 2021).

### Financial Constraints
A primary pillar of the analysis concerns the financial constraints that significantly hinder the realization of effective industrial policy in less-industrialized countries. These constraints manifest primarily through limited access to finance, high debt burdens, and fiscal limitations. As previously noted, the legacy of import substitution industrialization (ISI) has left many nations grappling with persistent trade deficits and escalating debt service payments, resulting in a net outflow of foreign capital that stifles economic growth (Gereffi 2014). High real interest rates further deter investments in the manufacturing sector, while overvalued currencies undermine export competitiveness (Chang & Andreoni 2020). Additionally, aligning traditional industrial policy instruments with macroeconomic policies proves challenging, exacerbating balance of payments constraints and limiting investment opportunities (Chang & Andreoni 2020).

The financialization of the global economy has weakened governmental capacities, thereby reducing the ability to implement effective industrial policies (Chang & Andreoni 2020). This issue is compounded by a lack of regulatory capabilities, leading to over-financialization and diverting funds from productive investments (Chang & Andreoni 2020). Addressing financialization is complex from a single-country policy perspective, as it involves various mechanisms and actors across different levels (Chang & Andreoni 2020). The reduced capacity of governments to make investments or support private investments through industrial policy, combined with the financialization of corporations, has pushed economies towards a spiral of dis-investment and de-accumulation (Chang & Andreoni 2020). Consequently, corporate governance and investment decisions can be captured by short-term oriented financial interests, resulting in declining investments and missed opportunities for innovation (Andreoni and Chang, 2019).

As discussed in earlier sections, the reliance on tariffs for revenue, due to limited fiscal space, often results in the employment of less effective industrial policy tools (Fernandes & Reed 2026). Debt burdens present a major challenge, with many countries unable to sustain necessary investments for industrial development due to high debt service obligations (World Bank Group 1991). The inadequacy of international financial mechanisms, which often rely on loans rather than grants, fails to deliver meaningful investment in critical infrastructure (Maltais & Suljada 2025). Moreover, the lack of financial institutions capable of supporting long-term investments hinders the implementation of green industrial policies, as the cost of achieving a green transformation is perceived as too high for low-income countries (Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

The fiscal constraints and high levels of debt in developing countries limit their ability to finance industrial policies effectively, as they struggle to secure the necessary financial resources (UNIDO 2024). The global financial architecture exacerbates these challenges by making access to financing inadequate and expensive, with borrowing terms more costly than in developed countries (UNIDO 2024). This unequal financial landscape limits the ability of less-industrialized countries to compete with wealthier nations that can leverage significant financial resources to dominate global markets (World Bank Group 2006). Additionally, the challenges of aligning industrial policy with macroeconomic stability, such as managing competitive exchange rate devaluations, are difficult to sustain and can trigger retaliation by other countries (Fernandes & Reed 2026).

Overall, the financial constraints faced by less-industrialized countries are multifaceted, involving high borrowing costs, limited fiscal space, and inadequate financial systems, all of which hinder the effective implementation of industrial policies necessary for sustainable economic development. These constraints are further compounded by the need for long-term financial commitments for clean technologies, which venture capitalists are not willing or able to undertake, necessitating sustained government funding (Mazzucato). The lack of financial resources and institutional capacity in developing countries limits their ability to invest in green technologies and implement effective industrial policies (Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

### Environmental and Energy Challenges
The material most densely clusters around the formidable environmental and energy challenges that less-industrialized countries face in their pursuit of effective industrial policy. Central to this discourse is the tension between industrial growth and environmental sustainability, a balance that is increasingly emphasized by the universality of the Agenda 2030 for Sustainable Development (Ferrannini et al. 2021). Despite this global mandate, many developing nations prioritize immediate economic growth over environmental objectives, positing that the resultant income can later fund environmental remediation (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). This approach, however, is fraught with complexity, as it necessitates a structural transformation to decouple human development from non-renewable resource consumption (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). The urgency of integrating human development, social cohesion, and sustainability into industrial policy is particularly pronounced in the post-Covid19 era (Ferrannini et al. 2021).

Energy constraints further exacerbate these challenges. Many developing countries remain tethered to inefficient technologies, a situation perpetuated by energy subsidies that entrench inequities and obstruct the adoption of green technologies (Clements et al. 2014; Altenburg & Assmann 2017). The transition to renewable energy is impeded by market failures, prohibitive costs, and the entrenched dominance of fossil fuel-based systems (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). Additionally, issues such as grid connectivity and the monopolistic control of state-owned enterprises pose significant barriers to renewable energy projects (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). The lack of infrastructure and institutional capacity further limits the implementation of industrial policies that align with environmental goals (World Bank 2012).

Environmental degradation presents additional hurdles, undermining economic productivity and exacerbating poverty, particularly in regions like Sub-Saharan Africa (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2024). The financial burden of replacing fossil-fuel infrastructure and the risk of stranded assets complicate efforts to transition to sustainable industrial practices (World Bank 2012; Altenburg & Assmann 2017). Moreover, the scarcity of clean freshwater and health issues related to pollution impose significant welfare burdens on impoverished populations (WHO 2016a).

As previously discussed, international trade and investment treaties impose further constraints by regulating environmental issues, complicating the competitive landscape for less-industrialized countries adhering to traditional industrial practices (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). The necessity for comprehensive environmental policies that avoid the pitfalls of past industrialization paths is critical for sustainable development (Dasgupta et al. 2002). The global shift towards green industrial policies poses significant risks for emerging markets and developing economies (EMDEs), potentially locking them into extractive roles without capturing higher-value production stages (UNCTAD, 2023a).

Addressing the dual challenge of fostering economic growth while ensuring environmental sustainability requires integrated policy frameworks and international cooperation. Green industrial policies offer a viable pathway to shaping inclusive, sustainable economies globally (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). However, these policies must be underpinned by stable, long-term frameworks to prevent misallocation and political capture (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). Notably, less-industrialized countries possess the advantage of not being entrenched in unsustainable infrastructures, allowing them to develop systems in more sustainable ways (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). Nonetheless, the perceived high cost of achieving a green transformation remains a significant constraint for low-income countries with pressing needs, challenging the implementation of effective industrial policy (Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

### Conflicts
The record presents a complex landscape of competing interpretations of the role of the state in the industrial policy of less-industrialized countries. One school of thought champions robust state intervention, positing that government involvement is indispensable for addressing market failures and catalyzing industrial growth. Advocates such as Amsden (1989) and Mazzucato argue that the state must mediate market forces, create price distortions to guide investment, and spearhead high-risk sectors where private investment is lacking. They cite successful examples like Korea and Taiwan, where state intervention has been pivotal in overcoming structural challenges and fostering technological advancement (Wade, 1990; Amsden, 1989). This perspective underscores the necessity for the state to assume an entrepreneurial role, engaging in risk-taking and crafting new visions beyond merely correcting market failures (Mazzucato). The developmental state, they argue, is better positioned to diversify than the private sector due to its comprehensive involvement in national and corporate planning, offering an economic overview that individual entrepreneurs cannot match (Amsden, 1989).

Conversely, another perspective critiques the constraints imposed by global economic structures and the ideological dominance of market fundamentalism. Critics assert that the Washington Consensus and IMF policies have curtailed the state's role, leading to premature deindustrialization and restricting policy space for strategic investments (Stiglitz, 2018; Khan, 2012). This view suggests that globalization and the influence of international institutions like the IMF and World Bank have eroded national policymaking autonomy, compelling less-industrialized countries to adopt policies misaligned with their developmental needs (Rodrik, 1997; Friedman, 1999/2000). The prevalence of market fundamentalist ideologies is seen as undermining the state's capacity to lead innovation and economic growth (Block, 2008). Additionally, the geopolitical rivalry between the United States and China, each representing distinct capitalist models, introduces tensions that affect less-industrialized countries as they strive to transform their economies and stimulate growth (Milanovic, 2020).

A third perspective highlights internal challenges within less-industrialized countries, such as weak state capacity, entrenched interest groups, and political pressures that impede effective policy implementation. Amsden (1989) observes that weak states struggle with policy execution, while Wade (1990) warns of the dangers posed by predatory rulers and emphasizes the need for a disciplined state that advances capital rather than hoarding it. The political economy of industrial policy is fraught with risks of rent-seeking and inefficiencies, as governments may extend support beyond its effective duration, fostering dependency (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). In fragile and conflict-affected countries, the set of feasible industrial policy tools is further constrained by violent conflict, insecurity, and displacement (Barattieri et al., 2025).

These conflicting views underscore the complexity of designing and implementing industrial policy in less-industrialized countries, where the balance between state intervention and market forces remains a contentious issue. Moreover, the challenge of aligning industrial policy with latent comparative advantage and targeting industries in countries with higher per capita income is significant (Lin, 2012). The debate on whether to follow or defy comparative advantage presents a challenge in determining the appropriate strategic orientation (Warwick, 2013). Furthermore, the absence of a symbiotic innovation ecosystem, where public and private sectors share risks and rewards, often leads to parasitic dynamics (Mazzucato). The political and ideological structures in less-industrialized countries can constrain the development of effective industrial policy by limiting political freedoms and civil rights, which are essential for fostering innovation and economic growth (Wade, 1990).

### Other
A subset of the corpus provides more focused insight into the multifaceted challenges that less-industrialized countries encounter in their pursuit of effective industrial policy, particularly highlighting structural and institutional constraints. A notable issue is the underutilization of regional cooperation and integration, which could significantly enhance productivity and competitiveness. For example, Nicaragua's apparel firms stand to gain from supply arrangements with textile firms in neighboring countries such as Honduras, El Salvador, and Guatemala (Gereffi, 2014). However, these potential regional synergies remain largely untapped.

The rapid and compressed development driven by global value chains (GVCs) introduces additional economic and social policy challenges, especially in sectors like healthcare and education (Gereffi, 2014). This accelerated development often surpasses the capacity of less-industrialized countries to adapt their social infrastructure accordingly, creating a mismatch between economic growth and social development.

As previously discussed, the shifting organization of global production and the increasing financialization of the world economy further complicate the landscape for these countries (Chang & Andreoni, 2020). These changes, along with evolving global economic rules, create a complex environment that less-industrialized countries must navigate. Additionally, the supply-side bias prevalent in industrial policy debates often overlooks the impacts of demand changes on sectoral diversification and restructuring (Chang & Andreoni, 2020).

The differential impact of global crises, such as the Covid-19 pandemic, exacerbates these challenges by exposing vulnerabilities within social and economic structures (Harvey, 2020; OECD, 2020a; Piketty, 2020; Stiglitz, 2020; Venkatapuram, 2020). Moreover, the integration of industrial policy into Neoclassical frameworks has introduced limitations that are particularly challenging for less-industrialized countries (Andreoni & Chang, 2018 in Ferrannini et al., 2021).

Finally, the collapse of global economic activity, rising unemployment, and increasing inequality contribute to a fragmented global landscape, complicating the implementation of effective industrial policies (Ferrannini et al., 2021). These countries also face the challenge of organizing social groups to effectively influence policy-making processes, as institutional structures often impede collective action and learning processes (Ferrannini et al., 2021).

## What key recommendations can Oxfam make to more industrialized countries so that their industrial policies do less harm to less-industrialized countries?
## Question Overview
Oxfam's recommendations for industrialized countries to mitigate harm to less-industrialized nations through their industrial policies are organized into several key themes. Firstly, preserving policy space is crucial, advocating for industrial policies that do not constrain the developmental autonomy of less-industrialized countries, while promoting ecological sustainability and gender equality. Reforming transnational institutions is also emphasized, suggesting changes in global governance to enhance the policy autonomy of developing nations. Avoidance of harmful industrial policies is another focus, recommending strategies that prevent the foreclosure of development pathways and encourage sustainable practices. Supporting sustainable development involves integrating ecological and social goals into industrial policies, ensuring they align with global climate targets. Promoting inclusive industrial policies is essential, advocating for stakeholder participation and transparency to prevent regulatory capture. Finally, managing conflicts in industrial policy design is highlighted, balancing protectionism and liberalization to ensure equitable industrial development.

### Policy Space Preservation
Fundamental to this body of work is the preservation of policy space for less-industrialized countries, a critical aspect that Oxfam emphasizes in its recommendations to more industrialized nations. Oxfam advocates for the design of industrial policies by wealthier countries that do not impede the ability of less-industrialized nations to pursue their own industrialization strategies. This involves avoiding the appropriation or foreclosure of essential development pathways, particularly those related to entry-level industrial opportunities (Chang & Andreoni, 2020; Altenburg & Assmann, 2017; Wade, 1990). It is imperative that industrialized countries refrain from leveraging their influence in global governance to curtail the policy autonomy of less-industrialized countries (Gereffi, 2014). Instead, these countries should align their industrial policies with macroeconomic strategies that preserve the developmental options available to less-industrialized nations (Chang & Andreoni, 2020).

Moreover, industrial policies should be crafted to support reductions in inequality, promote ecological sustainability, advance gender equality, and uphold human rights, ensuring they do not overshadow the development pathways of less-industrialized countries (Mazzucato; UNCTAD, 2018). This includes recognizing the strategic value of critical minerals and supporting less-industrialized countries in negotiating rights and conditions as part of their domestic industrial policy (UNIDO, 2024). More industrialized countries should ensure their policies enable less-industrialized countries to identify and select new industries consistent with their comparative advantage and coordinate private firms' investments to form competitive clusters (Joseph Stiglitz; Justin Lin; Célestin Monga; Ebrahim Patel, 2013).

Oxfam also suggests that industrialized countries adopt cooperative approaches and diverse partnerships that emphasize different regions' competitive advantages, thereby avoiding constraints on the roles of less-industrialized countries in global value chains (Hausmann & Ahuja, 2023). Furthermore, industrial policies should be flexible and adaptable to changing circumstances, allowing less-industrialized countries to pursue their own industrialization strategies without being constrained by rigid international agreements (UNCTAD, 2018). The success of East Asian economies in designing and implementing smart government interventions demonstrates the potential for promoting industries aligned with a country's latent comparative advantage (Joseph Stiglitz; Justin Lin; Célestin Monga; Ebrahim Patel, 2013).

Finally, Oxfam advocates for reforms in transnational institutions to expand the policy space available to less-industrialized countries, enabling them to pursue their own industrialization strategies without being overshadowed by the financial and competitive advantages of wealthier nations (World Bank Group, 1996). This includes supporting the development of strong policy frameworks in less-industrialized countries to address economic barriers and promote sustainable development (Apeaning & Thollander, 2013). A new global deal is needed, based on stronger solidarity, in which today’s wealthy industrialized nations support the most vulnerable countries in finance, capabilities, technologies, skills, and policy space (United Nations Industrial Development Organization, 2024).

Oxfam's advocacy can focus on urging more industrialized countries to support reforms in global governance bodies, such as the WTO, and in bilateral trade agreements, ensuring these frameworks do not limit the autonomy of developing nations (Gereffi, 2014; Stiglitz, 2018; Mazzucato). This includes revising trade and investment rules to prioritize development goals and allow for greater flexibility in policy implementation (Rodrik, 2001; United Nations Conference on Trade and Development, 2014).

To address tax competition, developing countries should collaborate to reach agreements that restrict the scope for race-to-the-bottom tax competition, instead fostering a race-to-the-top competition to provide good physical and institutional infrastructure (Stiglitz, Joseph E. 2018). 

Furthermore, Oxfam can suggest that more industrialized countries support the creation of a multilateral framework that ensures international financial stability and a more open trading system, which would help minimize adverse international spillovers and negative externalities created by national economic policies (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014). 

Finally, as noted in the previous section, Oxfam should advocate for reforms in transnational institutions to expand the policy space available to less-industrialized countries, enabling them to pursue their own industrialization strategies without being overshadowed by the financial and competitive advantages of wealthier nations (World Bank Group 1987).

### Avoidance of Harmful Industrial Policies
The analysis suggests that a central driver of this theme is the imperative for industrialized countries to avoid implementing industrial policies that inadvertently harm less-industrialized nations. Oxfam's strategic recommendations emphasize the necessity of designing policies that do not pre-empt or displace entry-level industrial opportunities, which are vital for the development of less-industrialized countries (Lin, Justin Yifu 2012; World Bank Group 2005). As highlighted in previous sections, it is crucial for industrialized countries to craft policies that preserve development pathways, particularly in sectors where less-industrialized countries hold comparative advantages (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017; United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). Furthermore, a nuanced understanding of global industrial organization patterns is essential to minimize potential harm (Gereffi & Sturgeon, supra note 4, at 353).

In addition, industrial policies must comply with WTO regulations to avoid countervailing tariffs that could restrict export market access for less-industrialized countries (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan 2026). Avoiding protectionist measures such as tariffs and local content requirements is critical, as these can increase costs and limit market access for developing economies (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan 2026). Instead, policies should prioritize public inputs and targeted production or innovation subsidies, which are more economically efficient and less detrimental (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan 2026). Moreover, industrialized countries can mitigate harm by employing policy tools that reduce demand uncertainty and support domestic firms, such as infant industry protection and government procurement preferences (Chang, Ha-Joon; Andreoni, Antonio 2020).

Oxfam also advocates for international cooperation among industrialized countries to discourage inefficient policies like export bans and local content requirements, which can negatively impact less-industrialized countries (Vidhi Mardia 2025). Additionally, integrating sustainable development concerns into industrial policies is crucial to prevent exacerbating inequalities or environmental degradation (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). It is important to avoid imposing models such as the Washington Consensus, post-Washington Consensus, or Beijing Consensus on developing countries (Ferrannini et al., 2021).

Furthermore, industrialized countries should facilitate the development of competitive industrial clusters in less-industrialized countries by promoting their integration into regional production networks (Kuroiwa 2009). This includes enhancing cross-border R&D collaborations to bolster technological capabilities and innovation in developing economies (OECD 2021). Industrial policies should not serve as temporary fixes but should set milestones for fundamental improvements over the policy's duration (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan 2026).

Finally, Oxfam recommends the removal of trade barriers and the reduction of agricultural subsidies that distort global markets, disadvantaging farmers in less-industrialized countries (World Bank Group 2005). By adopting these recommendations, industrialized countries can align their industrial policies with global development goals, minimizing harm to less-industrialized countries and fostering a more equitable global economic environment. Oxfam also suggests that industrialized countries engage in road-mapping exercises with the private sector to create joint visions and credible expectations, reducing uncertainty and enabling new market creation without harming less-industrialized countries (Chang, Ha-Joon; Andreoni, Antonio 2020).

### Support for Sustainable Development
The most prominent narrative identified across the corpus emphasizes the integration of sustainable development goals into the industrial policies of more industrialized countries, as advocated by Oxfam. This integration is crucial to ensure that such policies do not inadvertently harm less-industrialized nations. Oxfam underscores the importance of embedding ecological sustainability, gender equality, and human rights within these frameworks, thereby fostering socio-technical transitions towards sustainability and restructuring value chains for greater inclusivity (Ferrannini et al. 2021; Altenburg & Assmann 2017). As previously discussed, green industrial policies should not only accelerate structural transformation and enhance productivity but also provide significant economic and social co-benefits (Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

Consistent with earlier findings, Oxfam recommends the elimination of incentives that lead to environmental degradation, advocating for the adoption of cleaner technologies in developing countries to avoid the repetition of historical environmental missteps (Sumaila et al. 2016; Coady et al. 2017; Dasgupta et al. 2002). Furthermore, it is imperative that global environmental agreements are inclusive of developing countries, ensuring mutual benefits and addressing global environmental challenges collectively (Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

A systemic approach is advocated, involving mission-oriented innovation programs and coordinated investments aimed at developing critical technologies. This approach necessitates the reconfiguration of economic incentives to accelerate technological innovations and the phasing out of harmful subsidies (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). Moreover, industrial policies should be inclusive, supporting reductions in inequality and promoting gender equality, while avoiding the foreclosure of development pathways for less-industrialized countries (Aiginger & Rodrik 2020; Juhász et al. 2023).

International cooperation is essential to ensure that trade and industrial frameworks align with global climate targets and do not undermine the ability of less-industrialized countries to participate equitably (Maltais & Suljada 2025). Finally, Oxfam advocates for the support of green sector development in less-industrialized countries through technology transfer and adaptation, ensuring these nations benefit from sustainable industrial growth. This includes providing long-term investment horizons and stable incentives to reduce market uncertainty and boost investor confidence (OECD 2012b; Altenburg & Assmann 2017).

### Promotion of Inclusive Industrial Policies
A primary pillar of the analysis concerns the promotion of inclusive industrial policies, a theme that Oxfam underscores in its recommendations to industrialized countries. Central to this advocacy is the adoption of strategies that ensure broad stakeholder participation and transparency in the design and implementation of industrial policies. This approach involves participatory governance and collective appraisal, which are essential to prevent regulatory capture and to facilitate sustainable structural change (Ferrannini et al. 2021). As noted in previous sections, policy coherence and multi-level alignment are crucial to avoid redundancies and overlaps, thereby fostering a synergistic combination of top-down and bottom-up processes (Ferrannini et al. 2021). Managing conflicts that arise from industrial policy is also critical to ensuring inclusiveness in the industrial development process (Chang & Andreoni 2020).

In alignment with earlier findings, industrial policies should support small and medium enterprises (SMEs) by fostering linkages with large firms, thereby enhancing SME development and integration into global value chains (2006). This strategy should be complemented by market access assistance, including export promotion and supplier development programs, which aim to reduce costs and information gaps for businesses in less-industrialized countries (Fernandes & Reed 2026). Bundling services such as counseling, trade missions, and trade fairs often yields stronger results than stand-alone efforts in market access assistance (Fernandes & Reed 2026).

Oxfam advocates for industrial policies that enhance transparency and accountability to prevent corruption and collusive behavior (Rodrik 2014). This includes implementing reporting requirements, obligations to disclosure, and democratic checks and balances (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). Additionally, policies should maintain full autonomy in decision-making and employ disciplining devices against abuse to minimize risks of collusion and capture by private interests (Evans 1995). More industrialized countries should ensure their industrial policies maintain close contact with businesses, offer smart incentives, and operate with full transparency and accountability (Fernandes & Reed 2026).

To ensure inclusivity, industrial policies should promote ecological sustainability, gender equality, and uphold human rights, while avoiding the appropriation or foreclosure of development pathways essential to less-industrialized countries (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). This includes supporting reductions in inequality and ensuring fair competition by balancing trade-offs between labor-absorbing and high-wage activities (Fernandes & Reed 2026). ### Conflicts
The material reflects a clear divergence regarding the conflicts inherent in industrial policy recommendations, revealing significant discursive tensions. On one hand, there is a strong advocacy for transparency and conflict management in policy design. As noted in earlier sections, Chang and Andreoni (2020) emphasize the importance of industrialized countries announcing policy priorities with clear justifications and performance targets. This approach aims to mitigate accusations of favoritism and corruption, while also incorporating anticipatory conflict management measures, such as social insurance, to ease resistance to beneficial changes. This perspective underscores the necessity of managing conflicts to ensure that the industrial development process remains inclusive and equitable.

Conversely, critics like Manak (2024) argue that the resurgence of industrial policy is misguided, potentially leading to adverse economic and geopolitical outcomes. Berasaluce Iza (2024) warns against simplistic, protectionist policies that could have harmful international repercussions. This view aligns with the Economist (2010a), which suggests that innovation should be market-driven rather than government-directed, advocating for a liberalized approach that avoids picking winners.

A historical perspective offered by Wade (1990) and Chang (2007) challenges the neoclassical emphasis on free trade, noting that successful industrialized nations often employed high protection and controlled financial systems. This view is supported by the World Bank Group (1987), which highlights the mutual interest of management and labor in traditional industries to seek protection for maintaining jobs and profits.

Adding to the complexity, Ogletree (2012) cautions against concentrating economic and political power in government hands, critiquing both laissez-faire capitalism and Marxist communism. Thompson (1995) further warns that trade liberalization can negatively impact unskilled labor in industrialized countries, suggesting a need for careful consideration of domestic labor impacts.

These conflicting perspectives underscore a fundamental dispute over the balance between protectionism and liberalization, the role of government versus market forces, and the implications of industrial policy on both domestic and international scales. The debate reflects broader ideological divides and highlights the complexity of formulating industrial policies that minimize harm to less-industrialized countries.

## What key recommendations can Oxfam make to rich countries to advance reform of different transnational institutions, so as to increase the policy space available to less-industrialized countries, to a point that it is, at least, comparable with the policy space afforded to industrialized nations?

Oxfam can make several key recommendations to rich countries to reform transnational institutions, thereby increasing the policy space for less-industrialized countries. Flexibility in international agreements is emphasized, advocating for mechanisms that allow less-industrialized countries to adapt commitments to their developmental needs. Reforming the Foreign Direct Investment framework is crucial for equitable participation in global systems, while institutional governance reforms aim to ensure fair representation and decision-making. ### Investment Protection and Dispute Resolution Reform
At the core of the discussion lies the imperative to reform investment protection and dispute resolution mechanisms, a crucial step towards expanding the policy space available to less-industrialized countries. Central to this reform is the recommendation for the establishment of a centralized, permanent international investment tribunal. Such a tribunal would offer a more balanced and consistent forum for resolving disputes, thereby enhancing the policy space for these nations (UNCTAD, 2013b; United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014). This initiative addresses the current imbalance where developed nations, notably the United States, wield disproportionate influence over international institutions, as demonstrated by their capacity to block appointments to entities like the WTO Appellate Body (Julen Berasaluce Iza 2024).

In addition, a strategic retreat from restrictive investment treaties in favor of national law is advocated as a means to reclaim policy autonomy (UNCTAD, 2013b; United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014). This approach resonates with the principle of sovereign equality, ensuring that investment rules do not asymmetrically disadvantage less-industrialized nations (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014). Some countries have already pursued strategies such as terminating investment treaties and withdrawing from the ICSID Convention, with Ecuador proposing a mechanism within UNASUR to apply different legal standards (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014).

Oxfam can further champion the development of alternative dispute resolution mechanisms, which would alleviate the burden on national courts and provide more accessible avenues for resolving investment disputes (Taglioni and Winkler, 2014; Cattaneo et al., 2013). These mechanisms could involve adjustments to domestic laws concerning nationalization and expropriation, empowering host governments to regulate foreign investors in the public interest (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014). The vagueness of investment treaty standards and the authority of tribunals currently restrict the policy space of less-industrialized countries, underscoring the need for reform to expand their policy space (UNCTAD, 2011).

Moreover, Oxfam should advise rich countries against imposing investment agreements that constrain the policy space of developing countries, thereby enabling them to pursue industrialization strategies aligned with their development goals (Stiglitz 2018; United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). Supporting the WTO dispute settlement mechanism is also vital, as it provides a platform for less-industrialized countries to assert their rights under international law (World Bank Group 2006). Oxfam can suggest that rich countries enhance the credibility of international commitments by improving transparency in arbitration processes and ensuring high levels of mutual compliance (The International Bank for Reconstruction and Development / The World Bank 2005).

The proliferation of international rules and standards concerning investment climate matters necessitates that Oxfam recommend the removal of distortions by rich countries that harm the investment climates of developing countries, thereby increasing their policy space (The International Bank for Reconstruction and Development / The World Bank 2005). Additionally, Oxfam can propose that rich countries support the development of mechanisms to commit governments to sound long-term policies, such as independent regulatory agencies and international arbitration, to bolster policy credibility in less-industrialized countries (The International Bank for Reconstruction and Development / The World Bank 2005). Collectively, these reforms aim to rectify the current imbalance in international investment agreements, which often prioritize private over public interests (Akyüz Y 2007).

### Flexibility in International Agreements
A recurring point of emphasis across the discourse on international agreements is the necessity for flexibility mechanisms that empower less-industrialized countries to adapt or withdraw from commitments, aligning them more closely with their developmental needs. This flexibility is particularly crucial in trade and investment agreements, where rigid commitments often restrict policy space. Existing international investment agreements (IIAs) already incorporate some flexibility mechanisms to mitigate these constraints (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). As previously noted, Oxfam can advocate for the expansion of these mechanisms, enabling countries to modify or withdraw commitments, thereby enhancing their policy space (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018).

In the context of the World Trade Organization (WTO), there is a pressing call for a more flexible application of agreements, especially in allowing countries to temporarily increase tariffs under specific conditions (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). This flexibility should extend to the renegotiation of industrial tariffs to support sector-specific public policies, as evidenced by successful structural transformations in countries like the Republic of Korea (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014). Furthermore, Oxfam can recommend that affluent nations support reforms that permit less-industrialized countries to unbind and raise tariffs when justified, thereby fostering genuine flexibility in trade agreements (Ha-Joon Chang 2005; Akyuz, 2005).

Moreover, Oxfam can propose reforms to the General Agreement on Trade in Services (GATS) to prioritize development objectives, exclude essential public services from liberalization, and strengthen national sovereignty provisions (Oxfam 2002). This would ensure that governments retain the right to limit liberalization in areas critical to national development and poverty reduction. The timeframe for liberalization commitments should be extended, and governments should not be compelled to make commitments that conflict with national poverty-reduction strategies (Oxfam 2002).

The introduction of opt-out and exit clauses in international economic rules is another proposed reform. These clauses would allow countries to prioritize domestic developmental objectives over international obligations when necessary, thus expanding their policy space (Dani Rodrik 1997). Such reforms would also necessitate procedural safeguards to prevent abuse, ensuring transparency and accountability in decision-making processes (Dani Rodrik 1997). Escape clauses should be viewed as an inherent component of sustainable international economic arrangements, not as derogations or violations of the rules (Dani Rodrik 1997).

Finally, Oxfam can advocate for a broader interpretation of WTO safeguards, allowing countries to restrict trade for reasons beyond competitive threats, such as distributional concerns or conflicts with domestic norms (Dani Rodrik 1997). This approach would enable less-industrialized countries to pursue industrial policies tailored to their unique developmental contexts, thereby enhancing their policy space. Additionally, Oxfam can recommend that rich countries engage in multilateral reform discussions, including with regard to ISDS, to modernize existing treaties and ensure coherence with national investment policies and other bodies of international law (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018).

Oxfam can also suggest that affluent nations support the establishment of multilateral agreements through organizations like the GATT and the World Intellectual Property Organization, as these provide a comprehensive global approach and minimize trade retaliation risks, thereby increasing policy space for less-industrialized countries (World Bank Group 1991). Furthermore, Oxfam can recommend that rich countries focus on reducing agricultural protection and trade barriers, which would significantly enhance the export revenues and economic growth of less-industrialized countries (World Bank Group 1991).

### Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) Framework
Fundamental to this body of work is the establishment of a transparent, coherent, and balanced Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) framework, which is pivotal for enhancing the policy space of less-industrialized countries. This framework is essential for enabling these nations to participate equitably in global production systems. Oxfam can play a crucial role by recommending that affluent countries support reforms in transnational institutions to facilitate this process. Such reforms should aim to create a development-friendly multilateral framework for FDI that reflects the interests of all countries and strengthens the contribution of FDI to equitable development (World Commission on the Social Dimension of Globalization 2004).

A significant component of this framework involves the promotion of regional trade agreements (RTAs) such as MERCOSUR and the African Continental Free Trade Agreement. These agreements have the potential to create larger duty-free markets and facilitate technology transfer, thereby expanding the policy space for less-industrialized countries (Fernandes & Reed 2026). Leveraging these agreements to support South-South cooperation and 'deep agreements' that encompass a wide range of policy areas beyond tariff liberalization is crucial (Fernandes & Reed 2026). However, challenges persist in incorporating Special Economic Zones (SEZs) into their regulatory frameworks, highlighting the need for reform to increase policy space for less-industrialized countries (World Bank 2017).

Investment policy coherence is vital for productive capacity building, and reforms should ensure that international investment agreements (IIAs) align with national policies to support sustainable industrial development (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). This includes modernizing treaties to ensure coherence with national investment policies and other bodies of international law (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). Notably, IIA reform is progressing across all regions, with over 150 countries taking steps to formulate a new generation of sustainable development-oriented IIAs since 2012 (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018).

Oxfam can advocate for reforms that empower less-industrialized countries to impose sector-specific entry conditions on foreign investors and apply local-content requirements, unless prohibited by GATS commitments (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2014). Additionally, reforms should address the control over FDI, which is heavily concentrated among the largest transnational corporations, to increase the policy space for less-industrialized countries (United Nations 1999). The trend of suppliers, rather than lead firms, making many of the new investments that developing countries are seeking to capture suggests a need for reforms that expand the policy space for less-industrialized countries to attract these investments (Gereffi & Sturgeon, supra note 4, at 353).

Finally, Oxfam can propose changes to the TRIMS agreement to grant poorer countries more regulatory power over foreign investors, thereby expanding their policy space (Ha-Joon Chang 2007). By supporting these reforms, rich countries can assist less-industrialized nations in pursuing their own industrialization strategies and achieving sustainable economic growth. The importance of a conducive global investment environment, characterized by open, transparent, and non-discriminatory investment policies, is underscored as essential for sustainable industrial development in developing countries (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018). Moreover, the new industrial revolution necessitates a strategic review of investment policies for industrial development (United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2018).

Governments can still pursue a trade-led growth model by utilizing public inputs, such as industrial parks, skills development, market access assistance, and quality infrastructure, to help local exporters succeed. Expanding preferential trade agreements with a diverse set of partners—particularly South-South agreements and deeper trade agreements allowing for investment and movement of workers—is also recommended (Fernandes, Ana Margarida; Reed, Tristan 2026). Multilateral development banks can support regional cooperation in industrial policy by financing development projects, sharing best practices, conducting feasibility studies, and providing expertise in renewable energy, digitalization, and sustainable manufacturing (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2024).

### Institutional Governance and Representation
A highly consistent pattern emerges regarding the imperative for reforming transnational institutions to ensure equitable governance and representation for less-industrialized countries. This theme underscores the necessity of expanding policy space, enabling these nations to pursue industrialization strategies comparable to those of their industrialized counterparts. Historical power imbalances have allowed affluent countries to impose restrictive economic policies on developing nations, highlighting the urgent need for reforms that enhance policy space (Chang & Andreoni, 2020). This includes revising WTO agreements, such as the Agreement on Government Procurement, to permit less-industrialized countries to leverage procurement for promoting domestic enterprises without breaching international trade rules (WTO, 2017). Furthermore, emerging economies within the G-20 are increasingly utilizing their influence in global governance to carve out policy space for proactive industrial policies (Gereffi, 2014).

Reforms must also address the financialization of the global economy and new forms of imperialism that limit policy space (Chang & Andreoni, 2020). Integrating small-scale enterprises into value chains and markets is crucial for expanding policy space (Ferrannini et al., 2021). Moreover, aligning industrial policy across various levels is essential for sustainable development (Ferrannini et al., 2021). A stable foundation for sustainable development requires both visionary and pragmatic approaches to guide a new generation of inclusive growth policies and institutional arrangements within the global economy (Gereffi & Sturgeon, 2014).

The international community can play a pivotal role by facilitating exchanges of ideas and governance structures, enabling multi-directional learning in the design of industrial policy (Ferrannini et al., 2021). Oxfam can advocate for reforms that include clear rules and sunset clauses to curb rent-seeking behavior, thereby increasing policy space (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). Accelerating structural change necessitates a proactive public sector, with public policies supporting research and development, subsidizing entrepreneurial cost discovery, coordinating complementary investments, and facilitating information sharing and technological learning (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017).

Moreover, the WTO's constraints on government procurement, encapsulated in a plurilateral deal, underscore the need for reforms to expand policy space for less-industrialized countries (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). The UN Secretary General’s High-Level Panel emphasizes transforming economies towards inclusive growth, with industrialization as a central strategy (UNHLP, 2013). Reforms should also focus on expanding policy space under GATT Article XVIII, allowing less-industrialized countries to pursue industrial policies even when conflicting with GATT obligations (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). The WTO's rules on subsidies, which are technically permitted but 'actionable,' suggest a need for reform to expand policy space (Fernandes & Reed, 2026).

Finally, the Stockholm Statement calls for advanced countries to acknowledge the interdependence of nations and the significant externalities their policies impose on the global community (Alkire et al., 2016; Stiglitz, 2016a). A fairer global trade regime would help bridge the resource and knowledge gaps between developed and developing countries (Stiglitz, 2018). Reforms should ensure that less-industrialized countries have comparable policy space to pursue industrialization strategies (Aiginger & Rodrik, 2020). Oxfam can recommend that affluent nations reform transnational institutions to expand the policy space for less-industrialized countries, enabling them to pursue their own industrialization strategies (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). Additionally, the absence of an international standard for accreditation under the Kyoto Protocol has led countries to establish distinct procedures and criteria, complicating processes for less-industrialized countries (Olsen and Fenhann, 2008).

### Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) Reform
The analysis suggests that a central driver of the theme of Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) reform is the necessity to expand the policy space available to less-industrialized countries, thereby enabling them to pursue economic development strategies tailored to their unique needs and capacities. At the heart of this reform is the call for extending transition periods and allowing greater flexibility in patent protection, as underscored by the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (2014) and Oxfam (2002). Such measures would empower developing countries to more effectively adopt and adapt technologies, fostering innovation and industrial growth.

As previously discussed, the TRIPS Agreement imposes stringent IPR standards that disproportionately favor industrialized nations, thereby constraining less-industrialized countries' access to and utilization of new technologies (Ha-Joon Chang 2007; Oxfam 2002). Reforms should aim to alleviate the financial burdens associated with licensing fees and the costs of implementing IPR systems, which are significant barriers to technology adoption (Ha-Joon Chang 2007). Furthermore, revisiting the TRIPS Agreement to incorporate frameworks that reward local communities and protect traditional knowledge is crucial for increasing the policy space of less-industrialized countries (Oxfam 2002).

Facilitating technology transfer emerges as another pivotal aspect of IPR reform. Oxfam advocates for rich countries to support international technology cooperation mechanisms, such as the Global Environmental Facility, to fund research and development, knowledge sharing, and technical assistance to developing countries (Altenburg & Assmann 2017). This strategy would enhance the technological absorptive capacity of less-industrialized countries, enabling them to innovate and develop industries aligned with their comparative advantages (Lin 2012). Additionally, investments in education, technological training, and knowledge dissemination infrastructure are essential to bolster a country's ability to innovate (Altenburg, T.; Assmann, C. 2017).

Moreover, the reform should address the imbalance in the global IPR regime by permitting less-industrialized countries to implement more flexible IPR systems. This includes the right to issue compulsory licenses and to discriminate against patent rights when products are not locally produced (Wade 2003; Shadlen 2005). Such measures would prevent the patent system from obstructing knowledge flows and support the structural transformation of developing economies (Ha-Joon Chang 2007). The Doha Declaration on the TRIPS Agreement and Public Health has clarified some flexibilities within the TRIPS Agreement, which can be leveraged to promote domestic production (Correa, 2014).

Developing countries should resist pressures from developed nations to join international agreements that primarily serve the economic interests of tech giants (Stiglitz, Joseph E. 2018). Instead, developed countries must acknowledge that the intellectual property rights regime suitable for developing countries differs from that for advanced countries (Stiglitz 2018). Oxfam can recommend reforms to ensure that technology transfer agreements or investments in poorer nations facilitate their benefit from substantial investments in new technology (Inu Manak 2024).

The TRIPs agreement presents a high barrier to public debate due to the complexity of intellectual property law and the opaque nature of WTO texts (Oxfam 2002). Therefore, reforms should aim to simplify these frameworks and prioritize public-health interests, ensuring that people's right to healthcare takes precedence over companies' patent rights (Oxfam 2002). Additionally, the TRIPs agreement should align with the UN Convention on Biological Diversity and the International Treaty on Plant Genetic Resources for Food and Agriculture, ensuring no patents on genetic resources without prior informed consent and benefit-sharing (Oxfam 2002).

In conclusion, Oxfam's recommendations for IPR reform emphasize the need for a development-oriented regime that facilitates technology transfer, reduces the financial burden of IPR compliance, and allows for greater flexibility in patent protection. These reforms are essential for expanding the policy space of less-industrialized countries, enabling them to pursue sustainable economic development and technological innovation. Rich countries should support these reforms by advocating for changes in transnational institutions that facilitate technology transfer and capacity building, thereby expanding the policy space for less-industrialized countries.

### Green Industrial Policy and Environmental Considerations
The most prominent narrative identified across the corpus emphasizes the critical integration of environmental and social considerations into trade and industrial policies as a means to expand the policy space for less-industrialized countries. Oxfam can play a pivotal role by recommending that affluent nations reform transnational institutions to incorporate environmental issues within trade and investment treaties, thereby facilitating sustainable industrialization (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). This reform agenda includes addressing the constraints imposed by existing trade and investment laws on green industrial policies, particularly those related to tariff policy and subsidies (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). Notably, many green industrial policy options, such as feed-in tariffs and performance requirements, remain unaffected by these laws, offering viable pathways for policy development (Cosbey in Altenburg & Assmann, 2017).

Reforming transnational institutions to eliminate fossil fuel subsidies could significantly reduce pollution, raise revenue, and cut global carbon emissions, thereby increasing the policy space for less-industrialized countries (Coady et al., 2017). Furthermore, reforms should address the mispricing of carbon and the persistence of fossil fuel subsidies to further expand policy space (Rodrik, 2014). Oxfam can also advocate for rich countries to maintain low tariffs on imports of solar panels and electric vehicles, which are generally lower than those on imports of natural gas and combustion-engine vehicles, to assist less-industrialized countries in adopting low-pollution technologies (Fernandes & Reed, 2026).

Promoting the adoption of green technologies through international openness and trade policies that encourage the import and deployment of foreign green technologies is essential (World Bank, 2012). Oxfam can champion reforms that enable developing countries to leverage their natural endowments for green industries, such as solar energy in North Africa, thereby enhancing their policy space (Vidican Auktor, 2017). Additionally, linking green subsidies in high-income countries to preferential licensing and technology sharing with emerging markets and developing economies (EMDEs) can foster joint ventures and innovation hubs (Maltais & Suljada, 2025).

The establishment of international agreements that include climate finance for developing countries is crucial to support their mitigation and adaptation efforts (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). This includes ensuring that trade agreements incorporate sustainability criteria, which would help less-industrialized countries compete on a more equal footing (OECD, 2021). The Paris Agreement's non-binding plan to provide US$ 100 billion per year for decarbonization and adaptation measures underscores the importance of financial support for technology transfers (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017).

Finally, Oxfam can recommend that affluent nations support the creation of global funding mechanisms to disseminate climate-smart technology to countries with the greatest need but the least capacity to adopt them (World Bank, 2010). Such initiatives would expand the policy space for less-industrialized countries, enabling them to pursue sustainable industrialization without replicating the environmental degradation experienced by industrialized nations (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017). Additionally, reforms should focus on expanding policy space by addressing issues like trade disputes and over-capacity in sectors such as solar energy (Rodrik, 2014). Oxfam can also suggest that rich countries advocate for clearer guidelines and reduced uncertainty in trade and investment law to allow less-industrialized countries to pursue green industrial policies without fear of breaching obligations (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017).

### Financial and Macroeconomic Policy Reform
A primary pillar of the analysis concerns the imperative for Financial and Macroeconomic Policy Reform, underscoring the necessity for affluent nations to champion comprehensive reforms within transnational institutions. These reforms are pivotal in expanding the policy space available to less-industrialized countries, thereby enabling them to navigate economic cycles and implement long-term development strategies effectively. Central to this discourse is the advocacy for increased flexibility in macroeconomic policies, which includes endorsing competitive exchange rate devaluation—a strategy that has proven successful in regions such as East Asia and Viet Nam (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). Furthermore, the recognition of capital controls as legitimate instruments for maintaining economic stability is emphasized (Wade, 2003; Rodrik, 1997). Addressing capital market imperfections, externalities, and coordination failures is also crucial for enhancing policy space (Stiglitz, 2018).

In alignment with earlier discussions on investment and trade reforms, rich countries are urged to confront the constraints imposed by international tax agreements that hinder less-industrialized countries from attracting foreign direct investment through tax incentives (Fernandes & Reed, 2026). The Independent Commission on Reform of International Taxation (ICRIT) highlights the detrimental effects of tax competition and advocates for an international accord to mitigate this 'race-to-the-bottom' phenomenon (ICRIT 2018 in Stiglitz, 2018). Innovation within the tax system is essential to sustain high-risk public spending, which is vital for future private sector innovation (Mazzucato).

Moreover, enhancing fiscal space through domestic revenue mobilization and reducing dependency on foreign sources is critical, as reliance on external financing often leads to overindebtedness (UNCTAD, 2009). The development of robust domestic financial markets is essential for mitigating reliance on external financing and bolstering resilience against economic shocks (International Monetary Fund, 2010). Consistent with previous themes, Oxfam can advocate for rich countries to ensure the provision of a healthy and educated workforce, robust infrastructure, and a sound macroeconomic framework to support less-industrialized countries (Fernandes & Reed, 2026).

Oxfam can further propose the establishment of a new global financial order characterized by minimal international guidelines, granting less-industrialized countries greater autonomy in managing international capital flows and preventing financial instability (Rodrik, 1997). Additionally, reforms should facilitate access to long-term, low-interest foreign capital for infrastructure development, akin to the support provided by state agencies in industrialized nations (Mazzucato). The role of State investment banks is highlighted as they can offer countercyclical lending and generate returns to fund future investments (Mazzucato).

Rich countries should also support the creation of international financial charters that emphasize financial transparency and foster consultation among national regulators, thereby expanding policy space for less-industrialized countries (Rodrik, 1997). Integrating climate risks into macroeconomic models and forecasts is another critical reform to increase policy space (NGFS 2020). Given the significant debt burdens faced by many emerging markets and developing economies (EMDEs), some analysts advocate for linking debt relief to the creation of fiscal space necessary for investments in energy transition and sustainability projects (Achampong & Fresnillo, 2024; Future Matters, 2024).

Oxfam can recommend that affluent nations support systemic reform of the international financial architecture to address the high cost of debt and enhance access to affordable long-term financing for development, thereby expanding the policy space for less-industrialized countries (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2024). A new global deal, grounded in stronger solidarity, is essential, wherein today’s wealthy industrialized nations support the most vulnerable countries in finance, capabilities, technologies, skills, and policy space (United Nations Industrial Development Organization 2024).

Finally, Oxfam can advocate for reforms that recognize the State's role in risk absorption and management, as evidenced by the industrial policy history of the United States, to expand policy space for less-industrialized countries (Mazzucato). These reforms should challenge conventional economic development myths and expand the State's role in innovation, thereby increasing the policy space for less-industrialized countries to pursue their own industrialization strategies (Mariana Mazzucato). Collectively, these comprehensive reforms are crucial for enabling less-industrialized countries to pursue independent growth paths and achieve sustainable development.

### Other
Beyond the primary thematic clusters, the material also reflects a diverse array of strategies aimed at expanding the policy space for less-industrialized countries. As previously discussed, leveraging ambiguities in international rules provides developing nations with the opportunity to implement certain policies until they encounter challenges (Chang & Andreoni, 2020). This approach is complemented by the emphasis on fostering a 'learning economy,' a central development policy that seeks to bridge the gap between developing and developed nations (Stiglitz et al., 2013). Bilateral trade agreements incorporating labor standards are also suggested as a means to preserve policy space (Chang & Andreoni, 2020). Notably, unless developing countries have entered into bilateral agreements with affluent nations, they retain a significant degree of 'policy space' (Chang & Andreoni, 2020).

The COVID-19 pandemic has underscored the importance of industrial sovereignty, potentially leading to increased protectionism (OECD, 2020c; WEF, 2020b). This context highlights the necessity for developing countries to experiment with economic policies, as uniform conditions imposed by international organizations may not be universally applicable (Wade, 2010). There exist domestic industrial policy measures that are not subject to international agreements, such as targeted infrastructural investments and subsidies for worker training or research and development (Chang & Andreoni, 2020). Furthermore, the role of natural science research in understanding economic risks within ecosystems is crucial for informing policy decisions (Altenburg & Assmann, 2017).

Social media, NGOs, and citizens are encouraged to engage in shaping policy agendas to prevent polarization (Aiginger & Sieber, 2006). Redirecting technical progress to improve welfare and reduce growth dependency is also recommended (Aiginger & Rodrik, 2020). Moreover, establishing platforms for information exchange and best practice sharing is vital for capacity development in industrial policy (UNIDO, 2024). The international community can support developing countries in building the necessary conditions for implementing new industrial policies that can help accelerate progress towards the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) (UNIDO, 2024).

The international community is urged to support developing countries by mobilizing creative forces and latent potentials rather than merely providing supply-side fixes (2006). Collaboration among stakeholders is essential, as governments alone cannot address today's challenges (UNIDO, 2024). Governments can actively court nationals abroad, encourage their return, and use them to spawn new domestic economic activities (Rodrik, 2004). Furthermore, it is important to have a process in place that helps reveal areas of desirable interventions, rather than obsessing over policy instruments and modalities of interventions (Rodrik, 2004).

Finally, the creation of a WTO working group on trade and commodities is proposed to enhance the wealth-generating potential of commodities for developing countries (Oxfam, 2002). Oxfam can recommend reforms to transnational institutions to ensure that developing countries can capture more value from their commodities by processing them domestically, which would expand their policy space (Oxfam, 2002). These diverse recommendations collectively aim to expand the policy space for less-industrialized countries, enabling them to pursue tailored and effective development strategies. Additionally, Oxfam can recommend that rich countries support capacity-building efforts for developing countries, ensuring that agencies responsible are unbiased, to enhance the policy space available to less-industrialized countries (World Bank Group, 2006).
